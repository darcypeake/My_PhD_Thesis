\section{Introduction}
In this chapter, we present original research on the \textsc{ARES} method for $hh \to Z+\mathrm{jet}$ production. 
This is the first time the \textsc{ARES} framework has been performed on hadron--hadron collisions at NNLL accuracy. 

Here we present a general NNLL master formula, which includes both initial state and final state radiation.
One of the most notable discoveries during this research was the fact that, the resummed formula is
the same as that found in the previous literature for three-jet event shapes in $e^+e^-$ annihilation.
A key novel ingredient of this resummation formula, and of this work, is the extension of the \textsc{ARES} framework to include initial-state radiation effects at NNLL accuracy. 
Unlike in $e^+e^-$ collisions, where only final-state radiation is present, hadron collisions involve additional radiation from the incoming partons. 
Accounting for these effects at NNLL requires a non-trivial generalisation of the formalism and constitutes one of the main advances of this chapter.

To illustrate the method and highlight its features, we concentrate on an observable which measures how ``one-jet like'' an event is, namely the one-jettiness. \add{Relate to thrust.} \ndp{Sensitive to out of plane radiation?}. This examples make explicit how the different components of the calculation enter in practice, and help expose subtleties of the formalism that are not immediately apparent at a more abstract level.


The chapter proceeds as follows. We first define the observable and  present the master NNLL cumulant formula. Next, we
derive the initial-state radiation for the first time. Finishing with the explicit results for the one-jettiness.

partons of momenta $p_1=x_1 P_1, p_2=x_2 P_2$ producing a $Z$ boson

against additional partons. Its momentum will change to
$\tilde p_3$, and it will be clustered with other partons to form a hard jet $p_J$. At Born level, $p_J=p_3$.

\end{equation}

outgoing jet $p_J$ recoils against the $Z$ boson. We can also consider a normalised version of 1-jettiness,

A particular choice of $Q_1, Q_2, Q_3$
\begin{equation}

  \quad Q_3 = 2 p_{T,J}\,,
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

\end{equation}

One-jettiness is an example of a recursively infrared and collinear (rIRC) safe observables. In
general, we want to study the differential cross-section in the $q_T$ and
the rapidity $y_Z$, with the additional constraint that a given
observable $V(q_1,q_2,\dots,q_n)$ to be less than a given value
$v$. The observable is such that the near-to-planar region corresponds
to $v\to 1$. In the near-to-planar limit, the aforementioned
cross-section takes the form
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dsigma-def}
  \frac{d\sigma(v)}{dq_T dy_Z} \simeq \sum_{i,j} \int_0^1 \!\! dx_1\, f_i(x_1,\mu_F)\int_0^1 \!\! dx_2\, f_j(x_2,\mu_F)\frac{d\hat \sigma_{ij}}{dq_Tdy_Z} \Sigma_{ij}(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v)\,,
\end{equation}
In the above equation, we have introduced the partonic cross section
for the production of a $Z$ boson and an extra jet via the partonic
subprocess $ij$, where $i$ represents a quark, an antiquark of any
light flavour ($u,d,c,s.b$, considered massless), or a gluon.
The expression in eq.~\eqref{eq:dsigma-def}
requires the construction of a mapping between the full event, made up
of the three hard partons $\{\tilde p\}\equiv\{\tilde p_1,\tilde p_2,\tilde p_3\}$ and
an arbitrary number of soft and/or collinear emissions $\{k_i\}$, and
a Born event with only three hard partons $\{p_1,p_2,p_3\}$. The
effect of multiple soft and/or collinear emissions, and the
corresponding virtual corrections, for fixed Born kinematics \ABquestion{Fix?}. The
difference between the approximation on the right-hand side of
eq.~\eqref{eq:dsigma-def} and the full expression for
$ d\sigma(v)/(dq_T dy_Z)$ is a well-defined expansion in powers of the
strong coupling $\alpha_s$, with coefficients vanishins as a power of
$v$, potentially enhanced by logarithms of $v$.

The cumulative distribution $\Sigma_{ij}(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v)$ contains
logarithms of $v$ that become large in the near-to-planar region. The
largest of these contributions are double logarithms of the form
$(\alpha_s L^2)^n$, with $L\equiv \ln(1/v)$. Neglecting for the moment
coherence-violating effects, if $V(\{\tilde p\},\{k_i\})$ is rIRC
safe, for each Born phase space point, in the region
$\alpha_s L\sim 1$, $\Sigma_{ij}(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v)$ can be
reorganised as follows
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:log-counting}
  \Sigma_{ij}(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v) = e^{L g_1(\alpha_s L)}\left[G_2(\alpha_s L)+ \alpha_s G_3(\alpha_s L)+\dots\right]\,.
\end{equation}
The function $g_1(\alpha_s L)$ resums all leading logarithmic (LL)
terms, $\alpha_s^n L^{n+1}$, in $\ln\Sigma_{ij}$. For rIRC safe
observables, $g_1$ exponentiates. The function $G_2(\alpha_s L)$
resums next-to-leading logarithmic (NLL) contributions
($\alpha_s^n L^n$) to $\ln\Sigma_{ij}$, and so on. In this work, we
aim at NNLL accuracy, i.e.\ calculating $g_1$, $G_2$ and $G_3$.

\section{General form of NNLL resummation}
\label{sec:NNLL-general}

The main differences between event shapes in hadron collisions and $e^+e^-$ annihilation are
\begin{itemize}
\item the presence of initial-state radiation;
\item the fact that there is no single natural hard scale $Q$;
\end{itemize}
Nevertheless, in the next section we will be able to show that the structure of the NNLL resummation for $\Sigma_{ij}(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v)$ is the same as for $e^+e^-$ annihilation~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}, but some of the ingredients appearing in the resummation formula have to be slightly modified. In this section, we present the general structure of the NNLL resummation, and discuss the physical meaning of its ingredients. For each partonic channel $ij$, we have
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:Sigma-NNLL}
  \Sigma_{ij}(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v)= e^{-R_{\rm NNLL}(v)} \frac{f_i(x_1,v^{\frac{1}{a+b_1}}Q)}{f_i(x_1,\mu_F)} \frac{f_j(x_2,v^{\frac{1}{a+b_2}}Q)}{f_j(x_2,\mu_F)}
  \times \\ \times
  \left(\mathcal{F}^{(ij)}_{\rm NLL}(\lambda)\left(1+\frac{\alpha_s(Q)}{2\pi}H_{ij}^{(1)}(Q,\mu_R)
      +\sum_{\ell=1}^3\frac{\alpha_s(Q v^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{2\pi}\sum_{\ell}C_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}^{(1)}\right) +\frac{\alpha_s(Q)}{\pi} \delta\mathcal{F}^{(ij)}_{\rm NNLL}(\lambda)\right)\,,
\end{multline}
with $\lambda\equiv \alpha_s(Q)\beta_0\ln(1/v)$,
$\beta_0=(11 C_A\!-2 n_f)/(12\pi)$, and $\mu_R,\mu_F$ are the renormalisation and factorisation
scales in the $\overline{\rm MS}$ scheme. The parameters $a_\ell, b_\ell$ are determined by the general behaviour of an IRC safe observable $V$ in the presence of a single soft emission collinear to leg $\ell$
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:V-sc}
  V(\{p\},k)\simeq V_{\rm sc}(\{\tilde p\},k)=d_\ell\left(\frac{k^{(\ell)}_t}{Q}\right)^a e^{-b_\ell \eta^{(\ell)}} g_\ell(\phi^{(\ell)})\,,
\end{equation}
where $d_\ell$ is a coefficient that depends on the underlying Born
kinematics, and we have introduced an arbitrary ``resummation'' scale $Q$, which
we use as a reference normalisation scale for transverse momenta. 

It is possible to recover the physical meaning of all ingredients in
equation~(\ref{eq:Sigma-NNLL})
from~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn,Banfi:2018mcq,Banfi:2004yd}. We give below a
short summary and description of all of them, highlighting the
differences with respect to the $e^+e^-$ case:
\begin{itemize}
\item the ``radiator'' $R_{\rm NNLL}(v)$ includes all virtual
  corrections of soft and/or collinear origin. These are divergent,
  but their divergences cancel with those of real radiation. In order
  to cancel the singularities of virtual corrections analytically, the
  radiator includes also a subset of real corrections for suitably
  defined ``unresolved'' emissions. The procedure to define those
  emissions is not unique. A proposal that is adequate for three
  emitting legs can be found in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. This gives rise
  to a master formula for $R_{\rm NNLL}(v)$, whose explicit expression
  depends only on the observable properties with a single soft and
  collinear emission, more precisely
  $a,b_\ell,d_\ell,g_\ell(\phi)$~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. In hadron
  collisions, we can obtain the explicit expression for
  $R_{\rm NNLL}(v)$ from~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. In that reference, $Q$
  denoted the centre-of-mass energy of the $e^+e^-$ collision,
  whereas, in the present context, it is an arbitrarily chosen
  resummation scale.  Notice that, with the knowledge we have acquired
  so far, the radiator could be computed for a generic observable at
  N$^3$LL accuracy.

\item Parton distribution functions enter through the ratios
  $f_i(x_\ell,v^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}}Q)/f_i(x_\ell,\mu_F)$,
  where $f_i(x_\ell,\mu_F)$ is the parton distribution function
corresponding to the flavour of the parton $p_\ell$ entering the hard scattering, with
momentum fraction $x_\ell$. The physical meaning of this ratio is the contribution of
all unresolved hard collinear emissions below the veto scale
$v^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}}Q$ gives the DGLAP evolution of a
parton distribution function.

\item Resolved soft and collinear emissions widely separated in
  angle, and the appropriate virtual corrections, build up to the
  NLL function $\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}(\lambda)$. Its calculation requires the
following limit:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vsc}
  \frac{\Vsc{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{\Vfull{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
where $k_1,\dots,k_n$ are soft {\em and} collinear emissions. This
limit is guaranteed to exist for all rIRC safe observables. For event
shapes, we can integrate freely over rapidity fractions, so that the
limit in eq.~\eqref{eq:Vsc} is a function of the variables
$\{\zeta_i,\ell_i,\phi_i\}$, with $\zeta_i\equiv
\Vsc{k_i}/v$. Resolved emission are those which satisfy
$\zeta_i>\epsilon$, where $\epsilon$ is a cutoff. With this
parameterisation, $\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}(\lambda)$  can be
computed using the general formula
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:FNLL-formula}
    \mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}(\lambda) = \int\! \dZ \,\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\,,
  \end{equation}
where we have defined the soft-collinear measure $\dZ$ through the formula
  \begin{multline}
    \label{eq:dZ-def}
    \int\! \dZ \,G(\{k_i\}) \\ \equiv \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!} \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)G(k_1,\dots,k_n)\,.
  \end{multline}
  for any function $G$ of the emissions. The factors
  $R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}$ are NLL functions of $\lambda$, and arise
  as a jacobian factor for each emission and for each emitting leg.
  
\item Finite virtual corrections of relative order $\alpha_s$ are
  embedded in the coefficient $H^{(1)}$. This is a term of order
  $\alpha_s$ multiplying a NLL function, hence NNLL. For a generic
  process, $H^{(1)}$ will depend on $Q$, kinematic invariants and the
  renomalisation scale $\mu_R$.
\item A single unresolved hard collinear splitting of leg
  $\ell$ combines with the corresponding virtual corrections and, in
  the case of initial-state radiation, to the collinear counterterm
  needed to renormalise the parton distribution functions. This gives
  the coefficient $C_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}^{(1)}$, a NNLL
  contribution.

  For a final-state splitting, the explicit expression
  of $C_{\mathrm{hc},3}^{(1)}$ depends on the flavour of leg 3, and
  can be obtained from~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. For a quark or an
  antiquark
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:C1hc-q}
    C_{\mathrm{hc},3}^{(1)}=C_F\left(\frac{7}{2}\frac{b_3}{a+b_3}+\frac{3}{a+b_3}\left(\ln\langle d_3 g_3\rangle-b_3\ln\frac{2E_3}{Q}\right)+\frac 12\right)\,,
  \end{equation}
  while for a gluon
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:C1hc-q}
    \begin{split}
    C_{\mathrm{hc},3}^{(1)}& =\left(\frac{67}{18}C_A-\frac{13}{9}T_R n_f\right)\frac{b_3}{a+b_3}\\ & +\frac{1}{a+b_3}\left(\frac{11}{3}C_A-\frac{4}{3}T_R n_f\right)\left(\ln\langle d_3 g_3\rangle-b_3\ln\frac{2E_3}{Q}\right)+\frac{1}{3}T_R n_f\,.
    \end{split}
  \end{equation}

  For an initial-state splitting, the coefficient $C_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}^{(1)}$ is a new result of this work, and is given by
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:Cell-ISR}
    C_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}^{(1)} = \sum_j  \int_{x_\ell}^1\frac{dz}{z}\,C_{ij}^{(1)}(z) \frac{f_j(x_\ell/z,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{f_i(x_\ell,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}\,,
  \end{equation}
  where $i$ is the flavour of leg $\ell$. The coefficients $C_{ij}^{(1)}(z)$ are given in terms of the splitting functions in $4-2\varepsilon$ dimensions
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:Pij-general}
    \hat P_{ij}(z;\varepsilon)= \hat P_{ij}(z;0)+\varepsilon P_{ij}^{(\varepsilon)}(z)\,,
  \end{equation}
  and have the following expression
  \begin{multline}
    \label{eq:Cij-ISR}
    C_{ij}^{(1)}(z) = -\hat P_{ij}^{(\varepsilon)}(z) \\ +\frac{2 }{a+b_\ell}\left(b_\ell\left(\frac{\ln(1-z)}{1-z}\right)_+-\frac{1}{(1-z)_+}
      \left(\langle\ln d_\ell g_\ell\rangle-b_\ell \ln\frac{2E_\ell}{Q}\right)\right)(1-z)\hat P_{ij}(z;0)\,.
  \end{multline}
  We give a complete derivation of this result in
  sec.~\ref{sec:quark-in}. The explicit expressions of the splitting

	\end{align}
  \end{subequations}
  
  \item  
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:dFNNLL}
    \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm NNLL} = \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}+\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm wa}+\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm wa}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}\,.
  \end{equation}

\subsection{Soft and collinear contributions}
\label{sec:sc-contributions}
In this section we collect all contributions arising from soft and
collinear emissions, as stated in the title. These are
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}$ at NLL, and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$ and
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$, and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$ at NNLL. The NLL function
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}$ and the NNLL functions $\delta
\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$ and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$ involve
gluons widely separated in angle, while $\delta
\mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$ accounts for the splitting of one soft and collinear gluon into a $q\bar{q}$ or a gluon pair. 
\add{Mention Eq.~\eqref{eq:Vsc}.}

\paragraph{NLL resummation.} One-jettiness is additive, so
  $\FNLL(\lambda)$ takes the standard form for additive event
  shapes~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq: FNLL-tau_1}
  \FNLL(\lambda)=\frac{e^{-\gamma_E \RpNLL}}{\Gamma(1+\RpNLL)}\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{NNLL resummation.} The soft and collinear corrections at NNLL are, in order of complexity,
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$, and
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$. On top of the soft-collinear
emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ widely separated in angle/rapidity, 
that are needed for any resummation, the two
functions $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$
involve the presence of an additional soft and collinear emission $k$,
parametrised in terms of the usual variable $\zeta=\Vsc{k}/v$, as well
as its emitter's leg $\ell$, and azimuthal angle $\phi^{(\ell)}$. 
For $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$, this
additional emission is required to split into a pair $k_a$ and $k_b$,
with further dimensionless variables parametrising the splitting 
(cf.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFcorrel-general}). \add{Mention Eq.~\eqref{eq:Vsc}.}

The general expression for $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$ is given by 
\begin{multline}

These calculations involve dipoles and therefore require the use of Sudakov variables. This is the idea that a single emission $k$ can be decomposed along any pair of light-like momenta $(p_i,p_j)$, which constitute the $(ij)$ dipole. The decomposition is given by:
\begin{equation}
k^{(ij)}=z^{(i)}p_i+z^{(j)}p_j+\kappa^{(ij)}\cos\phi^{(ij)}n_{\rm{in}}^{(ij)}+\kappa^{(ij)}\sin\phi^{(ij)}n_{\rm{out}}^{(ij)}\,,
\label{eq:Sudakov-decomp}
\end{equation}
where all components of this Sudakov decomposition can be found in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.

These contributions use the constraint on the observable, $V_{\mathrm{wa}}$
