\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

So far, a general method for resummation for hadronic 
collisions at NNLL accuracy has not yet be found for jet observables.
There are, of course, parton showers which are currently
being worked on to find NNLL accurate showers at hadronic collisions,
but so far there are only results for this order for $e^+e^-$ annihilation
and DIS. \todo{Check this is true.} \add{Cite these.}
Moreover, SCET has managed to find observables in hadronic collisions
even up to N$^3$LL accuracy, however these methods are observable dependent
and rely on finding the individual factorisation formula for each.
While there have been attempts to find a general formalism \add{cite the lund 
shape observable paper}, there has not been yet one proposed to cover a 
range of event shapes and the methods which exist account for a specific subset
of event shapes.

For the very first time, we present a general method i.e.\ the ARES method
for hadronic collisions to NNLL accuracy. We look at the process of:  $hh\to Z+$ jet.
This process provides a remarkably clean signature in the detector and additionally
the colour algebra is significantly more simple due to only three coloured legs present here
with one colour singlet i.e.\ the $Z$-boson.

To illustrate this work we look at the commonly used event shapes,
the one-jettiness. This observable is particularly clean and has been 
resummed many times before so offers us a nice comparison to previous
results found in the literature. Moreover, it is an additive result and since
these sort of observables are very easy to calculate within the \textsc{ARES}
formalism, we are able to present the results for the observable easily while focussing 
on the new pieces of research. As  such, we are using this observable merely as an example to show how the formalism works. The results are not new for this observable as it has been resummed up to N$^3$LL accuracy \add{add citations.}

It is important to note that we will not derive the master NNLL
formula as we did for NLL, as this is not the main result of this chapter.
Instead one can find how it came about in Refs. \add{add references}.
We refer to reader to these references if they want a greater insight into
the details of this formalism.

The structure of this chapter is as follows. We being by presenting
the master formula for hadronic collisions for the \textsc{ARES}
method at NNLL. We then present how the initial state radiation was
derived for this formula. We then show how this formalism works
by calculating the one-jettiness within this framework,
finishing with a validation of this formalism against 
existing results.

We first discuss the splitting $q\to qg$ in detail, and generalise the result to the splitting $g\to q\bar q$.
We then consider an
incoming quark carrying a fraction $x/z$ of the proton momentum, which
emits a collinear gluon carrying a fraction $1-z$ of its emitter's
momentum. After the emission, the quark enters the hard scattering
with a fraction $x$ of the proton momentum. \question{again unsure
if this is consistent with before?} The contribution of this emission to $\Sigma_{ij}(\{\tilde p\},v)$ gives
a function that we call $\mathcal{F}_{qq}$. This function will
be reorganised to give various NNLL functions. Our starting point is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FISR-start}
  \begin{split}
    \mathcal{F}_{qq} & = \int \dZ \left\{\int_0^{Q^2}\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\times\right. \\& \times \left[\int_{x_\ell}^1 \frac{dz}{z}\hat P_{qq}(z;\varepsilon)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\}}{v}\right) \frac{f_q(x_\ell/z,\mu_F)}{f_q(x_\ell,\mu_F)}- \int_0^1 dz \frac{2C_\ell}{1-z}\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\right] \\
   & \left[-\gamma_\ell^{(0)}\int_0^{Q^2v^{2/(a+b_\ell)}}\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}\frac{\alpha_s(k)}{2\pi}
     +\frac{1}{\varepsilon} \left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{\mu_F^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi} \int_{x_\ell}^1 \frac{dz}{z} P_{qq}(z) \frac{f_q(x_\ell/z,\mu_F)}{f_q(x_\ell,\mu_F)}\right] \times \\ & \times \left. \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\right\} \,.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where we have introduced the regularised Altarelli-Parisi (AP) splitting function
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:regularised-Pij}
  \hat P_{ij}(z;\varepsilon) = \hat P_{ij}(z;0)+\varepsilon P^{(\varepsilon)}_{ij}(z)\,, \qquad i=q,g\,. 
\end{equation}
In the present case
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Pqq}
  \hat P_{qq}(z;0)  = C_F \frac{1+z^2}{1-z}\,, \qquad \hat P_{qq}^{(\varepsilon)}(z)=-C_F (1-z)\,.
\end{equation}
The function $P_{qq}(z)$ is the full AP splitting function, which in this case is given by $\left(\hat P_{qq}(z;0)\right)_+$. 

We now comment on the physical meaning of the ingredients of eq.~(\ref{eq:FISR-start}).
Its second line represents the splitting of a
quark emitting a collinear gluon $k$ in $4-2\varepsilon$ dimensions,
minus the contribution when the gluon is soft, already accounted for
in $\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}$. The third line contains virtual
corrections, as well as the collinear counterterm needed to renormalise
the parton distribution function. The parameter $b_\ell$ corresponds
to the leg identified by the quark entering the hard process.  The
expression in eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-start} contains single logarithms of
the form $\alpha_s\ln[Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}/\mu_R]$ and
$\alpha_s\ln[Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}/\mu_F]$. These logarithms can be
resummed by fixing $\mu_R=\mu_F=Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}$. Also, we can set
$\alpha_s(k)=\alpha_s(k_t)=\alpha_s(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})$ and obtain a
finite expression, up to subleading terms of order $\alpha_s^2$.

In order to facilitate the calculation of
$\mathcal{F}_{qq}$ in four dimensions, we add and subtract
a counterterm, $\mathrm{CT}$, which has the following form:
\begin{multline}
    \mathrm{CT}=\int \dZ \frac{\alpha_s(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{2\pi} \int\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi} \times \\
    \times \left[\int_{x_\ell}^1\frac{dz}{z}\hat P_{qq}(z;\varepsilon)\frac{f_q(x_\ell/z,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{f_q(x_\ell,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}-\int_0^1dz\frac{2 C_\ell}{1-z}\right]\times \\ \times \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)}{v}\right)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\,.
  \end{multline}
  Thanks to the introduction of this counterterm, we can recast
$\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{ISR}}$ in the form of
eq.~(\ref{eq:Sigma-NNLL}):
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FISR-split}
  \mathcal{F}_{qq} = \frac{\alpha_s(Q)}{\pi}\left( \delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm rec,qq}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc,qq}\right)+ \frac{\alpha_s(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{2\pi} C^{(1)}_{\rm hc,qq} \mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}\,.
\end{equation}
The constant terms $C^{(1)}_{\rm hc,qq}$ plays the same role as an integrated counterterm in fixed-order calculations, whereas the sum $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm rec,qq}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc,qq}$ represents the subtracted contribution of real radiation. We now proceed to the detailed derivation of eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-split}. 

First, we calculate $C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc, qq}}$. This quantity is obtained by adding $\mathrm{CT}$ to the last two lines of eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-start}. This gives:

\section{ NNLL resummation: one-jettiness}
\label{sec:one-jettiness}

There are various possibilities to define observables that are sensitive
radiation out of the plane identified by the beam and the $Z$
boson. One that is widely studied for its remarkable factorisation
properties in SCET is 1-jettiness, defined as
%One-jettiness is a special case of the more general $N$-jettiness event shape~\cite{Stewart:2010tn}, which quantifies how well the final state can be described by $N$ narrow jets. 
\begin{equation}
 \label{eq:T1}
 \mathcal{T}_1\equiv \sum_{i} \min\left\{\frac{2 P_1\cdot q_i}{Q_1}, \frac{2 P_2\cdot q_i}{Q_2}, \frac{2 p_J \cdot q_i}{Q_3}\right\}\,,
\end{equation}
where $q_i$ is the four-momentum of a \emph{hadron}, and
$Q_1, Q_2, Q_3$ three momentum scales.  When $\mathcal{T}_1\to 0$, the
outgoing jet $p_J$ recoils against the $Z$ boson. We can also consider a normalised version of 1-jettiness,
$\tau_1\equiv \mathcal{T}_1/M$, with $M$ a momentum-scale normalisation.
A particular choice of $Q_1, Q_2, Q_3$
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Qi-inv}
  Q_1 = \frac{2 P_1\cdot p_J}{p_{T,J}}\,,  \quad Q_2 = \frac{2 P_2\cdot p_J}{p_{T,J}}\,,
  \quad Q_3 = 2 p_{T,J}\,,
\end{equation}
where $p_{T,J}$ is the transverse momentum of the outgoing jet with
respect to the beam, provides a definition of $\mathcal{T}_1$ that is
manifestly invariant under boosts along the beam direction:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:T1-binv}
	\mathcal{T}_1\equiv  \sum_{i} k_{t,i}\min\{e^{(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, e^{-(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, \cosh(\eta_{i}-\eta_J)-\cos(\phi_{i}-\phi_J)\}\,.
\end{equation}
With this definition, $\mathcal{T}_1$ is independent of the two incoming momenta. To reduce
the impact of experimental systematics in the measurement of particle momenta, we
set $M=H_T$, with $H_T$ the scalar sum of all
hadron transverse momenta, leading to our preferred definition of $\tau_1$, namely
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:tau1-binv}
	\tau_1\equiv  \sum_{i} \frac{k_{t,i}}{H_T}\min\{e^{(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, e^{-(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, \cosh(\eta_{i}-\eta_J)-\cos(\phi_{i}-\phi_J)\}\,.
\end{equation}

\add{One-jettiness is an rIRC safe observable.}

The starting point is to consider the behaviour of this observable in the presence of a single soft emission collinear to leg $\ell$. 
By definition,
  $k\cdot p_\ell/E_\ell=k_t^{(\ell)} e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}$, where
  $k_t^{(\ell)}$ is the relative transverse momentum with respect to
  the emitting leg $\ell$, $\eta^{(\ell)}$ is the rapidity of the
  emission, again with respect to the emitting leg $\ell$. Using the general definition $\tau_1=\mathcal{T}_1/M$, with $\mathcal{T}_1$ in eq.~(\ref{eq:T1}) we obtain the same expression for
  the same expression for any leg $\ell$:
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:tau1-sc-1emsn}
    \tau_1(\{\tilde p\},k)\simeq \tau^{\rm sc}_1(\{\tilde p\},k) =  \frac{2E_\ell}{x_\ell Q_\ell}\frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{M} e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}\,.
  \end{equation}
Using the explicit expression for the scales $Q_1,Q_2,Q_3$ in eq.~(\ref{eq:Qi-inv}), as well as the fact that, for soft emissions, $H_T\simeq q_T$ and $\eta_J\simeq \eta_3$, we obtain
  \begin{equation}
\label{eq:tau1-sc-1emsn}
  \tau_1(\{\tilde p\},k) \simeq \tau^{\rm sc}_1(\{\tilde p\},k) = \frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{q_T}e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}\times \left\{
    \begin{split}
    & e^{\eta_3}\,,\qquad\qquad \ell=1\\
    & e^{-\eta_3}\,, \qquad\quad\> \ell = 2 \\
      & \cosh\eta_3 \,,\qquad \ell=3\,.
    \end{split}
  \right. 
\end{equation}

Note that the one-jettiness is an additive observables, meaning that 
for multiple soft and collinear emissions they take the form:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:Vsc-additive}
	V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\cdots, k_n\right) = \sum_{i=1}^nV_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i\right)\,.
\end{equation}

From the Eq.~(\ref{eq:tau1-sc-1emsn}), we read the single-emission properties of
$\tau_1$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:tau1-1em-props}
  a=1\,, \quad b_\ell=1\,,  \quad g_\ell(\phi) = 1\,,\qquad \ell=1,2,3\,,
\end{equation}
and 
\begin{equation}
 \label{eq:tau1-1em-props1}
d_\ell = \frac{Q}{q_T}\times \left\{
    \begin{split}
    & e^{\eta_3} \,,\quad \ell=1\,,\\
    &  e^{-\eta_3} \,,\quad \ell=2\,, \\
      & \cosh \eta_3 \,, \quad \ell=3\,.
    \end{split}
  \right. 
\end{equation}

We now examine $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\lambda)$ together with the various contributions to $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NNLL}}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFNNLL} for each observable. To clarify their physical origin, we organise the discussion according to the kinematic region and corresponding approximation from which each contribution arises: soft and collinear, hard and collinear, and soft wide-angle radiation.

\subsection{Soft and collinear contributions}
\label{sec:sc-contributions}
In this section we collect all contributions arising from soft and
collinear emissions, as stated in the title. These are
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}$ at NLL, and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$ and
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$, and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$ at NNLL. The NLL function
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm NLL}$ and the NNLL functions $\delta
\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$ and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$ involve
gluons widely separated in angle, while $\delta
\mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$ accounts for the splitting of one soft and collinear gluon into a $q\bar{q}$ or a gluon pair.

We will compute these contributions analytically whenever
possible. Otherwise, they will be computed numerically using the ARES
Monte Carlo procedure~\cite{Banfi:2014sua}.   Given a
set of rescaled variables $\{\zeta_i,\ell_i,\phi_i\}$, the
corresponding soft and collinear momenta, and hence the limit in
eq.~\eqref{eq:Vsc} can be computed numerically with
CAESAR~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}. Therefore, we expect all soft and
collinear contributions to be calculable with CAESAR out of the box.

\paragraph{NLL resummation.}

Soft and collinear emissions widely separated in angle give the only NLL function embodying the effect of resolved emission. This is the well-known function 

For event shapes, $\Vsc{\{k_i\}}/v$ is only a function of $\{\zeta_i,\ell_i, \phi_i\}$ fixed. Therefore one can freely
integrate over emissions' rapidities. One-jettiness is additive, so
  $\FNLL(\lambda)$ takes the standard form for additive event
  shapes~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq: FNLL-tau_1}
  \FNLL(\lambda)=\frac{e^{-\gamma_E \RpNLL}}{\Gamma(1+\RpNLL)}\,.
\end{equation}


\paragraph{NNLL resummation.} The soft and collinear corrections at NNLL are, in order of complexity,
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$, and
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$. On top of the soft-collinear
emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ widely separated in angle/rapidity, 
that are needed for any resummation, the two
functions $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$
involve the presence of an additional soft and collinear emission $k$,
parametrised in terms of the usual variable $\zeta=\Vsc{k}/v$, as well
as its emitter's leg $\ell$, and azimuthal angle $\phi^{(\ell)}$. 
For $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}$, this
additional emission is required to split into a pair $k_a$ and $k_b$,
with further dimensionless variables parametrising the splitting 
(cf.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFcorrel-general}). 

The general expression for $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}$ is given by 
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:dFhc-general}
   \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}=\sum_\ell \frac{\alpha_s\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\alpha_s(Q)\left(a+b_\ell\right)}B_\ell \int\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}
 \int \dZ  \times\\ \times  \left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right) -\Theta(1-\zeta)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right]\,.
\end{multline}

The additive result for this expression is~\cite{Banfi:2014sua,Banfi:2018mcq}:
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:dFhc-general-add}
   \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}=-\FNLL(\lambda)\left(\psi^{(0)}(1+\RpNLL)+\gamma_E\right)\sum_\ell \frac{\alpha_s\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\alpha_s(Q)\left(a+b_\ell\right)}B_\ell =\\
   =\frac{d\FNLL}{d\RpNLL}\sum_\ell \frac{\alpha_s\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\alpha_s(Q)\left(a+b_\ell\right)}B_\ell \,.
\end{multline}
For final-state radiation ($\ell=3$), this correction reproduces the result obtained for the $\ee$ 3-jet scenario~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. This follows from the lack of PDFs, for which $B_\ell = \gamma^{(0)}_\ell$ (see Eq.~\eqref{eq:Bell-ISR}).
One-jettiness is an additive observable. Therefore, using Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFhc-general-add},
we find
 \begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dFhc-tau_1}
    \delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm hc}(\lambda) = \frac{\alpha_s(\sqrt{\tau_1}\,Q)}{2\,\alpha_s(Q)}\frac{d\FNLL}{d\RpNLL} \sum_{\ell}B_\ell\,.
  \end{equation}

We now discuss the function $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}$, whose general expression is given by
\begin{multline}
	\label{eq:dFsc-general}
	\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{sc}}(\lambda)=\frac{\pi}{\alpha_s(Q)}\int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\sum_{\ell=1}^3\left[\Delta R'_{\ell,\mathrm{NNLL}}+R''_{\ell,\mathrm{NNLL}}\left(\ln\frac{d_\ell g_\ell(\phi)}{\zeta}-b_\ell\ln\frac{2E_\ell}{Q}\right)\right]\times 
	 \\ \times \int \dZ  \left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{k,\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)
       -\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\Theta(1-\zeta)
     \right]\,.
\end{multline}

The soft-collinear correction for additive observables yields the same
form regardless of what the observable is~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn, Banfi:2018mcq}:
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:dFsc-general-add}
    \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm sc}(\lambda)  = -\frac{\pi}{\alpha_s(Q)}\FNLL(\lambda)  \left[\left(\delta R'_{\NNLL}+\sum_{\ell=1}^3 R''_{\ell,\NNLL} \left(\langle  \ln(d_\ell g_\ell)\rangle-b_\ell\ln\frac{2E_\ell}{Q}\right)\right)\left(\psi^{(0)}(1+\RpNLL)+\gamma_E\right)\right.\\ 
    +\left.\frac{R''}{2}\left(\left(\psi^{(0)}(1+\RpNLL)+\gamma_E\right)^2-\psi^{(1)}(1+\RpNLL)+\frac{\pi^2}{6}\right)\right]\\   =
    \frac{\pi}{\alpha_s(Q)} \left[\left(\delta R'_{\NNLL}+\sum_{\ell=1}^3 R''_{\ell,\NNLL} \left(\langle  \ln(d_\ell g_\ell)\rangle-b_\ell\ln\frac{2E_\ell}{Q}\right)\right) \frac{d\FNLL}{d\RpNLL}-\frac{R''}{2}\left(\frac{d^2\FNLL}{d(\RpNLL)^2}+\frac{\pi^2}{6}\right)\right]\,.
\end{multline}

The final soft and collinear contribution is the 
correlated correction, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{correl}}$, 
whose general expression is
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:dFcorrel-general}
\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{correl}}(\lambda)=\sum_{\ell=1}^3\frac{\lambda R''_{\ell,\mathrm{NNLL}}}{2a\beta_0\alpha_s(Q)}
\int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi^{(\ell)}}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty
\frac{d\mu^2}{\mu^2(1+\mu^2)}\int_0^1dz\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\frac{1}{2!}\mathcal{A}^2(z,\mu,\phi)\times\\
\int\dZ\left[ \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_a,k_b,\{k_i\}\right)}{v}\right) - 
\lim_{\mu^2\to0}\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_a+k_b,\{k_i\}\right)}{v}\right) \right]\,,
\end{multline}
where $k = k_a + k_b$ so that $k_{a/b}$ are the partons into which
emission $k$ splits, $\mu^2 \equiv m^2/k_t^2$ is the rescaled
invariant mass of the splitting, and $\mathcal{A}^2$ is the
double-emission function defined in Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.

For additive observables this simplifies to
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:dFcorrel-general-add}
\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{correl}}(\lambda)=-\FNLL(\lambda)
\sum_{\ell=1}^3\frac{\lambda R''_{\ell,\mathrm{NNLL}}}{2a\beta_0\alpha_s(Q)}\times\\
\times
\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi^{(\ell)}}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty
\frac{d\mu^2}{\mu^2(1+\mu^2)}\int_0^1dz\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\frac{1}{2!}\mathcal{A}^2(z,\mu,\phi)
\ln f^{(\ell)}_{\mathrm{correl}}\left(z,\mu,\phi,\phi^{(\ell)}\right)\,.
\end{multline}
One-jettiness is identical to thrust in
  $e^+e^-$ annihilation, so we adapt the angularity result
  of~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq} at $x=0$, which gives
\begin{equation}
 	f^{(\ell)}_{\rm correl}\left(z,\mu,\phi,\phi^{(\ell)}\right)=\sqrt{1+\mu^2}\,,
	\label{eq: f-correl-tau_1}
 \end{equation}
and hence
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dFcorrel-tau_1}
	\delta  \mathcal{F}_{\rm correl}(\lambda)=\frac{\pi}{2\alpha_s(Q)}\lambda\,R'' \zeta_2 \FNLL(\lambda)\,.
\end{equation}

\subsection {Hard and collinear contributions}
\label{sec:hc-contributions}
We now consider the contributions which arise from the hard and collinear region. These are the recoil corrections:
\begin{itemize} 
\item $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}$, which arises from a hard-collinear emission recoiling against the soft-collinear ensemble at the level of the kinematics.
\item  $\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}$, which first appears in near-to-planar three-jet events~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn} and accounts for correlations between the spin of a hard-collinear gluon and the event plane.
\end{itemize}

Both corrections involve, in addition to the soft-collinear limit of Eq.~\eqref{eq:Vsc}, the hard-collinear limit
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vhc}
  \frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\}, k_{\mathrm{hc}}, k_1,\dots,k_n)}{v}\equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{\Vfull{k_{\mathrm{hc}}, k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
where $k_{\mathrm{hc}}$ is a single hard-collinear parton 
and $k_1,\cdots k_n$ are an ensemble of soft \emph{and} colliner emissions,
all emitted independently.

The general expression for $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}$ is
 \begin{multline}
 \label{eq:dFrec-general}
   \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}=\sum_\ell \frac{\alpha_s\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\alpha_s(Q)\left(a+b_\ell\right)}\int_0^1 dz\,P_\ell(z) \int\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}
 \int \dZ  \times\\ \times  \left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k_{\mathrm{hc}},\{k_i\})}{v}\right) -\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right]\,.
\end{multline}

For additive observables, where
$V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k) = f_{\mathrm{hc}}(z)\cdot
V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)$, this simplifies to  \ndp{Does this $V_{\mathrm{hc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k\right)$ need a $k_{\mathrm{hc}}$?}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dFrec-general-add}
  \delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec} = -\FNLL(\lambda)
  \sum_\ell
  \frac{\alpha_s\!\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}
       {\alpha_s(Q)(a+b_\ell)}
  \int_0^1 dz\,P_\ell(z)\ln f_{\mathrm{hc}}(z)\,.
\end{equation}
Since one-jettiness is additive, 
we use Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFrec-general-add} with the results
  from Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq} at $x=0$, giving
  \ndp{Add a comment about how the dynamics change between ISR and FSR, physically speaking.}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tau_1-fhc}
f_{\mathrm{hc}}(z)=
\begin{cases}
z\,, \quad \ell=1,2\,, \\
1/z\,, \quad \ell=3\,. 
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The $z$-integration over the initial-state legs $\ell=1,2$, cannot be
performed explicitly since $P_\ell(z)$ involves PDFs (cf.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:Pell-ISR}). 
For the final-state leg $\ell=3$, the result is identical to the 3-jet $e^+e^-$ scenario,
where for a final-state quark:
\begin{equation}

  \int_0^1dz \, P_\ell(z) \ln f_{\mathrm{hc}}  = -C_F\left(\frac{5}{4}-\frac{\pi^2}{3}\right)\,,
\end{equation}
while for a final-state gluon

\end{equation}

The general expression for $\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm rec}$ is:	
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:DFrec-general}
\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec}}(\lambda)=
\frac{\alpha_s\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_g)}\right)}{\left(a+b_g\right)\alpha_s(Q)}\int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}
\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\int_0^1dz\Delta P_g\left(z,\phi\right)\int\dZ\times\\ 
\times \left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right) -\Theta\left(1-\zeta\right)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right]\,.
\end{multline}

\subsection{Soft wide angle contributions}
\label{sec:wa-contributions}
Finally, we consider contributions originating from a soft wide-angle region. The correction $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm wa}$ arises from a soft emission near $\eta=0$ and accounts for the variation between the observable and its soft-collinear parametrisation for a single soft wide-angle emission, accompanied by an ensemble of soft-collinear gluons. $\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm wa}$ represents the NNLL contribution arising from wide angled resolved emissions.

These calculations involve dipoles and therefore require the use of Sudakov variables. This is the idea that a single emission $k$ can be decomposed along any pair of light-like momenta $(p_i,p_j)$, which constitute the $(ij)$ dipole. The decomposition is given by:
\begin{equation}
k^{(ij)}=z^{(i)}p_i+z^{(j)}p_j+\kappa^{(ij)}\cos\phi^{(ij)}n_{\rm{in}}^{(ij)}+\kappa^{(ij)}\sin\phi^{(ij)}n_{\rm{out}}^{(ij)}\,,
\label{eq:Sudakov-decomp}
\end{equation}
where all components of this Sudakov decomposition can be found in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.

These contributions use the constraint on the observable, $V_{\mathrm{wa}}$
 which is the observable in the limit when a single soft gluon is emitted at large angles.
 \ndp{We need to show the limit.}


The general expression for $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm wa}$ is:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:dFwa-general}
	\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}=\sum_{(ij)}C_{(ij)}\frac{\alpha_s(v^{1/a}Q)}{a\,\alpha_s(Q)}\int_0^\infty 
	\int_{-\infty}^\infty d\eta^{(ij)}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi^{(ij)}}{2\pi}\times\\
	\times \int \dZ\left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{wa}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\}, k^{(ij)},\{k_i\}\right)}{v}\right) -\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\}, k^{(ij)},\{k_i\}\right)}{v}\right)\right]\,,
\end{multline}
where, $C_{(ij)}$ is a colour factor that depends on the
flavour (quark or gluon) of the constituents of each of the three
dipoles that build the Born event. These are
$C_{qq}=2C_F-C_A, C_{qg}=C_{gq}=C_A$.
The soft wide-angle correction for this observable is found to be:
\begin{equation}
	\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}\left(\lambda\right)= \frac{\alpha_s(\tau_1Q)}{\alpha_s(Q)}\sum_{(ij)} \frac{C_{(ij)}}{2} 
\left[ \ln^2 \frac{d_i}{d_j} - 2(I_{ij,k} + I_{ji,k}) \right]\,,
\end{equation}
where the $I_{ij,k}$'s are those found in Eq. (51) in Ref.~\cite{Alioli:2023rxx}. \ndp{Note about the radiator being split around $\eta=0$ and how the numerical calculations
are taken from Ref.~\cite{Jouttenus:2011wh}.}

The general expression for $\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\rm wa}$ is:

\begin{multline}
\label{eq:DFwa-general}
	\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}=\sum_{(ij)}C_{(ij)}\frac{\alpha_s(v^{1/a}Q)}{a\,\alpha_s(Q)}\ln\frac{Q_{ij}}{Q}\int_0^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}
	\int_{-\infty}^\infty d\eta^{(ij)}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\int \dZ\times \\
	\times \left[\Theta\left(1-\lim_{v\to0}\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\}, k,\{k_i\}\right)}{v}\right) -\lim_{v\to0}\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\}, \{k_i\}\right)}{v}\right)\left(1-\zeta\right)\right]\,.
\end{multline}
 For additive observables, this correction is defined to be
 \begin{equation}
 \label{eq:DFwa-general-add}
 	\Delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}(\lambda)=-\FNLL(\lambda)\left(\psi^{(0)}\left(1+R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)+\gamma_E\right)\frac{\alpha_s\left(v^{1/a}Q\right)}{a\alpha_s(Q)}\sum_{(ij)}C_{(ij)}\ln\left(\frac{Q_{ij}}{Q}\right)\,,
 \end{equation}
 where for one-jettiness

    & = \frac{\alpha_s(\tau_1\,Q)}{\alpha_s(Q)}\frac{d\FNLL}{d\RpNLL}\sum_{(ij)} C_{(ij)}\ln\left(\frac{Q_{ij}}{Q}\right)\,. 
  \end{split}
  \end{equation}

&-2C_c\left(\ln\frac{d_c Q}{2E_c} - \ln\frac{Q^2_{ab}}{Q^2} + \ln\frac{Q^2_{ac}}{Q^2} + \ln\frac{Q^2_{bc}}{Q^2}\right)\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Using the relations: $2 q_i\cdot q_j = Q_{ij}^2$ and $d_\ell=\frac{2E_\ell}{Q_\ell}$, it is clear that this is exactly what is presented above, and so the matching of $h_{11}$ is correct.

\subsubsection{h24}
Using the mapping:
\begin{equation}
	h_{24}=\frac{C_{3}^{(2)}}{16}\,,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}

which is exactly correct.

\subsubsection{h23}
Using the mapping, we find that:
\begin{equation}
h_{23} = -\frac{C^{(2)}_2}{12}\,,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
	&C_{2}^{(2)}=X_2^{(2)}+\frac{3}{2}\left[X_{0,1}^{(1+1)}+X_{1,0}^{(1+1)}\right]=\\
	&J_{c,2}^{(2)}+\mathcal{I}_{ab,2}^{(2)}+\mathcal{I}_{ba,2}^{(2)}+\hat{S}_{\kappa,2}^{(2)}+3\left(J_{c,3}^{(2)}\ln\frac{Q_c Q}{\mu^2}+\mathcal{I}_{ab,3}^{(2)}\ln\frac{Q_a Q}{\mu^2}+\mathcal{I}_{ba,3}^{(2)}\ln\frac{Q_bQ}{\mu^2}+\hat{S}_{\kappa,3}^{(2)}\ln\frac{Q}{\mu}\right)\\
	&+\frac{3}{2}\left[\left(\mathcal{I}_{ab,0}^{(1)}+\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_a Q}{\mu^2}\right)\,J_{c,1}^{(1)}+\left(\mathcal{I}_{ba,0}^{(1)}+\mathcal{I}_{ba,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_b Q}{\mu^2}\right)\,J_{c,1}^{(1)}+\left(\mathcal{I}_{ab,0}^{(1)}+\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_a Q}{\mu^2}\right)\,\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\right]\\
	&+\frac{3}{2}\left[\left(J_{c,0}^{(1)}+J_{c,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_c Q}{\mu^2}\right)\hat{S}_{1}^{(1)}+\left(\mathcal{I}_{ab,0}^{(1)}+\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_a Q}{\mu^2}\right)\hat{S}_{1}^{(1)}+\left(\mathcal{I}_{ba,0}^{(1)}+\mathcal{I}_{ba,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_b Q}{\mu^2}\right)\hat{S}_{1}^{(1)}\right]\\
	&+\frac{3}{2}\left[\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\left(J_{c,0}^{(1)}+J_{c,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_c Q}{\mu^2}\right)+\mathcal{I}_{ba,1}^{(1)}\left(J_{c,0}^{(1)}+J_{c,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_c Q}{\mu^2}\right)+\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\left(\mathcal{I}_{ba,0}^{(1)}+\mathcal{I}_{ba,1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q_b Q}{\mu^2}\right)\right]\\
	&+\frac{3}{2}\left[J_{c,1}^{(1)}\left(\hat{S}_{0}^{(1)}+\hat{S}_{1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q}{\mu}\right)+\mathcal{I}_{ab,1}^{(1)}\left(\hat{S}_{0}^{(1)}+\hat{S}_{1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q}{\mu}\right)+\mathcal{I}_{ba,1}^{(1)}\left(\hat{S}_{0}^{(1)}+\hat{S}_{1}^{(1)}\ln\frac{Q}{\mu}\right)\right]\,.
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Expanding the terms we find that:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
C_{2}^{(2)} &= 6(C_a+C_b+C_c)\beta_{0S} \\
&+12(C_a+C_b+C_c)\Bigg[
   -2C_a B_a - 2C_b B_b - 2C_c B_c \\
&-2C_a\left(\ln\frac{Q_a}{Q}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_c}{Q_a Q_c}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_b}{Q_a Q_b}-\ln\frac{2q_b\cdot q_c}{Q_b Q_c}\right)\\
	&-2C_b\left(\ln\frac{Q_b}{Q}-\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_c}{Q_a Q_c}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_b}{Q_a Q_b}+\ln\frac{2q_b\cdot q_c}{Q_b Q_c}\right)\\
	&-2C_c\left(\ln\frac{Q_c}{Q}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_c}{Q_a Q_c}-\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_b}{Q_a Q_b}+\ln\frac{2q_b\cdot q_c}{Q_b Q_c}\right)\Bigg]\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Using the relation $\beta_{0S}=\frac{11C_A-4T_F n_f}{3}$ (where the $S$ refers to SCET definition), we find:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
h_{23}&= -\frac{11C_A-4T_F n_f}{6}(C_a+C_b+C_c) \\
&-(C_a+C_b+C_c)\Bigg[
   -2C_a B_a - 2C_b B_b - 2C_c B_c \\
&-2C_a\left(\ln\frac{Q_a}{Q}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_c}{Q_a Q_c}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_b}{Q_a Q_b}-\ln\frac{2q_b\cdot q_c}{Q_b Q_c}\right)\\
	&-2C_b\left(\ln\frac{Q_b}{Q}-\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_c}{Q_a Q_c}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_b}{Q_a Q_b}+\ln\frac{2q_b\cdot q_c}{Q_b Q_c}\right)\\
	&-2C_c\left(\ln\frac{Q_c}{Q}+\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_c}{Q_a Q_c}-\ln\frac{2q_a\cdot q_b}{Q_a Q_b}+\ln\frac{2q_b\cdot q_c}{Q_b Q_c}\right)\Bigg]\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Using equation~\ref{eq:SCET-h11} we find that:
\begin{equation}
h_{23}=  -\frac{11C_A-4T_F n_f}{6}(C_a+C_b+C_c)-(C_a+C_b+C_c)h_{11}\,.
\end{equation}
This gives perfect agreement since $h_{23}$ is defined as the following in CAESAR:
\begin{equation}
	h_{23}=-2\pi\beta_{0A}(C_a+C_b+C_c)-(C_a+C_b+C_c)h_{11}\,,
\end{equation}
where $\beta_{0A}=\frac{11C_A-4T_R\,n_f}{12\pi}$ (the $A$ refers to ARES definition).
