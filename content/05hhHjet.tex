\section{Introduction}
In this chapter, we present original research on the \textsc{ARES} method for $hh \to Z+\mathrm{jet}$ production. 
This constitutes the first application of the \textsc{ARES} framework to hadron--hadron collisions at NNLL accuracy. 
As a test case, we consider event-shape observables in processes where a $Z$ boson recoils against a high-transverse-momentum jet, 
which provides a clean environment and a well-defined hard scale, making it particularly suitable for precision studies and resummation.

We concentrate on observables sensitive to out-of-plane radiation, and present a general NNLL resummation formula, which includes both initial state and final state radiation.
The NNLL contributions arising from soft emissions, as well as hard-collinear radiation in the final state, can, to a large extent, be obtained using the same formalism developed
for three-jet event shapes in $e^+e^-$ annihilation, as outlined in Ch.~\ref{ch:resum}. 

A key novel ingredient of this resummation formula, and of this work, is the extension of the \textsc{ARES} framework to include initial-state radiation effects at NNLL accuracy. 
Unlike in $e^+e^-$ collisions, where only final-state radiation is present, hadron collisions involve additional radiation from the incoming partons. 
Accounting for these effects at NNLL requires a non-trivial generalisation of the formalism and constitutes one of the main advances of this chapter.

Combining these elements, we derive a general NNLL resummation formula applicable to a broad class of observables in events with a $Z$ boson produced in association with a jet. 
To illustrate the method and highlight its features, we focus on three representative observables: one-jettiness, transverse thrust, and thrust minor. 
These examples make explicit how the different components of the calculation enter in practice, and help expose subtleties of the formalism that are not immediately apparent at a more abstract level.

The chapter proceeds as follows. We first define the observables and determine the necessary resummation parameters. 
We then present the master NNLL cumulant formula. Next, we compute its individual ingredients for each observable, explicitly showing how the initial-state radiation contributions enter the formalism.


\section{Proof-of-concept observables in $Z$+jet events}
\label{sec:observable}

We consider the production of a $Z$ boson and a hard jet in hadron collisions,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Z+jet}
  h_1(P_1)+h_2(P_2) \to Z(q) + \mathrm{jet}(p_J)+\dots\,,
\end{equation}
where $h_1, h_2$ are the two incoming hadrons, and the dots represent
accompanying hadrons. At Born level, such events results from two
partons of momenta $p_1=x_1 P_1, p_2=x_2 P_2$ producing a $Z$ boson
$q$ and a hard parton $p_3$. Beyond Born level, $p_3$ will recoil
against additional partons. Its momentum will change to
$\tilde p_3$, and it will be clustered with other partons to form a hard jet $p_J$. At Born level, $p_J=p_3$. 

We set the $z$-axis along the direction of the beam, the $y$-axis
along the direction of the transverse momentum of the $Z$ boson. There
are various possibilities to define observables that measure radiation
out of the plane identified by the beam and the $Z$ boson.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{One-jettiness} is a special case of the more general $N$-jettiness event shape~\cite{Stewart:2010tn}, which quantifies how well the final state can be described by $N$ narrow jets. For $N=1$, one-jettiness is defined as
\begin{equation}
 \label{eq:T1}
 \mathcal{T}_1\equiv \sum_{i} \min\left\{\frac{2 P_1\cdot q_i}{Q_1}, \frac{2 P_2\cdot q_i}{Q_2}, \frac{2 p_J \cdot q_i}{Q_3}\right\}\,,
\end{equation}
where $q_i$ is the four-momentum of a \emph{hadron}, and
$Q_1, Q_2, Q_3$ three momentum scales.  When $\mathcal{T}_1\to 0$, the
outgoing jet $p_J$ recoils against the $Z$ boson. We can also consider a normalised version of 1-jettiness,
$\tau_1\equiv \mathcal{T}_1/M$, with $M$ a momentum-scale normalisation.
A particular choice of $Q_1, Q_2, Q_3$
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Qi-inv}
  Q_1 = \frac{2 P_1\cdot p_J}{p_{T,J}}\,,  \quad Q_2 = \frac{2 P_2\cdot p_J}{p_{T,J}}\,,
  \quad Q_3 = 2 p_{T,J}\,,
\end{equation}
where $p_{T,J}$ is the transverse momentum of the outgoing jet with
respect to the beam, provides a definition of $\mathcal{T}_1$ that is
manifestly invariant under boosts along the beam direction:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:T1-binv}
	\mathcal{T}_1\equiv  \sum_{i} k_{t,i}\min\{e^{(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, e^{-(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, \cosh(\eta_{i}-\eta_J)-\cos(\phi_{i}-\phi_J)\}\,,
\end{equation}
which is of course independent of the two incoming momenta. To reduce
the impact of experimental systematics in the measurement of particle momenta, we
set $M=H_T$, with $H_T$ the scalar sum of all
hadron transverse momenta, leading to our preferred definition of $\tau_1$, namely
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:tau1-binv}
	\tau_1\equiv  \sum_{i} \frac{k_{t,i}}{H_T}\min\{e^{(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, e^{-(\eta_i-\eta_J)}, \cosh(\eta_{i}-\eta_J)-\cos(\phi_{i}-\phi_J)\}\,.
\end{equation}

The normalisation factors are $Q_i = 2 \rho_i E_i$, with $\rho_i$ depending on the reference frame (e.g., $\rho_i = 1$ in the laboratory frame). While $\mathcal{T}_1$ is dimensionful, it is often convenient to define the dimensionless variable $\tau_1 \equiv \mathcal{T}_1 / Q$, where $Q$ is the hard scale of the interaction.

\item \textbf{Transverse thrust}, first introduced in Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2004nk}, is the extension of the $e^+e^-$ event-shape thrust (cf.\ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:thrust}) to hadronic collisions. It is defined in the transverse plane as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tperp-def}
  T_t \equiv \max_{\vec{n}_\perp}
  \frac{\sum_i \left| \vec{q}_{\perp i} \cdot \vec{n}_\perp \right|}
       {\sum_i \left| \vec{q}_{\perp i} \right|},
  \qquad
  \tau_t \equiv 1 - T_\perp \,,
\end{equation}
where $\vec{p}_{\perp i}$ denotes the transverse momentum of final-state particle $i$ with respect to the beam direction, and the unit vector $\vec{n}_\perp$ that maximises this quantity is referred to as the \emph{transverse thrust axis}. This variable is invariant
under longitudinal boosts, and, in the present case, can be defined in
terms of hadron transverse momenta $\vec q_{t,i}$ and the $Z$-boson
transverse momentum $\vec q_T$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:tthr}
  T_t\equiv \frac{\sum_i |\vec q_{t,i}\cdot \vec q_T|}{ H_T q_T}\,.
\end{equation}
In particular, the near-to-planar region corresponds to $\tau_t\equiv 1-T_t$ close to zero. Both $\tau_1$ and $\tau_t$ are sensitive to out-of-plane radiation through imbalance between large in-plane momentum components. 

\item \textbf{Thrust minor}, also introduced in Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2004nk}, is a measure of out-of-plane radiation and is defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Tmin-def}
  T_m \equiv \frac{\sum_i |\vec q_{\perp i}\times \vec n_\perp|}{\sum_i q_{\perp i}}\,.
\end{equation}
For the present case, this can be written as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Tmin}
  T_m \equiv \sum_i\frac{ |\vec q_{t,i}\times \vec q_T|}{H_T q_T}\,.
\end{equation}
Similarly to transverse thrust, the thrust minor is in analogy with an existing observable defined in $e^+e^-$ annihilation known as ``thrust major''.

\section{General Formalism for NNLL Resummation}

The one-jettiness, one minus the transverse thrust and the thrust
minor are all examples of rIRC safe observables. These are generic
functions $V(\{\tilde p\},k_1,\dots,k_n)$ of final-state momenta
$\{\tilde p\}\equiv\{\tilde p_1,\tilde p_2,\tilde p_3\}$, accompanied
by additional emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$. Any rIRC safe observable $V$
is zero at Born level, i.e.\
$V(\{\tilde p_1,\tilde p_2,\tilde p_3\}) = V(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\})=0$, and
satisfies additional conditions on its scaling with an arbitrary
number of soft and/or collinear emissions~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}.  As
such, all the aforementioned observables fall within the scope of the
CAESAR/ARES formalism.

The starting point is to consider the behaviour of each observable in the presence of a single soft emission collinear to leg $\ell$. We consider one observable at a time, introducing the relevant kinematical variables as we go along.

\begin{itemize}

\item{\textbf{One-jettiness.}} By definition,
  $k\cdot p_\ell/E_\ell=k_t^{(\ell)} e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}$, where
  $k_t^{(\ell)}$ is the relative transverse momentum with respect to
  the emitting leg $\ell$, $\eta^{(\ell)}$ is the rapidity of the

  \end{equation}
Using the explicit expression for the scales $Q_1,Q_2,Q_3$ in eq.~(\ref{eq:Qi-inv}), as well as the fact that, for soft emissions, $H_T\simeq q_T$ and $\eta_J\simeq \eta_3$, we obtain
  \begin{equation}
\label{eq:tau1-sc-1emsn}
  \tau_1(\{\tilde p\},k) \simeq \tau^{\mathrm{sc}}_1(\{\tilde p\},k) = \frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{q_T}e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}\times \left\{
    \begin{split}
    & e^{\eta_3}\,,\qquad\qquad \ell=1\\

      & \cosh\eta_3 \,,\qquad \ell=3\,.
    \end{split}
  \right. 
\end{equation}

\item {\textbf{Transverse thrust.}} This case is the most complicated. First we observe that, in the presence of a single soft emission $k$, we have
\begin{equation}
\label{eq: taut-1em}
  \begin{split}
  \tau_t(\{\tilde p\},k)& \simeq  \frac{k_t-|k_y|+\sqrt{\tilde p_{3y}^2+\tilde p_{3x}^2}-|\tilde p_{3y}|}{q_T}= \frac{k_t-|k_y|}{q_T}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{k_x^2}{q_T^2}\right)\,.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
This gives a different behaviour according to the emitting leg:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:taut-sc-1emsn}
  \tau_t(\{\tilde p\},k) \simeq \tau^{\mathrm{sc}}_t(\{\tilde p\},k) = \frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{q_T}\times \left\{
    \begin{split}
    & \left(1-|\cos\phi^{(\ell)}|\right) \,,\quad \ell=1,2\\
      & \frac{E_\ell}{q_T}e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}} \sin^2\phi^{(\ell)}\,,\qquad \ell=3\,.
    \end{split}
  \right. 
\end{equation}
\item{\textbf{Thrust minor.}} In this case, we need the out of plane
  component of transverse momentum of emission $k$. Setting the
  $y$-axis along the direction of the transverse momentum of the $Z$
  boson, we call this component $k_x$. We then introduce
  $\phi^{(\ell)}$, the azimuthal angle of the transverse momentum of
  emission $k$ with respect to leg $\ell$. Setting by convention
  $\phi^{(\ell)}=0$ when the emission is in the event plane gives
  $k_x\equiv k_t^{(\ell)} \sin\phi^{(\ell)}$ for any emitting leg
  $\ell$. With these kinematical variables, we obtain
  \begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
  T_m(\{\tilde p\},k)\simeq  \frac{|k_x|+|\tilde p_{3x}|}{q_T} \,.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Using transverse momentum conservation $\tilde p_{3x}+k_x=0$, we arrive at
our final expression
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Tm-sc-1emsn}
  \begin{split}
  T_m(\{\tilde p\},k)& \simeq  T^{\mathrm{sc}}_m(\{\tilde p\},k) = \frac{2|k_x|}{q_T} = 2 \frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{q_T} |\sin\phi^{(\ell)}| \,.
\end{split}
\end{equation}

multiple soft and collinear emissions $k_1, \dots, k_n$, eq.~(\ref{eq:Tmin}) can be
approximated as follows:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Tm-sc}
  \begin{split}
  T_m(\{\tilde p\},k_1,\dots,k_n) & \simeq \frac{1}{q_T} \left(\sum_{i=1}^n |k_{ix}| + |\tilde p_{3x}|\right)\\ & =\frac{1}{q_T} \left(\sum_{i=1}^n |k_{ix}| + \left|\sum_{i=1}^n k_{ix}\right|\right)\equiv T^{\mathrm{sc}}_m(\{\tilde p\},k_1,\dots,k_n)\,,
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
where we have used the fact that, in the presence of soft emissions,
$p_{\mathrm{t,jet}}\simeq q_T$, and that $\tilde p_{3x}$ can be obtained
from conservation of transverse momentum. For a soft emission $k$
collinear to leg $\ell$, $T^{\mathrm{sc}}_m(\{\tilde p\},k)$ is given by
eq.~(\ref{eq:Tm-sc-1emsn}).

\subsection{NNLL Master Formula}
The general formula for hadronic collisions for $Z$+jet events is adapted from the NNLL resummation formula
derived for three-jet observables in $e^+e^-$ annihilation (as shown in Eq.~\eqref{eq:Sigma-NNLL-fs}):
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:Sigma-NNLL-fs}
  \Sigma(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v)= e^{-R_{\mathrm{NNLL}}(v)} \prod_{\ell=1}^{n_i}\frac{q^{(\ell)}(x_\ell,v^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}}\mu_F)}{q^{(\ell)}(x_\ell,\mu_F)}  \times \\ \times
  \left(\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\lambda)\left(1+\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi}H^{(1)}(Q)
      +\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R v^{\frac{1}{a+b_3}})}{2\pi}\sum_{\ell}C_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}^{(1)}\right) +\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{\pi} \delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NNLL}}(\lambda)\right)\,,
\end{multline}
where $\mu_R$ and $\mu_F$ are the renormalisation and factorisation
scales in the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ scheme. 
%\ABquestion{Why a change of $\mu_R$ from $Q$ in $e^+e^-$?} \ABquestion{Mistake with the $C_{\mathrm{hc},3}^{(1)}$ in this eq? }
This accounts for initial-state radiation (ISR), as well as final-state radiation (FSR), where the latter is illustrated in Ch.~\ref{ch:resum}.
At first glance, we can outline some of the differences found between this equation and that for $e^+e^-$ annihilation in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:Sigma-NNLL-fs}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The luminosity enters through the $\prod_{\ell=1}^{n_i}\frac{q^{(\ell)}(x_\ell,v^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}}\mu_F)}{q^{(\ell)}(x_\ell,\mu_F)}$ term. Here $n_i$ are the number of incoming hadronic legs, each which are associated with a parton distribution $q^{(\ell)}(x_\ell,Q)$ at a Bjorken momentum fraction $x_\ell$. 
%\ABquestion{Comment made in draft about re-deriving this.} 

\item The coefficient $H^{(1)}$. Unlike the
  $e^+e^-$ case, there is no natural choice for the resummation scale
  $Q$. Therefore, for a generic process, $H^{(1)}$ will depend on $Q$,
  the Mandelstam invariants $s,t,u$ and, when ultraviolet divergences
  are present, the renomalisation scale $\mu_R$. 
  
\item A single unresolved hard collinear emission emitted from the
  final-state leg $p_3$ combines with the corresponding virtual
  corrections to give the coefficient $C_{\mathrm{hc},3}^{(1)}$, and a
  NNLL contribution. The explicit expression of $C_{\mathrm{hc},3}^{(1)}$ can be obtained from Eq.~\eqref{eq:C1hc-q}, and depend on the flavour of leg 3.

\end{enumerate}

We will discuss two of the contributions in this formula which we can take generally, namely $H^{(1)}$ and $R_{\mathrm{NNLL}}(v)$.

\subsection{The coefficient $\mathbf{H^{(1)}(Q)}$}
\label{sec:H1}

It is instructive to start from the calculation of $H^{(1)}(Q)$, despite
its contribution being NNLL, because this makes it possible to
introduce the main ingredients for all the calculations that will
follow, as well as set conventions. We will try to follow the widely used
notation of~\cite{Catani:1998bh}, slightly adapted to our needs.

The starting point to compute $H^{(1)}(Q)$ is the following representation of UV-renormalised virtual corrections~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq,Arpino:2019ozn}:
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:HQ}
  \mathcal{H}(Q;\varepsilon) = H(\alpha_s(\mu_R),Q) \exp\left\{\int^{Q^2}\!\!\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\sum_{\ell=1}^3 \gamma_\ell\left[\alpha_s(k^2;\varepsilon)\right]\right\}\times \\ \times \exp\left\{-\int \frac{d^dk}{(2\pi)^2}\sum_{\ell=1}^3 \sum_{\bar \ell\ne \ell} w_{\ell \bar \ell}(m^2,m^2+\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}^2;\varepsilon) \Theta\left(\frac 12\ln\frac{Q^2_{\ell\bar \ell}}{m^2+\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}^2}-|\eta^{(\ell\bar \ell)}| \right) \Theta(Q_{\ell \bar \ell}-\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell})\right\}\,,
\end{multline}
with $Q_{\ell\bar \ell}^2=2(p_\ell p_{\bar \ell})$.  Here dimensional
regularisation is used to regulate soft and collinear divergences,
with $d=4-2\varepsilon$ the number of space-time dimensions. Other
important quantities entering eq.~\eqref{eq:HQ} are:
\begin{itemize}
\item
  the ``hard'' function $H(\alpha_s,Q)$ which has the following expansion in powers of $\alpha_s(Q)$:
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:HQ-exp}
    H(\alpha_s) = 1+\frac{\alpha_2}{2\pi}H^{(1)}(Q) +\left(\frac{\alpha_2}{2\pi}\right)^2H^{(2)}(Q)+\dots 
  \end{equation}
\item the $d$-dimensional strong coupling $\alpha_s(k^2;\varepsilon)$, defined as the solution of the renormalisation-group equation
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:RG-coupling}
  k^2\frac{d\alpha_s}{dk^2} = - \varepsilon \alpha_s+\beta(\alpha_s)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\beta(\alpha_s)$ is the QCD beta function, and renormalisation
is performed in the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ scheme. The collinear anomalous dimension $\gamma_\ell(\alpha_s)$ has the expansion
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:gammal}
  \gamma_\ell (\alpha_s) = \gamma^{(0)}_\ell \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} + \gamma^{(1)}_\ell \left(\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \right)^2+\dots
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:gammal-flav}
  \gamma_\ell^{(0)} = \left\{
    \begin{split}
      & \frac{3}{2}C_F\,,\qquad\text{if $\ell$ is a quark,} \\
      & 2\pi \beta_0 \,,\qquad\text{if $\ell$ is a gluon;}
    \end{split}
    \right.
\end{equation}
;
\item the ``web'' function
  $w_{\ell \bar \ell}(m^2,m^2+\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}^2;\varepsilon)$
  which consistently resum the contribution of multiple soft real
  emissions from the dipole made up from the legs $\ell$ and
  $\bar \ell$. Physically, the web function is the weight associated
  with the emission of a gluon of mass $m$, inclusive with respect to
  all its splittings. Since Feynman rules are invariant under
  rescaling of the emitters' four-momenta, the web function can only
  depend on variables that are invariant under such rescalings. These
  are $m^2$ and \ABquestion{Maybe a better notation is $\kappa^{(\ell\bar \ell)}$}
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:kappa}
    \kappa_{\ell\bar\ell}^2 = 2 \frac{(p_\ell k)(k p_{\bar \ell})}{(p_\ell p_{\bar\ell})}\,. 
  \end{equation}
  The web function admits an expansion in
  powers of the strong coupling,
  $w_{\ell \bar \ell} = \alpha_s(\mu_R) w_{\ell \bar
    \ell}^{(1)}+\alpha_s^2(\mu_R) w_{\ell \bar \ell}^{(2)}+\dots$, and
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:w1}
    w_{\ell \bar \ell}^{(1)}(m^2,m^2+\kappa^2;\varepsilon) = (4\pi)^2\frac{C_{\ell\bar\ell}}{\kappa^2}\,\mu_R^{2\epsilon}\frac{e^{\varepsilon\gamma_E}}{(4\pi)^{\varepsilon}}\,\delta(m^2)\,,
  \end{equation}
  where $C_{\ell\bar \ell}=C_A/2$ if either leg is a gluon and $C_{\ell\bar \ell}=C_F-C_A/2$ otherwise. Note also that
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:Cl}
    \sum_{\bar \ell \ne \ell} C_{\ell \bar \ell}= C_\ell\,,
  \end{equation}
  where $C_\ell$ is the colour factor of leg $\ell$, $C_F$ for a quark and $C_A$ for a gluon;
\item the $d$-dimensional phase space, given by
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:dk}
%    \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d} = \frac{d\eta^{(\ell\bar \ell)}}{2\pi}\, dm^2 \,\frac{d\kappa^2_{\ell\bar \ell}}{(4\pi)^2}\left(\frac{4\pi}{\kappa^2_{\ell\bar\ell}}\right)^\varepsilon\frac{d\phi\,(\sin^2\phi)^{-\varepsilon}}{\sqrt \pi\,\Gamma(1/2-\varepsilon)}\,\Theta(\sin\phi)\,.
    \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d} = \frac{d\eta^{(\ell\bar \ell)}}{2\pi}\, dm^2 \,\frac{d\kappa^2_{\ell\bar \ell}}{(4\pi)^2}\left(\frac{\pi}{\kappa^2_{\ell\bar\ell}}\right)^\varepsilon\frac{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}{\Gamma(1-2\varepsilon)}\frac{d\phi}{\pi}\,(\sin^2\phi)^{-\varepsilon}\,\Theta(\sin\phi)\,. 
  \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
With everything in place, we obtain $H^{(1)}$ by expanding both sides of eq.~\eqref{eq:HQ} and comparing the result. In particular:
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:w-exp}
  -\alpha_s(\mu_R)  \int \frac{d^dk}{(2\pi)^d} w^{(1)}_{\ell \bar \ell}(m^2,m^2+\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}^2;\varepsilon) \Theta\left(\frac 12\ln\frac{Q^2_{\ell\bar \ell}}{m^2+\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}^2}-|\eta^{(\ell\bar \ell)}| \right) \Theta(Q_{\ell \bar\ell}-\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}) \\ = -\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi}
  \left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{Q^2_{\ell\bar \ell}}\right)^\varepsilon \frac{e^{\varepsilon\gamma_E}}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)} \frac{C_{\ell\bar \ell}}{\varepsilon^2}\,,
\end{multline}
and
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:gamma0-int}
  \int_0^{Q^2}\!\!\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\gamma_\ell^{(0)}\frac{\alpha_s(k^2;\varepsilon)}{2\pi} = \frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi}\gamma_\ell^{(0)} \int_0^{Q^2}\!\!\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} = -\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{Q^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \gamma_\ell^{(0)}\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi}\,.
\end{equation}
Now, using the fact that $\mathcal{H}(Q;\varepsilon)$ has the universal structure~\cite{Catani:1998bh}\footnote{Note that the general formula of~\cite{Catani:1998bh} includes also phase factors (Coulomb phases) that arise when the two legs $\ell$ and $\bar \ell$ are both incoming or outgoing. In the case of three emitters, these phase factors give rise to a finite contribution, included in $\mathcal{H}_1$.}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:HQ-expanded}
  \mathcal{H}(Q;\varepsilon) = 1+\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi}\left[-\frac{e^{\varepsilon\gamma_E}}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}\sum_\ell\left(\frac{1}{\varepsilon^2}+\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\frac{\gamma_\ell^{(0)}}{C_\ell}\right)\sum_{\bar \ell\ne \ell} C_{\ell\bar\ell}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{Q_{\ell\bar \ell}^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}+\mathcal{H}_1\right]\,,
\end{equation}
and comparing the above result with the right-hand side of eq.~(\ref{eq:HQ})
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:HQ-rep-expanded}
  \mathcal{H}(Q;\varepsilon) = 1+\frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi}\left\{-\frac{e^{\varepsilon\gamma_E}}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}\sum_\ell\left[\sum_{\bar \ell\ne \ell}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{Q^2_{\ell\bar\ell}}\right)^{\varepsilon}\frac{C_{\ell\bar \ell}}{\varepsilon^2}+\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{Q^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}\frac{\gamma_\ell^{(0)}}{\varepsilon}\right]+H_1\right\}\,,
\end{equation}
we find
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:H1-final}
  H^{(1)}(Q) = \mathcal{H}_1+ \sum_\ell\frac{\gamma_\ell^{(0)}}{C_\ell}\sum_{\bar \ell\ne \ell} C_{\ell\bar \ell}\ln\left(\frac{Q^2_{\ell\bar\ell}}{Q^2}\right)
    \,.
\end{equation}

The radiator $R_{\mathrm{NNLL}}(v)$ has both NLL and NNLL contributions.  It is obtained starting from the following sum over legs

``unresolved'' soft emission. The latter combine with the web

\begin{multline}

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dll}
  d_{\ell\bar\ell} = d_\ell \left(\frac{Q_{\ell\bar \ell}}{Q}\right)^a \left(\frac{Q_{\ell\bar \ell}}{2 E_\ell}\right)^{b_\ell}\,.
\end{equation}
We can account for the $\phi$ and $m^2$ dependence of the observable at NNLL accuracy, by reorganising $\mathcal{R}_{\ell\bar\ell}(v)$ as follows
\begin{equation}

  \mathcal{R}_{\ell\bar\ell}(v) = \int\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\, R_{\ell\bar \ell}\left(\frac{v}{d_{\ell\bar \ell} \,g_\ell(\phi)},Q_{\ell\bar \ell}\right)+\delta R_{\ell\bar \ell}(v)\,,

\begin{multline}

  \delta R_{\ell\bar\ell}(v) = -\int\frac{d^4

        \ell}}{m^2+\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}^2}

        \ell}}{\kappa_{\ell\bar \ell}}-\eta^{(\ell\bar \ell)}  \right)\,,
\end{multline}
and $R_{\ell\bar \ell}(v,Q_{\ell\bar \ell})$ by \ABquestion{check factors of two}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Rll-final}
  R_{\ell\bar \ell}(v,Q_{\ell\bar \ell})\equiv \int_0^{Q^2_{\ell\bar \ell}} \frac{d\kappa^2}{\kappa^2} \frac{\alpha_s(\kappa)}{\pi} \int_0^\infty\!\! d\eta\, \Theta\!\left(\ln\frac{Q_{\ell\bar\ell}}{\kappa}-\eta\right)\Theta\left(
      d_{\ell\bar \ell} \left(\frac{\kappa}{Q_{\ell\bar \ell}}\right)^a e^{-b_\ell\eta}-v
    \right) \,.
\end{equation}

With this notation, the azimuthal integral over $R_{\ell\bar \ell}$, up to NNLL accuracy, can be expanded as follows
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:Rll-expanded}
\int\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\, R_{\ell\bar \ell}\left(\frac{v}{d_{\ell\bar \ell} \,g_\ell(\phi)},Q_{\ell\bar \ell}\right)\simeq  
R_{\ell\bar \ell}\left(v,Q_{\ell\bar \ell}\right)\\+\langle \ln(d_{\ell\bar \ell}\,g_\ell)\rangle R'_{\ell\bar \ell}\left(v,Q_{\ell\bar \ell}\right)+\frac 12
\langle \ln^2(d_{\ell\bar \ell}\,g_\ell)\rangle R''_{\ell\bar \ell}\left(v,Q_{\ell\bar \ell}\right)\,,
\end{multline}
where, for any function $f(\phi)$ of the azimuth:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:azimuthal-average}
  \langle f \rangle\equiv \int\frac{d\phi}{2\pi} f(\phi)\,,
\end{equation}
and $R'_{\ell\bar\ell}\equiv -v dR_{\ell\bar\ell}/dv$, $R''_{\ell\bar\ell}\equiv -v dR'_{\ell\bar\ell}/dv$.

\label{sec:NNLL-initial}

missing. There exist results for $N$-jettiness both in deep-inelastic
scattering and hadron collisions \ABquestion{References missing}, as well as

We restrict ourselves to event-shape variables and, as is customary in
the ARES approach, we identify the relevant emissions that give rise
to a specific NNLL contribution. In this case, this is one hard and
collinear emission $k$, accompanied by an arbitrary number of soft and
collinear emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$. Emission $k$ carries a fraction
$z^{(\ell)}=(1-z)/z$ of the momentum $p_\ell$ entering the hard
scattering, more precisely
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:k||l}
  k = \frac{1-z}{z} p_\ell + k_t^{(\ell)}+ \mathcal{O}\left(\left(k_t^{(\ell)}\right)^2\right)\,.
\end{equation}
In order to extend ARES to initial-state radiation, we follow the same principles as for final-state radiation, namely:
\begin{itemize}
\item we consider one collinear emission $k$, and the corresponding virtual
  corrections, accompanied by an arbitrary
  number of soft and collinear emissions $\{k_i\}$;
\item we subtract the double counting occurring when $k$ is also soft.
\end{itemize}
We use dimensional regularisation to make each contribution
individually finite. In the case of initial-state radiation, virtual
corrections include the counterterm arising from the renormalisation
of PDFs. We need to distinguish two cases, according to whether the parton entering the hard scattering is a quark or a gluon.

\paragraph{Splitting $\mathbf{q\to qg}$.} We first consider the case of an
incoming quark carrying a fraction $x/z$ of the proton momentum. This
emits a collinear gluon carrying a fraction $1-z$ of its emitter's
momentum. After the emission, the quark enters the hard scattering
with a fraction $x$ of the proton momentum. The above procedure gives
a function that we call $\mathcal{F}_{qq}$. This function will
be reorganised to give various NNLL functions. Our starting point is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FISR-start}
  \begin{split}
    \mathcal{F}_{qq} & = \int \dZ \left\{\int_0^{Q^2}\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\times\right. \\& \times \left[\int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z}C_F \left[\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}-\varepsilon(1-z)\right]\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\}}{v}\right) \frac{f_q(x/z,\mu_F)}{f_q(x,\mu_F)}+\right.\\
   &\left. - \int_0^1 dz \frac{2C_F}{1-z}\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\right] +\\
   &+ \left[-\gamma_q^{(0)}\int_0^{Q^2v^{2/(a+b_\ell)}}\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}\frac{\alpha_s(k)}{2\pi}
   +\frac{1}{\varepsilon} \left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{\mu_F^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi} \int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z} C_F\left(\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}\right)_+ \frac{f_q(x/z,\mu_F)}{f_q(x,\mu_F)}\right]  \\ & \times \left. \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\right\} \,.
\end{split}
\end{equation}
The second line of this equation contains the splitting function for a
quark emitting a collinear gluon $k$ in $4-2\varepsilon$ dimensions,
minus the contribution when the gluon is soft, already accounted for
in $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$. The third line contains virtual
correction, as well as the collinear counterterm needed to renormalise
the parton distribution function. The parameter $b_\ell$ corresponds
to the leg identified by the quark entering the hard process.  The
expression in eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-start} contains single logarithms of
the form $\alpha_s\ln[Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}/\mu_R]$ and
$\alpha_s\ln[Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}/\mu_F]$. This logarithms can be
resummed by fixing $\mu_R=\mu_F=Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}$. Also, we can set
$\alpha_s(k)=\alpha_s(k_t)=\alpha_s(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})$ and obtain a
finite expression, up to subleading terms of order $\alpha_s^2$.
We note that, eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-start} can be written in a more compact way by introducing the conventional splitting functions:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Pqq}
  \hat P_{qq}(z)  = C_F \frac{1+z^2}{1-z}\,, \qquad \hat P_{qq}^{\epsilon}(z)=-(1-z)\,, \qquad P_{qq}(z) = \left(\hat P_{qq}(z)\right)_+\,.
\end{equation}

This gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FISR-Pqq}
  \begin{split}
    \mathcal{F}_{qq} & = \int \dZ \left\{\int_0^{Q^2}\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}\times\right. \\& \times \left[\int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z}\left[\hat P_{qq}(z)+\varepsilon \hat P_{qq}(z)\right]\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\}}{v}\right) \frac{f_q(x/z,\mu_F)}{f_q(x,\mu_F)}+\right.\\
    &\left. - \int_0^1 dz \frac{2C_F}{1-z}\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\right]+ \\
   &+ \left[-\gamma_q^{(0)}\int_0^{Q^2v^{2/(a+b_\ell)}}\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}\frac{\alpha_s(k)}{2\pi}
     +\frac{1}{\varepsilon} \left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{\mu_F^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(\mu_R)}{2\pi} \int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z} P_{qq}(z) \frac{f_q(x/z,\mu_F)}{f_q(x,\mu_F)}\right]  \\ & \times \left. \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\right\} \,.
\end{split}
\end{equation}


In order to facilitate the calculation of
$\mathcal{F}_{qq}$ in four dimensions, we add and subtract
a counterterm, $\mathrm{CT}$, which has the following form:
\begin{multline}
    \mathrm{CT}=\int \dZ \int\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\times \\
    \times C_F\left[\int_x^1\frac{dz}{z}\left(\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}-\varepsilon(1-z)\right)\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{f_q(x,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}-\int_0^1dz\frac{2}{1-z}\right]\times \\ \times \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)}{v}\right)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\}}{v}\right)\,.
  \end{multline}
  Thanks to the introduction of this counterterm, we can recast
$\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{ISR}}$ in the form of
eq.~(\ref{eq:Sigma-NNLL-fs}):
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FISR-split}
  \mathcal{F}_{qq} = \frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}\left( \delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}\right)+ \frac{\alpha_s(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{2\pi} C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}} \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\,.
\end{equation}
The constant terms $C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}$ plays the same role as an integrated counterterm in fixed-order calculations, whereas the sum $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}+\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}$ represents the subtracted contribution of real radiation. We now proceed to the detailed derivation of eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-split}.

First, we calculate $C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc, qq}}$. This quantity is obtained by adding $\mathrm{CT}$ to the last two lines of eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-start}. This gives:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{\alpha_s(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{2\pi}  C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}} \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\lambda)= \int \dZ \Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\})}{v}\right) \times \\ & \times \left\{ -\gamma_q^{(0)}\int_0^{Q^2v^{2/(a+b_\ell)}}\frac{dk^2}{k^2}\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}\frac{\alpha_s(k)}{2\pi}+\right.\\
&+\frac{1}{\varepsilon} \frac{\alpha_s(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{2\pi} \int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z} C_F\left(\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}\right)_+ \frac{f_q(x/z,Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{f_q(x,Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}  \\

   &\left.\left.-\int_0^1dz\frac{2}{1-z}\right]\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)}{v}\right)\right\}\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
By comparing the first term on the right-hand-side of the above
equation~(\ref{eq:FNLL-formula}), we recognise $\FNLL(\lambda)$ to be
the first line. Using the parametrisation of
$V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)$ in terms of the splitting variables
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Vsc-param}
V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)=d_\ell g_\ell(\phi)\left(\frac{k_t}{Q}\right)^{a+b_\ell}\frac{1}{(1-z)^{b_\ell}}\,,
\end{equation}
along with $\gamma_q^{(0)}=-3/2 C_F$, we find 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc, qq}}= \int_x^1\frac{dz}{z}
C_F\left[ \left(\frac{2 }{a+b_\ell}\left(b_\ell\left(\frac{\ln(1-z)}{1-z}\right)_+-\frac{\ln d_\ell g_\ell(\phi)}{(1-z)_+}\right)\right)(1+z^2)+(1-z)\right]\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{f_q(x,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Referring back to Eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-split}, we now evaluate the remaining contribution 
$\frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}\left(\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}+\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}\right)$. 
In analogy with the extraction of $C^{(1)}_{\mathrm{hc},qq}$, this term is obtained by subtracting the counterterm $\mathrm{CT}$ from the first two lines of Eq.~\eqref{eq:FISR-start}, yielding
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dFISR+CT}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}&\left(\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}+\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}\right)=\int \dZ \left\{\int_0^{Q^2}\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}
\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon} 
\frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\times\right. \\
&\times \left[\int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z}C_F 
\left[\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}-\varepsilon(1-z)\right]
\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)
\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{f_q(x,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}\right. \\
&\left.- \int_0^1 dz \frac{2C_F}{1-z}
\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right] \\
&- C_F \int\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}
\left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{k_t^2}\right)^{\varepsilon}
\frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\left[\int_x^1\frac{dz}{z}
\left(\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}-\varepsilon(1-z)\right)
\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}{f_q(x,Qv^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}})}
-\int_0^1dz\frac{2}{1-z}\right]\times \\
&\times\left.
\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)}{v}\right)
\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\})}{v}\right)
\right\}\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
To simplify the above expression, we introduce the rescaled variable $
\zeta \equiv V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)/v$, and change variables using the parametrisation of
$V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:Vsc-param}. At NNLL, this allows the replacement
\begin{equation}
	\frac{dk_t^2}{k_t^2}\frac{\alpha_s(k_t)}{2\pi}\simeq\frac{\alpha_s\left(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\pi\left(a+b_\ell\right)}\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\,.
\end{equation}
Using this substitution and taking the limit $\varepsilon \to 0$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFISR+CT} simplifies to
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:dFrec+dFhq}
\frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}&\left(\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}+\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}\right)=\int \dZ \frac{\alpha_s\left(Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\pi\left(a+b_\ell\right)}\left\{\int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\times\right. \\
&\times \left(\int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z} C_F \frac{1+z^2}{1-z}\,\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{f_q(x,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}
\left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)+\right.\right.\\
&\left.\left.-\Theta(1-\zeta)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right]
\right. \\
&\left.\left.-\int_0^1 dz \frac{2C_F}{1-z}\left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right) -\Theta(1-\zeta)\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right]\right)\right\}\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
We observe that the second and third line give two contributions that
are separately finite in the collinear limit $\zeta\to 0$. However, in
order to take the limit
$z\to 1$ and obtain a finite results, one needs to compute the expression in
eq.~\eqref{eq:dFrec+dFhq} as a whole. A further simplication can be achieved by adding and subtracting the following term
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dFhc-sub}
  \begin{aligned}
 \int \dZ &\frac{\alpha_s\left(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\pi\left(a+b_\ell\right)}\\
  &\int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}
 \int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z} C_F \frac{1+z^2}{1-z} \frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{f_q(x,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\,.
 \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This gives two contributions, namely $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}$ and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dFrec}
\begin{aligned}
\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec,qq}}& =\int \dZ \frac{\alpha_s\left(Q v^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\alpha_s(Q)\left(a+b_\ell\right)}\left\{\int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z}\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{f_q(x,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}\times\right. \\
&\times  C_F \frac{1+z^2}{1-z}
\left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{hc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)-\Theta\left(1-\frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k,\{k_i\})}{v}\right)\right]\,,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dFhc}
\begin{aligned}
\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc,qq}}& = \frac{\alpha_s\left(v^{1/(a+b_\ell)}\right)}{\alpha_s(Q)\left(a+b_\ell\right)}\left(\int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z}\frac{f_q(x/z,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})}{f_q(x,Qv^{1/(a+b_\ell)})} C_F \frac{1+z^2}{(1-z)_{\!+}}\right) \times \\

\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\section{NNLL Resummation for Selected Observables}
In this section, we wish to calculate the terms which make up Eq.~\ref{eq:Sigma-NNLL-fs}, for the each observable. We divide these into sections, depending on what limit the observables must make use of in the definition of the correction. We will make a clear note of when we are considering initial- or final-state radiation - particularly regarding the $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec}}$ and $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{rec}}$ terms.

\subsection{Soft and collinear contributions}
\label{sec:sc-contributions}
In this section we collect all contributions arising from soft and
collinear emissions, as stated in the title. These are
$\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{sc}}$ and
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc}}$, for which all gluons are widely
separated in angle, and $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{correl}}$ where one
soft and collinear gluon splits into $q\bar{q}$ or a gluon pair. We
will compute these contributions analytically whenever
possible. Otherwise, they will be computed numerically using the ARES
Monte Carlo procedure~\cite{Banfi:2014sua}. Note that all these
functions require to compute the quantity
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vsc}
  \frac{\Vsc{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{\Vfull{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{NLL resummation.}
Soft and collinear emissions widely separated in angle give the only NLL function embodying the effect of resolved emission. This is the well-known function $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\lambda)$, which can be computed using the general formula shown in Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-formula}. We now proceed to the calculation of $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\lambda)$ for the three considered observables. 

\begin{itemize}
\item {\textbf{One-jettiness.}} One-jettiness is additive meaning that
  $\FNLL(\lambda)$ has the general expression for all additive event
  shapes~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq: FNLL-tau_1}
  \FNLL(\lambda)=\frac{e^{-\gamma_E \RpNLL}}{\Gamma(1+\RpNLL)}\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{NNLL resummation.} The soft and collinear corrections which
contribute to the NNLL resummation are, in order of complexity,
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc}}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{sc}}$, and
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{correl}}$. On top of the soft-collinear
emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ that are needed for any resummation, the two
functions $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc}}$, $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{sc}}$
involve the presence of an additional soft and collinear emission $k$,
parametrised in terms of the usual variable $\zeta=\Vsc{k}/v$, as well
as its emitter's leg $\ell$, and azimuthal angle $\phi^{(\ell)}$. For
$\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{correl}}$, this additional emission is
required to split into $k_a$ and $k_b$, with further dimensionsless
variables parametrising the splitting (see later). We now compute each
of these functions in turn, for the three considered observables. 

The general expression for $\delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc}}$ is given by
 \begin{multline}
   \label{eq:dFhc-general}
   \delta \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{hc}}(\lambda) \equiv \int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\sum_\ell \frac{\alpha_s\left(v^{\frac{1}{a+b_\ell}}Q\right)}{(a+b_\ell)\alpha_s(Q)}\gamma_\ell^{(0)} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi^{(\ell)}}{2\pi} \int\! \dZ \,\times \\ \times \left[\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{k,\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)
       -\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\Theta(1-\zeta)
     \right] \,.
 \end{multline}
Note that this is slightly different from that shown for $e^+e^-$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFhc-general}, as we have simply performed the $z^{(\ell)}$-integration to go from $\langle P_{f_\ell}(z^{(\ell)},\epsilon)\rangle$ and produce the $\gamma^{(0)}_\ell$ coefficient.
