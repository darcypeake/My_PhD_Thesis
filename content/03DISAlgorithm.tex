\chapter{A generalised-$k_t$ jet algorithm for Deep Inelastic Scattering}
\label{chapter:DIS}
\section{Introduction}

Lepton-hadron colliders such as the Hadron Elektron
Ringanlage (HERA) and the forthcoming Electron--Ion Collider (EIC) are
unique laboratories for the clean investigation of the inner structure
of the nucleons and the nuclei that they form.
%
Although the HERA collider has not been operational since 2007, access
to previously recorded HERA data has enabled recent re-analysis taking
advantage of modern jet algorithms and event-shape observables in Deep
Inelastic Scattering (DIS)~\cite{ZEUS:2023zie,H1:2023fzk,ZEUS:2024mhu,H1:2024nde,H1:2024aze,H1:2024pvu}.
%
In the theory community this has spurred a renewed interest in
formulating jet
algorithms~\cite{Arratia:2020ssx,vanBeekveld:2023chs,Caucal:2024vbv},
and in resummed calculations of event shapes for
DIS~\cite{Makris:2021drz,Knobbe:2023ehi,Fang:2024auf,Cao:2024ota,Ee:2025scz,vanBeekveld:2025zjh}, the latter 
building upon earlier work (see e.g.\ Refs.~\cite{Antonelli:1999kx, Dasgupta:2001eq, Dasgupta:2003iq, Kang:2013nha, Kang:2013lga, Chu:2022jgs}).
%
Such modern QCD tools will be instrumental for the EIC,\footnote{And the LHeC~\cite{LHeC:2020van} should it be approved.} currently under construction at
Brookhaven National Laboratory, whose operation is scheduled to start
in the early 2030s.  The EIC will provide new opportunities to study
the structure of QCD, including the spin structure of the proton, the
dynamics of confinement, and gluon
saturation~\cite{Accardi:2012qut,AbdulKhalek:2021gbh}.
%
%The aim of the present paper is to introduce a family of DIS jet algorithms that are 1. simple to use; 2. allow for a  
Since jet production is the dominant process at hadron-lepton
colliders, jet reconstruction will play an essential part in many
(re)-analysis for both the EIC and HERA. \\ \\
Most developments in jet algorithms over the past decades have been
driven by hadron collider applications, in particular at the LHC,
where the anti-$k_t$ algorithm~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp} has become
the standard choice. Jet algorithms for DIS were developed around the
same time as those for hadronic collisions, but unlike for hadron
collisions, no single algorithm dominates the landscape.
%
The exclusive (spherically-invariant) $k_t$ algorithm was introduced
in Ref.~\cite{Catani:1992zp} extending the Durham algorithm for
$e^+e^-$ collisions~\cite{Catani:1991hj,Brown:1991hx} to DIS by
treating the proton remnant as a particle of infinite momentum. An
angular-ordered version of this algorithm was later proposed in
Ref.~\cite{Wobisch:1998wt}. It modifies the $e^+e^-$ Cambridge
algorithm~\cite{Dokshitzer:1997in} to DIS again by treating the proton
remnant as a particle of infinite momentum. A longitudinally invariant
(LI) $k_t$ algorithm was introduced in
Ref.~\cite{Ellis:1993tq,Catani:1993hr}, and was extended to an
angular-ordered version (the Aachen algorithm) in
Ref.~\cite{Wobisch:1998wt}. The latter two algorithms are identical to
the hadron-collider versions, except that the clustering takes place
in the Breit frame as is the case for all the DIS jet algorithms
listed here (cf.\ Sec.~\ref{sec:Kinematics} for details on the Breit
frame). More recently, the Centauro algorithm~\cite{Arratia:2020ssx}
was proposed, combining features of longitudinally-invariant $k_t$
algorithms with those of spherically-invariant algorithms. Likewise a
set of jet definitions suitable for small-\xB DIS were proposed in
Ref.~\cite{Caucal:2024vbv}. 
%
Most of the historical algorithms, including a modified version the
JADE algorithm~\cite{JADE:1986kta,JADE:1988xlj}, have been studied
extensively at HERA and Fermilab's E665
experiment~\cite{E665:1992xqj,E665:1993vlk,H1:1994lps,ZEUS:1994jfw,ZEUS:1995tgg,H1:1995tux,H1:1998bvm,H1:1998rpm,H1:1998cuj,H1:2000bqr,H1:2002qhb,ZEUS:2002nms,ZEUS:2005iex,ZEUS:2006xvn,H1:2007xjj,H1:2009pqp,H1:2010mgp,H1:2014cbm},
highlighting the importance of jet finding algorithms in DIS. The
modern Centauro algorithm has also recently been used by the H1
collaboration to perform the first determination of groomed event
shapes~\cite{H1:2024pvu}. \\ \\
Unlike at the LHC, where one of the main requirements of a good jet
algorithm is the insensitivity to pile-up and underlying events, in
DIS, where these effects are absent, a good jet algorithm is one that
preserves a meaningful clustering sequence and allows soft radiation
to influence jet boundaries while respecting the natural hemisphere
structure of the Breit frame. For this reason it is natural to
consider $k_t$ and Cambridge/Aachen~(C/A) type algorithms in DIS, rather
than anti-$k_t$. A C/A-based DIS algorithm designed for
the study of Lund variables~\cite{Dreyer:2018nbf,vanBeekveld:2025zjh}
and parton shower logarithmic accuracy was introduced in
Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs}, and forms the starting point for a
spherically-invariant generalised-$k_t$ algorithm adapted to DIS,
which we present and study in more detail in the present paper.  The formulation
of the jet algorithm itself follows the structure of the generalised
$k_t$ algorithm used in $e^+e^-$ collisions as implemented and
introduced in \FASTJET~\cite{Cacciari:2011ma}.  It is formulated in
the Breit frame and does not have an anti-$k_t$ variant. We also
discuss a prescription to select the jet that is most likely to
contain the struck quark based on the lightcone fraction that the jet
is carrying. This allows for a clean separation between \emph{target}
and \emph{current} hemisphere jets. Finally, we study the robustness
of the new jet algorithm against hadronisation corrections. \\ \\
The structure of this chapter is as follows: in Section~\ref{sec:ourDISalgo} we present our new
family of inclusive, spherically-invariant generalised-$k_t$ jet
algorithms.
%
In Section~\ref{sec:pheno} we examine the behaviour of the new
algorithms and we provide a comparison with the Centauro one.
%
Finally, we present our conclusions in Section~\ref{sec:concl}.


\section{A new generalised-$k_t$ algorithm for DIS}
\label{sec:ourDISalgo}
\subsection{The algorithm}
We now introduce the inclusive spherically-invariant generalised-$k_t$ algorithm for DIS, implemented as  a \textsc{Fastjet} plugin in \texttt{fjcontrib}.
%
The algorithm shares the essential structure of its $e^+e^-$ counterpart, with the key modification that an infinite-energy parton aligned along the proton direction is included in the cluster sequence.
%
Because of this modification, only values $p\geq 0$ are physical, and no anti-$k_t$ variant exists. 
%
The angular-ordered variant of the algorithm ($p=0$) was already presented by some of us in Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs}, and used to define Lund Tree Event shapes with remarkably simple all-orders structure~\cite{vanBeekveld:2025zjh}. \\ \\
We define the distances
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
	d_{ij}& = \min(E_{i}^{2p},E_{j}^{2p}) (1-\cos\theta_{ij}) \,, \\
	d_{iB} &= E_{i}^{2p}(1-\cos\theta_{iB})\,,
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
with $p \geq 0$,
% 
as well as dimensionful resolution variable for clusterings between two final-state pseudo-jets
\begin{align}
	v_{ij}& = \min(E_{i}^{2},E_{j}^{2}) (1-\cos\theta_{ij}).
\end{align}
The clustering proceeds as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Introduce a dimensionful parameter $v_{\mathrm{cut}}^2>0$, which can be of the order of $Q^2$, or even infinite.%
  \footnote{Technically one needs to set the \texttt{v2cut} parameter in the \texttt{FastJet} plugin to -1 when one aims to set $v_{\\mathrm{cut}} = \infty$.}
  \item Find the smallest $d_{ij}$ or $d_{iB}$.
  \item If the smallest is a $d_{iB}$, call $i$ a jet, and remove it from the list of pseudojets.
  \item If the smallest is a $d_{ij}$, if $v_{ij}<v_{\mathrm{cut}}^2$, merge $i$ and $j$, otherwise call the softest pseudojet a jet, and remove it from the list of pseudojets.
  \item Continue until there are no particles in the list. 
\end{enumerate}
As in the standard inclusive (LI)
algorithms for $pp$ collisions, some reconstructed jets may originate
entirely from initial-state radiation or from soft large-angle emissions. 
In $pp$ collisions, such
jets are removed by imposing a transverse-momentum (and a rapidity)
cut.
%
This however cannot be applied to DIS, as the jet originating from the
fragmentation of the struck quark does not necessarily carry a large
transverse momentum (in fact, in the case where no additional
radiation is produced, this jet will carry zero transverse momentum).
%
We therefore have to define a different procedure for how to identify 
the jet that is most likely associated with the struck quark.
%
In Sec.~\ref{sec:macrojet} we describe a procedure for how to identify
this jet, that we call the macrojet, that can be applied to our
generalised $k_t$ algorithm and the Centauro algorithm.
%
The variable $v_{\mathrm{cut}}$ introduced in the first step of the clustering algorithm
has a role similar to introducing a jet radius $R$ (as is done in e.g.\ Sec.~\ref{sec:gen-kt}),
and controls how much radiation gets clustered with a final-state jet. 
%
A smaller value for $v_{\mathrm{cut}}$ prevents clustering between two final-state pseudojets, 
and thereby leads to a larger number of final-state jets. \\ \\
Before moving to Sec.~\ref{sec:macrojet}, let us first briefly comment
on the main differences between this jet algorithm and the
pre-existing jet algorithms.
%
Compared to the Cambridge algorithm for DIS (Sec.~\ref{sec:camdis}),
which shares most similarities with our algorithm, the present
algorithm does not 
include a second stage in
which beam jets are removed and the remaining jets are reclustered
with the $e^+e^-$ Cambridge algorithm. 
As a consequence our algorithm
is significantly simpler. \\ \\
Compared to the algorithm of Section~\ref{sec:gen-kt}, the present
algorithm does not contain a $1-\cos R$ in the inter-pseudojet
distance $d_{ij}$.
%
Including such a factor would introduce a fixed jet radius $R$,
limiting the angular reach of each jet and allowing final-state
radiation at angles larger than $R$ to be left unclustered or absorbed
into a neighbouring jet.
%
In the Breit frame, where the natural geometry is spherical rather
than cylindrical, this is undesirable: a radius parameter breaks this
spherical symmetry.
%
By omitting it, our algorithm ensures that every final-state particle
is either clustered into a jet or assigned to the beam, with no
radiation falling between jets due to an (arbitrary) angular cutoff.
%
This makes the algorithm better suited to capturing the full structure
of the hadronic final state in the Breit frame, and provides a cleaner
correspondence with the $e^+e^-$ algorithm.
%
As explained before, the size of the resulting jets in the present jet
algorithm may instead be controlled by the $v_{\mathrm{cut}}$ parameter. \\ \\
Differences between Centauro (Sec.~\ref{sec:centauro}) and our new jet
algorithm will be explored explicitly in Sec.~\ref{sec:pheno}.

\subsection{Identifying the macrojet}
\label{sec:macrojet}
\begin{figure}[t!]
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,page=7]{figs_new/paper_rivet_cmp_algos.pdf}
\caption{Normalised Breit-frame energy distribution of the final-state macrojet for neutral-current DIS, $\sqrt{s}$=300~GeV using the Centauro~(left) jet algorithm, as well as our variant of the C/A~(center) and $k_t$ jet algorithm~(right).
%
The final-state macrojet is identified as the one which maximises the light-cone component opposite in direction to the proton ($z_{\mathrm{jet}}$, solid line) or the Breit-frame pseudo-rapidity ($\eta_{\mathrm{jet}}$, dash-dotted lines).
%
Events are generated using the \PYTHIAn~\cite{Bierlich:2022pfr} default shower, with dipole recoil option~\cite{Cabouat:2017rzi}.
See Tab.~\ref{tab:input-params} for the precise settings.
 }
 \label{fig:jetEIR}
\end{figure}
This section describes how we identify the \emph{final-state
macrojet}, defined as the jet that is most likely to contain the 
fragments of the struck quark.
%
Our prescription is to select the jet with that carries the largest
light-cone component $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ along the direction of 
the original struck quark.
%
To make this precise, we perform a Sudakov decomposition of each
jet momentum (see also Sec.~\ref{sec:Kinematics} and Ref.~\cite{Makris:2021drz})
\begin{equation}
  p_{\mathrm{jet}}^\mu = \frac{|k_\perp^2|}{z_{\mathrm{jet}} x_{\mathrm{B}} Q^2} P^\mu + z_{\mathrm{jet}} \, p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu + k_\perp^\mu,
\end{equation}
where $P^\mu$ is the proton momentum, $p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu = q^\mu -
x_{\mathrm{B}} P^{\mu}$ can be identified as the original struck-quark
momentum, and $k_\perp^\mu$ is a space-light vector orthogonal to
$P^\mu$ and $p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu$.
%
Maximising $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ is then equivalent to selecting the jet whose
momentum $p_{\mathrm{jet}}^{\mu}$ has the largest dot product with
$P^\mu$. As discussed in Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs}, this
prescription is infrared safe.
%
This prescription is in principle independent of the jet clustering
algorithm employed, although the exclusive jet algorithms have a
potential to throw away the jet that is most likely to contain the
struck quark. \\ \\
A natural alternative is to identify the macrojet as the jet with the
most negative rapidity.
%
However, this prescription is not infrared safe, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:jetEIR}.
%
There, one can observe that both for the new family
of jet algorithms introduced here and for the Centauro jet algorithm, identifying the
final-state macrojet by rapidity (dash-dotted lines) leads to a soft tail. 
%
This tail is produced by wrongfully identifying soft emissions, generated either from radiation off the
DIS hard-scattering process or from the beam remnant, as the final-state macrojet. 
%
We note that for the $k_t$-algorithm the
soft tail is partially suppressed even when selecting the macro jet with the
most negative rapidity, but there is no exponential decrease in the tail.
%
Maximising the light-cone component in the direction of the 
original struck quark (solid curves) exponentially suppresses the tail for all
algorithms. \\ \\
Beyond macrojet identification, the properties of the macrojet itself provide a useful way
to characterise the behaviour of the algorithm.
%
In particular, since clustering in the Breit frame can lead to contamination 
between the current and target hemispheres, the macrojet offers a 
direct handle on how well the algorithm separates these regions 
and how faithfully it reconstructs the struck-quark jet. 
%
We will explore this further in Section~\ref{sec:pheno}.
%
Note that, similarly to the exclusive algorithms in Secs.~\ref{sec:exclktalgo} and
\ref{sec:camdis}, one can choose to recluster the final-state macrojet with
a dimensionful $v_{\mathrm{cut}}'$ parameter, smaller than the original
$v_{\mathrm{cut}}$ used in the first clustering procedure (which can also
be set to $\infty$), to resolve jet substructure.
%
This will not be explored further in this paper.

To better understand how the new jet algorithms work
and how they compare to the Centauro jet algorithm, 
we show in the top panel of Figs.~\ref{fig:events} and \ref{fig:events2} the
$(\theta,\phi)$ distribution of particles from two DIS events obtained
after showering.
%
The location of the central point in each disk corresponds to the
particle's position in the $\theta$ and $\phi$ plane, while the size
of the disk illustrates the particle's energy, with
larger radii corresponding to higher relative energies.
%
Particles in the remnant hemisphere lie at $\theta<\pi/2$ (i.e.\ the
left-hand side of the plot), while particles in the current hemisphere
lie at $\theta>\pi/2$ (i.e.\ the right-hand side of the plot).
%
Particles entering the same jet share the same colour.
%
Those forming the final-state macrojet are coloured in red and are encircled
by a thick black line.
%
For each figure, the bottom row represents the same event, but placed in a sea
of 1000 additional ultra-soft particles. 
%
We generate these ultra-soft particles with a fixed energy and with a uniform 
distribution in $\theta$ and $\phi$. 
%
We stress here that the exact location of the jet boundaries depend
on the exact location of the soft particles. \\ \\
In Fig.~\ref{fig:events} we show an event with relatively large $x_B$ and $Q$. 
%
We clearly observe that the Centauro algorithm tends to cluster fewer particles together.
%
As it suppresses jet clustering for $\theta < \pi/2$ (i.e.\ in the remnant hemisphere), the total number of jets is higher than for the $k_T$ or C/A algorithms.
%
On the other hand, the  $k_T$ or C/A algorithms result in fewer and bigger jets, and for this particular event, they lead to the same jets. 
%
However, when we add the sea of ultra-soft emissions, one starts seeing the differences, as the C/A algorithm leads to slightly more jets than the $k_t$ algorithm,
for which soft large-angle radiation gets pulled inside other jets. 
%
The feature of the Centauro algorithm of suppressing clustering in the remnant
hemisphere is even more manifest when the sea of ultra-soft particles is included, as soft radiation that enters the final-state macrojet is predominantly at larger rapidities (i.e.\ larger $\theta$). \\ \\
In Fig.~\ref{fig:events2} we show an event with a slightly smaller value for $x_B$ and $Q$. 
%
We again observe that the Centauro algorithm leads to a larger number of jets.
%
Secondly, it is interesting to notice that the $k_t$ algorithm forms a jet that covers a larger area in the $\theta-\phi$ plane than the C/A algorithm. 
%
This is explained by the fact that the C/A clusters following
angular ordering, while for the $k_t$ one first merges around
soft particles.
%
These clustering features of the C/A is what makes it particularly
suited for the design of resolution variables with simple all-orders
corrections~\cite{vanBeekveld:2025zjh}. \\ \\
We now describe the properties of the clustered jets.

Fig.~\ref{fig:zeta} shows the $z-\eta$ plane for all final-state jets constructed with the
three algorithms considering $10^6$ events. 
%
Here, $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$, the  lightcone-momentum fraction carried by each of the final-state jets, is defined as
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:zjet}
	z_{\mathrm{jet}} = \frac{P \cdot p_{\mathrm{jet}}}{P \cdot q} = \frac{p_{\mathrm{jet}}^E - p_{\mathrm{jet}}^z}{|Q|}\,,
\end{equation} 
%
where the last equation holds in the Breit frame. We show contours
corresponding to areas that contain $68\%$ (dash-dotted), $38\%$
(dashed), and $20\%$ (solid) of the total number of points in the
plot, for each of the three considered jet algorithms.
%
For this plot, and all plots that follow, we will show Centauro in
grey, C/A in blue and $k_t$ in orange. \\ \\
We observe that there are two more dense regions: one at small  $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ and (large and) positive pseudo-rapidity, and one at large $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ and negative rapidity.
%
The Centauro algorithm, which suppresses clustering in the remnant hemisphere, yields more jets in the former region compared to the other jet algorithms, which can be seen by the gray area extending to somewhat larger $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ values.
%
The central region is populated by soft wide-angle radiation that form stand-alone jets.
It is therefore not surprising that the $k_t$ algorithm yields more jets in this region, as it tends to cluster soft particles first.
