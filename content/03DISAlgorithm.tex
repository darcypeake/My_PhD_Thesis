\chapter{ A generalised-$k_t$ jet
algorithm for Deep Inelastic Scattering}
\label{chapter: DIS}


\section{Introduction}

Lepton-hadron colliders such as the decommissioned Hadron Elektron
Ringanlage (HERA) and the forthcoming Electron--Ion Collider (EIC) are
unique laboratories for the clean investigation of the inner structure
of the nucleons and the nuclei that they form.
%
Although the HERA collider has not been operational since 2007, access
to previously recorded HERA data has enabled recent re-analysis taking
advantage of modern jet algorithms and event-shape observables in Deep
Inelastic Scattering (DIS)~\cite{ZEUS:2023zie,H1:2023fzk,ZEUS:2024mhu,H1:2024nde,H1:2024aze,H1:2024pvu}.
%
In the theory community this has spurred a renewed interest in
formulating jet
algorithms~\cite{Arratia:2020ssx,vanBeekveld:2023chs,Caucal:2024vbv},
and in resummed calculations of event shapes for
DIS~\cite{Knobbe:2023ehi,Fang:2024auf,Cao:2024ota,Ee:2025scz,vanBeekveld:2025zjh}.
%
Such modern QCD tools will be instrumental for the EIC,\footnote{And the LHeC~\cite{LHeC:2020van} should it be approved.} currently under construction at
Brookhaven National Laboratory, whose operation is scheduled to start
in the early 2030s.  The EIC will provide new opportunities to study
the structure of QCD, including the spin structure of the proton, the
dynamics of confinement, and gluon
saturation~\cite{Accardi:2012qut,AbdulKhalek:2021gbh}.
%
%The aim of the present paper is to introduce a family of DIS jet algorithms that are 1. simple to use; 2. allow for a  
Since jet production is the dominant process at hadron-lepton
colliders, jet reconstruction will play an essential part in many
(re)-analysis for both the EIC and HERA. \\ \\
Most developments in jet algorithms over the past decades have been
driven by hadron collider applications, in particular at the LHC,
where the anti-$k_t$ algorithm~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp} has become
the standard choice. Jet algorithms for DIS were developed around the
same time as those for hadronic collisions, but unlike for hadron
collisions, no single algorithm dominates the landscape.
%
The exclusive (spherically-invariant) $k_t$ algorithm was introduced
in Ref.~\cite{Catani:1992zp} extending the Durham algorithm for
$e^+e^-$ collisions~\cite{Catani:1991hj,Brown:1991hx} to DIS by
treating the proton remnant as a particle of infinite momentum. An
angular-ordered version of this algorithm was later proposed in
Ref.~\cite{Wobisch:1998wt}. It modifies the $e^+e^-$ Cambridge
algorithm~\cite{Dokshitzer:1997in} to DIS again by treating the proton
remnant as a particle of infinite momentum. A longitudinally invariant
(LI) $k_t$ algorithm was introduced in
Ref.~\cite{Ellis:1993tq,Catani:1993hr}, and was extended to an
angular-ordered version (the Aachen algorithm) in
Ref.~\cite{Wobisch:1998wt}. The latter two algorithms are identical to
the hadron-collider versions, except that the clustering takes place
in the Breit frame as is the case for all the DIS jet algorithms
listed here (cf. Sec.~\ref{sec:Kinematics} for details on the Breit
frame). More recently, the Centauro algorithm~\cite{Arratia:2020ssx}
was proposed, combining features of longitudinally-invariant $k_t$ algorithms with those of
spherically-invariant algorithms. Likewise a set of jet definitions
suitable for small-\xB DIS were proposed in
Ref.~\cite{Caucal:2024vbv}. \\ \\
Unlike at the LHC, where one of the main requirements of a good jet
algorithm is the insensitivity to pile-up and underlying events which
are both mostly absent in DIS, a good jet algorithm is one that
preserves a meaningful clustering sequence and allows soft radiation
to influence jet boundaries while respecting the natural hemisphere
structure of the Breit frame. For this reason it is natural to
consider $k_t$ and Cambridge/Aachen type algorithms in DIS, rather
than anti-$k_t$. A Cambridge/Aachen-based DIS algorithm designed for
the study of Lund variables~\cite{Dreyer:2018nbf,vanBeekveld:2025zjh}
and parton shower logarithmic accuracy was introduced in
Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs}, and forms the starting point for a
spherically-invariant generalised $k_t$ algorithm adapted to DIS. It
follows the structure of the generalised $k_t$ algorithm used in
$e^+e^-$ collisions as implemented and introduced in
\FASTJET~\cite{Cacciari:2011ma}, but is formulated in the Breit frame
and does not have an anti-$k_t$ variant. \\ \\
The structure of this paper is as follows. 
%
In Section~\ref{sec:Kinematics} we introduce the kinematics relevant to DIS and define the Breit frame. 
%
In Section~\ref{sec:oldDISalgo} we summarise already existings DIS jet algorithms, while in Section~\ref{sec:ourDISalgo} we present our new family of inclusive, spherically-invariant generalised-$k_t$ jet algorithms.
%
In Section~\ref{sec:pheno} we examine the behaviour of the new algorithms and we provide a comparison with the Centauro one.
%
Finally, we present our conclusions in Section~\ref{sec:concl}.


\section{DIS Kinematics and the Breit Frame}
\label{sec:Kinematics}
DIS is defined by the process
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DIS-Process}
    \ell_i(k) + h(P) \rightarrow \ell'_f(k') + X\,,
\end{equation}
where $P^\mu$ is the momentum of the incoming nucleon $h$,
$k^\mu$ is the momentum of the lepton beam $\ell_i$,
$k'^\mu$ is the momentum of the outgoing lepton $\ell'_f$,
and $X$ denotes the hadronic final state. 
The standard DIS invariants are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DIS-invariants}
    Q^2 = -q^2\,, \qquad
    \xB = \frac{Q^2}{2 P\!\cdot q}\,, \qquad
    \ydis = \frac{P\!\cdot q}{P\!\cdot k}\,,
\end{equation}
with $q^\mu = k^\mu - k'^\mu$ the momentum of the exchanged virtual photon.
At the lowest perurbative order, the hadronic final state consists of a single outgoing parton,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DIS-LO}
    \ell_i(k) + p_{\mathrm{in}} \rightarrow \ell_f(k') + p_{\mathrm{out}}\,,
\end{equation}
where $p_{\mathrm{in}}^\mu = \xB P$ and $p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu = q^\mu
-p_{\mathrm{in}}^\mu$ are the momenta of the incoming and outgoing
partons, respectively.  The \emph{Breit frame}\footnote{The frame was
first introduced in Ref.~\cite{Breit:1929zz} in the context of
relativistic scattering kinematics. The term \emph{Breit frame} was
first coined by Ernst, Sachs, and Wali in
Refs.~\cite{Ernst:1960zza,Sachs:1962zzc}. For an explicit
implementation we refer the reader to Appendix 7.11 in
Ref.~\cite{Devenish:2004pb}.} plays a central role in many DIS jet
algorithms~\cite{Webber:1993bm}.
%
It is defined by
\begin{equation}
  2\xB \vec{P} + \vec{q} = 0\,,
\end{equation}
such that the virtual photon carries no energy component and is aligned along the $z$-axis, i.e.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DIS-photon}
    q^\mu = (0,0,0,-Q)\,,
\end{equation}
and is therefore purely space-like.  
In this frame, the incoming and outgoing parton momenta take the simple form
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:DIS-partons}
    p_{\mathrm{in}} ^\mu &= \frac{Q}{2}(1,0,0,+1)\,, \quad
    p_{\mathrm{out}}^{\mu} &= \frac{Q}{2}(1,0,0,-1)\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Thus, in the Breit frame, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:Breit-frame},
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/Breit-frame.png}
    \caption{The DIS Born event in the Breit frame, taken from Ref.~\cite{Arratia:2020ssx}.}
    \label{fig:Breit-frame}
\end{figure}
 the proton and virtual photon collide head-on.
%
Within this frame, the proton remnant appears in the region at positive rapidity (the \emph{target hemisphere}), while the struck quark lies in the negative rapidity region (the \emph{current hemisphere}). 
%
In practice, the jet clustering in this frame can lead to radiation from the proton remnant  being absorbed into the struck-quark jet, making the separation between the two hemispheres an important feature of DIS jet algorithms.



\section{Existing DIS jet algorithms}
\label{sec:oldDISalgo}

Before presenting a generalised $k_t$ algorithm for DIS, it is
instructive to first discuss existing jet algorithms. That will also
allow us to highlight similarities and differences. We stress here,
that all the DIS jet algorithms are formulated in the Breit frame.

\subsection{Durham and Cambridge jet algorithms for $e^+e^-$ collisions}
%
Since the exclusive jet algorithms for DIS are adapted from their
$e^+e^-$ counterparts, and in fact use them explicitly in a
re-clustering step, we start by reviewing those. The $k_t$ variant
(Durham) was introduced in Ref.~\cite{Catani:1991hj,Brown:1991hx}
while the angular ordered variant (Cambridge) was introduced in
Ref.~\cite{Dokshitzer:1997in}. Here we present them together.
%
Given the list of objects undergoing the clustering procedure, that are conventionally called pseudojets, one defines a distance measure  $d_{ij}$
\begin{align}
d_{i j} = \frac{2 (1 - \cos \theta_{i j})}{s^p} \min (E_i^{2p}, E_j^{2p})\,,
\end{align}
where $s$ is the center-of-mass squared energy, and $p=1$ corresponds to the Durham algorithm and $p=0$ to the Cambridge one. 
%
One also introduces a resolution parameter
\begin{eqnarray}
  y_{ij} = \frac{2 (1 - \cos \theta_{i j})}{s} \min (E_i^{2}, E_j^{2}),
\end{eqnarray}
which coincides with $d_{ij}$ when $p=1$, i.e. for the Durham variant, 
and a parameter $y_{\mathrm{cut}}\leq 1$. The algorithm then proceeds as follows
\begin{enumerate}
\item \label{item1eekt}For every pair of pseudojets compute $d_{ij}$.
%
\item The pair with the smallest $d_{ij}$ is clustered together
  if $y_{ij}<y_{\mathrm{cut}}$, and an object with momentum equal to
  the sum of the pseudojet $i$ and $j$ momenta is inserted in the list
  of pseudojets instead of $i$ and $j$.
%
This recombination is dubbed the $E$-scheme but other alternatives
exist~\cite{JADE:1986kta,JADE:1988xlj,Catani:1991hj}.
%
If istead $y_{ij}>y_{\mathrm{cut}}$, the softest (i.e. less energetic)
pseudojet is removed from the list and is promoted to a jet: this
mechanism is called soft freezing, and acts to prevent the algorithm
from returning a large number of soft jets.\footnote{Here we present the algorithm as implemented in
    \textsc{FastJet}~\cite{Cacciari:2011ma}. In the original formulation, one moves to the second smallest $d_{ij}$ if the smallest has
    $y_{ij}>1$.} In effect, the parameter
$y_{\mathrm{cut}}$ therefore defines the transverse momentum resolution of
the algorithm.
%
\item Repeat \ref{item1eekt} until there are no more pseudojets to be clustered. 
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Exclusive $k_t$-algorithm for DIS}
\label{sec:exclktalgo}
The exclusive $k_t$-algorithm for DIS was presented in
Ref.~\cite{Catani:1992zp}.
%
It is based on the equivalent $e^+e^-$ variant~\cite{Catani:1991hj} that we discussed above. 
%
Instad of using the center-of-mass energy $\sqrt{s}$, one introduces an energy scale $E_t$ satisfying $\Lambda \ll E_t\le
Q$\footnote{A larger value of $E_t$ will result in a ``fatter'' beam
jet} and defines the distance measure $d_{ij}$ between two pseudojets
as
\begin{align}
d_{i j} = \frac{2 (1 - \cos \theta_{i j})}{E_t^2} \min (E_i^2, E_j^2)\,,
\end{align}
as well as a beam distance for each pseudojet
\begin{align}
  d_{iB} = \frac{2 (1 - \cos \theta_{i B})}{E_t^2} E_i^2\,,
\end{align}
where $\theta_{ij}$ is the angular separation between two pseudojets,
$\theta_{iB}$ is the angle of the pseudojet with respect to the beam, and
$E_{k}$ is the energy of the pseudojets.
%
We remind the reader that energies and angles are defined in the Breit frame.
%
The cluster procedure works in two steps.  For the first step:
%
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \label{exclkt1} For every pseudojet $i$ compute $d_{iB}$ and for every pair
    $i,j$ compute $d_{ij}$.
  \item Take the smallest value of all $d_{ij}$ and $d_{iB}$. If the
    smallest value is a $d_{ij}$ and $d_{ij}<1$, combine their momenta
    into a new pseudojet. If $d_{iB}<1$ is the smallest, include $i$
    as a beam jet, and remove it from the list of pseudojets. 
  \item Repeat the procedure from step \ref{exclkt1} for all
    pseudojets, until all distance measures $d_{ij}, d_{iB}>1$. The
    resulting pseudojets are called the macro jets.
\end{enumerate}
In the second step of the algorithm we remove the beam jet and undo
the clustering of the macro jets, and re-cluster the constituents
using the $e^+e^-$ exclusive $k_t$-algorithm (Durham), introducing
$y_{\mathrm{cut}}<1$, and $E_t^2$ instead of $s$ to define $d_{ij}$.

\subsection{Cambridge algorithm for DIS}
\label{sec:camdis}
The Cambridge algorithm for DIS was introduced in
Ref.~\cite{Wobisch:1998wt}. It is again inspired by the Cambridge
algorithm for $e^+e^-$-collisions~\cite{Dokshitzer:1997in}, and is
very similar to the exclusive $k_t$-algorithm.
Alongside the distance $d_{ij}$ between two pseudojets
\begin{align}
d_{i j} = 2 (1 - \cos \theta_{i j})\,,
\end{align}
one includes the beam distance
\begin{align}
d_{i B} = 2 (1 - \cos \theta_{i B})\,.
\end{align}
 We also define the resolution variable 
$y_{ij}$
\begin{align}
  y_{i j} = d_{ij} \frac{\min (E_i^2, E_j^2)}{Q^2}.
\end{align}
that in the case of a recombination with a beam takes the form
\begin{align}
  y_{i B} = d_{iB} \frac{E_i^2}{Q^2}\,,
\end{align}
because the beam is assumed to have infinite
energy.\footnote{Similarly to the $k_t$-variant one could introduce
$E_t$ instead of $Q$. }


Also in this case the algorithm then proceeds through two steps. 
The first step is
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \label{cam1} For every pseudojet $i$ compute $d_{iB}$ and for every pair
    $i,j$ compute $d_{ij}$.
  \item Take the smallest value of all $d_{ij}$ and $d_{iB}$.
    If the
    smallest value is a $d_{ij}$, combine their momenta
    into a new pseudojet if $y_{ij}<1$ otherwise call the softest (less energetic) a jet. 
    %
    If $d_{iB}$ is the smallest include $i$ as a beam jet, and remove
    it from the list of pseudojets if $y_{iB}<1$, otherwise promote it
    to a jet.
  \item Repeat the procedure from step \ref{cam1} for all
    pseudojets, until all resolution variables $y_{ij}, y_{iB}>1$,
    at which point we promote all the remaining pseudojets to jets and proceed with the second step. 
\end{enumerate}
In the second step of the algorithm we undo the clustering of the
all the jets (excluding the beam jet), and re-cluster the constituents using the
$e^+e^-$ Cambridge algorithm using a resolution parameter $y_{\text{cut}} < 1$.

\subsection{Generalised inclusive $k_t$-algorithms for $pp$ and DIS}
\label{sec:gen-kt}
The inclusive longitudinally-invariant~(LI) $k_t$-algorithm was first
introduced in Ref.~\cite{Catani:1993hr} (see Ref.~\cite{Ellis:1993tq}
for the exclusive version) for hadron-hadron collisions. An angular
ordered variant was since introduced in Ref.~\cite{Wobisch:1998wt} and
then eventually the family of generalised inclusive $k_t$-algorithms
was introduced in Ref.~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp} along the anti-$k_t$
algorithm.
%
The DIS variant is in this case identical to the hadron-hadron
variant, except that the clustering takes place in the Breit frame.
%
As usual we define a set of distance measures $d_{ij}$ and $d_{iB}$ given by
\begin{align}
  d_{ij} = \min(p_{Ti}^{2p},p_{Tj}^{2p}) \frac{\Delta R_{ij}^2}{R^2}\,,\quad d_{iB} = p_{Ti}^{2p}\,,
\end{align}
where $\Delta R_{ij}^2 = (y_i -y_j)^2 + (\phi_i - \phi_j^2)$, with
$y_i$ the rapidity and $\phi_i$ the azimuthal angle of pseudojet
$i$. The procedure is then given by
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute all the distances $d_{ij}$ and $d_{iB}$. If a $d_{ij}$
  is the smallest, cluster $i$ and $j$, if $d_{iB}$ is the smallest
  call $i$ a jet.
\item Repeat until no more pseudojets remain.
\end{enumerate}
We note that the $k_t$-algorithm corresponds to $p=1$,
Cambridge/Aachen to $p=0$ and anti-$k_t$ to $p=-1$.

The algorithm can be made spherically invariant~(SI) instead of LI by
replacing the distance measures with
\begin{align}
  d_{ij} = \min(E_{i}^{2p},E_{j}^{2p}) \frac{1-\cos\theta_{ij}}{1-\cos R}\,,\quad d_{iB} = E_{i}^{2p}\,,
\end{align}
as is appropriate for $e^+e^-$ collisions~\cite{Cacciari:2011ma}. 
\subsection{Centauro algorithm}
The Centauro algorithm~\cite{Arratia:2020ssx} is a longitudinally
invariant jet algorithm designed to capture jets close to the Born
configuration in the Breit frame. Longitudinally invariant $k_t$-type
algorithms such as those presented in Sec.~\ref{sec:gen-kt} cluster in the
$(y,\phi)$ plane and are unable to form a jet along the beam axis,
where $y \to -\infty$. Conversely, the spherically invariant
algorithms can cluster around the beam axis but do not preserve
longitudinal invariance.

The Centauro algorithm
is designed to address both of these limitations. It is built around
the variable $\bar{\eta}_i$, which behaves
differently in the two hemispheres:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Current hemisphere:}  
      $\bar{\eta}_i$ decreases as particles become closer in angle, allowing particles in this hemisphere to cluster.
\item \textbf{Target hemisphere:}  
      $\bar{\eta}_i$ becomes large and diverges. This prevents clustering in the proton beam region, similar to the behaviour of the anti-$k_t$-algorithm.
\end{itemize}
%
Working in the Breit frame, for every momentum $p_i^\mu$ we define the variable $\bar{\eta}_i$ as
\begin{align}
\bar{\eta}_i = -2\frac{Q^2}{q\cdot n_{\mathrm{out}}} \frac{2 p_{T,i}}{p_i \cdot n_{\mathrm{in}}}\,,
\end{align}
with 
\begin{subequations}
  \begin{align}
  n_{\mathrm{in}}^\mu =& \frac{2}{Q}\xB P^\mu \\
  n_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu =& \frac{2}{Q} \left(q^\mu -\xB P^\mu \right)\\
  p_{T,i}^2 =& \frac{2 (p_i \cdot n_{\mathrm{in}})(p_i \cdot n_{\mathrm{out}})}{ n_{\mathrm{in}}\cdot n_{\mathrm{out}}},
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
meaning that $p_{T,i}$ the transverse component of the momentum in the Breit frame (or any other frame that only differs by a longitudinal boost from the Breit frame).
%
From this, one constructs the inter-pseudojet distance measure as
\footnote{Note that we are reporting the definition used in the \texttt{fjcontrib} implementation, that is the one we have also used in this work.}
\begin{align}
d_{ij} = \frac{(\bar{\eta}_i - \bar{\eta}_j)^2 + 2 \bar{\eta}_i \bar{\eta}_j (1- \cos \Delta \phi_{ij})}{R^2}\,.
\end{align}
The distance to the beam is always equal to $d_{iB} = 1$. 
%
One then clusters jet according to the procedure given in Sec.~\ref{sec:gen-kt}.


\section{A new generalised-$k_t$ algorithm for DIS}
\label{sec:ourDISalgo}
\subsection{The algorithm}
We now introduce the inclusive spherically-invariant generalised $k_t$ algorithm for DIS which we have implemented in the \textsc{Fastjet} plugin \texttt{fjcontrib}.
%
Our aim is to produce an algorithm that corresponds to the $e^+e^-$ one,
where one includes an infinite-energy parton aligned along the proton direction in the cluster sequence.
%
Therefore, it is immediately clear that this algorithm will not admit
an anti-$k_t$ variant, but only $p\ge 0$ values will be physical. 
%
The angular-ordered variant of the algorithm ($p=0$) was already presented by some of us in Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs}, and used to define Lund Tree Event shapes with remarkably simple all-orders structure~\cite{vanBeekveld:2025zjh}.

We introduce the following distances
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
	d_{ij}& = \min(E_{i}^{2p},E_{j}^{2p}) (1-\cos\theta_{ij}) \,, \\
	d_{iB} &= E_{i}^{2p}(1-\cos\theta_{iB})\,,
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
with $p \geq 0$,
% 
as well as dimensionfull resolution variable for clusterings between two final-state pseudo-jets
\begin{align}
	v_{ij}& = \min(E_{i}^{2},E_{j}^{2}) (1-\cos\theta_{ij}).
\end{align}
The clustering proceeds as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Introduce a dimensionfull parameter $v_{\mathrm{cut}}^2>0$, which can be of the order of $Q^2$, or even infinite.
  \item Find the smallest $d_{ij}$ or $d_{iB}$.
  \item If the smallest is a $d_{iB}$, call $i$ a jet, and remove it from the list of pseudojets.
  \item If the smallest is a $d_{ij}$, if $v_{ij}<v_{\mathrm{cut}}^2$, merge $i$ and $j$, otherwise call the softest pseudojet a jet, and remove it from the list of pseudojets.
  \item Continue untill there are no particles in the list. 
\end{enumerate}
As in the standard inclusive LI
algorithms for $pp$ collisions, some of the reconstructed jets
originate either solely from partons emitted by the incoming leg or
exclusively from soft, large-angle radiation. In $pp$ collisions, such
jets are removed by imposing a transverse-momentum (and a rapidity)
cut.
%
This however cannot be applied to DIS, as the jet originating from the
fragmentation of the struck quark does not necessary carry a large
transverse momentum.
%
Therefore, in Sec.~\ref{sec:macrojet} we illustrate how to identify
the jet most likely associated with the struck quark, that we dub as macrojet.
%
This identification can be applied also to other jet algorithms, such
as Centauro.


\subsection{Identifying the macrojet}
\label{sec:macrojet}
\begin{figure}[!t]
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,page=7]{figs_new/paper_rivet_cmp_algos.pdf}
\caption{Normalised Breit-frame energy distribution of the final-state macrojet for neutal-current DIS, $\sqrt{s}$=300~GeV using the Centauro~(left) jet algorithm, as well as our variant of the C/A~(left) and $k_t$ jet algorithm~(right).
%
The final-state macrojet is identified as the one which maximises the light-cone component opposite in direction to the proton ($z_{\mathrm{jet}}$, solid line) or the Breit-frame pseudo-rapidity ($\eta_{\mathrm{jet}}$, dash-dotted lines).
%
Events are generated using the \PYTHIAn~\cite{Bierlich:2022pfr} default shower, with dipole recoil option~\cite{Cabouat:2017rzi}.
See Tab.~\ref{tab:input-params} for the precise settings.
 }
 \label{fig:jetEIR}
\end{figure}
Inside the list of jets, we now identify the \emph{final-state
macrojet}, i.e. the jet that is more likely to contain the remainders
of the original struck quark. 
%
One choice is to select the jet with that carries the largest light-cone component $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ of the original struck quark.
%
From Sec.~\ref{sec:Kinematics} we see we can perform the following Sudakov decomposition of a jet momentum
\begin{equation}
  p_{\mathrm{jet}}^\mu = \frac{|k_\perp^2|}{z_{\mathrm{jet}} x_{\mathrm{B}} Q^2} P^\mu + z_{\mathrm{jet}} \, p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu + k_\perp^\mu,
\end{equation}
where $P^\mu$ is the proton momentum, $p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu = q^\mu - x_{\mathrm{B}} P^{\mu}$ can be identified as the original struck quark momentum, and $k_\perp^\mu$ is a space-light vector orthogonal to $P^\mu$ and $p_{\mathrm{out}}^\mu$.
%
Therefore, the jet with the largest lightcone component $z_{\mathrm{jet}} $ is the one whose momentum $p_{\mathrm{jet}}^{\mu}$ maximises the dot product with $P^\mu$. \\ \\
As discussed in Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs} this definition ensures that the macrojet is infrared safe, in contrast to alternative prescriptions, such as selecting the jet with the most negative rapidity.
%
In Fig.~\ref{fig:jetEIR} one can observe that both for the new family
of jet algorithms we are proposing and for Centauro, identifying the
final-state macrojet using the rapidity (dash-dotted lines) leads to a soft tail. The tail
corresponds to soft ``junk'' generated either from radiation from the
DIS process or from the beam remnant, that happens to be in the
backwards direction. We note that for the $k_t$-algorithm the
soft tail is partially suppressed even selecting the macro jet with the
most negative rapidity, but there is no exponentiall decreas in the tail.
%
Maximising the light-cone component in the direction of the the
original struck quark (solid curves) exponentially suppresses the tail for all
algorithms. \\ \\
Similarly to the exclusive algorithms in Secs.~\ref{sec:exclktalgo} and
\ref{sec:camdis}, one can then recluster the final-state macrojet with
a dimensionful $v_{\mathrm{cut}}'$ parameter, smaller than the original
$v_{\mathrm{cut}}$ used in the first clustering procedure (which can also
be set to $\infty$), to resolve more jet substructure. \\ \\
The properties of the macrojet provide a useful way to characterise
the behaviour of the algorithm.
%
As discussed earlier, clustering in the Breit frame can lead to
contamination between the current and target hemispheres.
%
Studying the macrojet provides a direct way to assess how effectively
the algorithm separates these regions and how well it reconstructs the
struck-quark jet.

In the top panel we show a small $x_{\mathrm{B}}$ and small $Q$ event.

When $x_{\mathrm{B}}$ is small, the incoming parton can radiate substantially, and therefore the struck quark may absorb a large transverse momentum and move away from $\theta=0$.

We indeed notice an energetic particle coloured in red positioned at $\theta \approx \pi/3$ ($\eta \approx -0.5$) and $\phi \approx 0$, that forms the final-state microjet clustering with several less energetic particles (smaller red disks).

In total this algorithm yields 252 jets for the event under consideration. \\ \\

%
However we can observe that soft particles in the current hemisphere, produced by soft-large angle radiation, and are largely separated in rapidity from the recoiled struck quark, do not cluster inside the final-state macro-jet for the C/A, while they do with the $k_t$ algorithm.
% 
This is a consequence of the fact that the $k_t$ algorithm starts clustering around soft particles. \\ \\
In the bottom panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:events} we can notice similar features but in a less crowded environment, as we select an event with much larger $x_{\mathrm{B}}$ (and $Q$), so there is less radiation.
%
As a result there are fewer but bigger disks, signalling a diminished energy degradation activity.
%
The final-state macrojet now comprises particles around $\theta=\pi$, as the struck quark did not absorb much transverse momentum recoil, and again the Centauro algorithm tends to form more jets smaller in size.
%
Once again the C/A algorithm yields fewer jets compared to Centauro (13 instead of 25), and more compared to $k_t$ (6 jets).
%
It is however interesting to notice that there is a group of particles around with $\theta \gtrapprox \pi/2$ that in the case of the $k_t$ algorithm clusters with other particles in the remnant hemisphere to form their own jet, while for the C/A algorithm they cluster within the final-state macrojet.
%
Again this is explained by the fact that the C/A clusters following angular ordering, while for the $k_t$ one first merges what is around soft particles. These clustering features of the C/A is what makes it particularly suited for the design of resolution variables with simple all-orders corrections~\cite{vanBeekveld:2025zjh}. \\ \\

\subsection{Energy and $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ of the macro jet}
\label{sec:macrojet}

We now investigate the properties of the final-state macrojet, identified as the jet that maximises the light-cone component of the original struck quark.
%
We begin by examining the energy of the macro jet, $E_{\mathrm{jet}}$,
normalised to $Q/2$. At the Born-level this variable is exactly
$1$. The normalised energy distributions of the macro jet for the C/A,
$k_t$, and Centauro algorithms are shown shown in the left panel of
Fig.~\ref{fig:E-z-algs}.
%
\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth,page=1]{figs_new/paper_rivet_cmp_algos.pdf}
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth,page=3]{figs_new/paper_rivet_cmp_algos.pdf}
    %\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/link_E_kT_CA.pdf}
    \caption{Normalised energy distribution~(letft panel) and $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ of the final-state macro jet in the Breit frame for the C/A ($p=0$), $k_t$ ($p=1$), and Centauro variants of the DIS algorithm.}
    \label{fig:E-z-algs}
\end{figure}
%
For this plot, and all plots that follow, we will show Centauro in
grey, C/A in blue and $k_t$ in orange. Let us first compare C/A and
$k_t$. The $k_t$ variant displays a noticeably longer
high-$E_{\mathrm{jet}}$ tail compared to the C/A variant.  This
behaviour reflects the $k_t$ algorithm's tendency to cluster softer
particles first, allowing more soft radiation to be included in the
hard jets. Consequently, the macro jet becomes more energetic,
incorporating additional radiation from the target hemisphere. We also
note that in the region around the peak, the two algorithms give very
similar results. \\ \\
We now compare these results with those obtained using the Centauro
algorithm. The Centauro distribution displays a longer tail at small
$E_{\mathrm{jet}}$ than either of the generalised $k_t$ variants. This
suggests that the Centauro algorithm tends to not to cluster any of
the soft radiation and instead returns a large number of softer
jets. This can most likely be understood by inspecting the
$\bar{\eta}$ variable, which effectively prevents partons from
clustering in the target hemisphere, and prevents the struck quark in
the current hemisphere from clustering with anything that is not very
close in angle. The large energy tail behaves very similarly to C/A. The
observed off-set is due to the histograms being normalised. This is
also expected as in the current hemisphere the Centauro algorithm
mimics an angular-ordered algorithm. \\ \\
To further investigate the behaviour of soft radiation, we examine the struck-quark momentum fraction carried by the macro jet, defined as
\begin{equation}
    z_{\mathrm{jet}} = \frac{P \cdot p_{\mathrm{jet}}}{P \cdot q} = \frac{p_{\mathrm{jet}}^E - p_{\mathrm{jet}}^z}{|Q|}\,.
\end{equation}
The resulting distributions are shown in the right panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:E-z-algs}.

One can notice the presence of three regions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a peak region, around $z_{\mathrm{jet}} = 0.95$, characterised by only soft and collinear emissions;
  \item a ``dijet" region, with $0.5 < z_{\mathrm{jet}} \lesssim 0.8$, where the final-state macrojet still carries most of the original momentum, but a non-negligible fraction has been distributed to other jets;
  \item a ``multi-jet" region, with $z_{\mathrm{jet}}<0.5$, where the struck quark lost the majority of its energy.
\end{itemize}
We notice that our generalised-$k_t$ algorithms behave very similarly in the peak and dijet region, which terminates with a shoulder around $z_{\mathrm{jet}}=0.5$.
%
This similarity is expected, as these regions are dominated by configuration with at most one hard emission, and the two algorithms behave identically at $\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s)$.
%
Below this shoulder the distributions fall off very quickly, although we observe larger differences between the two algorithms.
%
The Centauro algorithm produces a quite different distribution, where the peak region being noticeably smeared. We however
notice that the slope of the distribution again agrees well with the
C/A variant in the ``dijet" region. This is
perhaps not surprising, as this is the region that is dominated by two
well-separated jets. Towards $z_{\mathrm{jet}} \to 1$, the spectrum
differs significantly, which can probably again be attributed to the
behaviour of the $\bar{\eta}$ variable which prevents the clustering
of soft radiation carrying away a fraction of the energy. Similarly,
for $z_{\mathrm{jet}} < 0.5$ the spectrum is dominated by soft jets in
the target hemisphere. 


\subsection{Impact of hadronisation and beam remnants}
\label{sec:hadronisation}
In this section we study the impact of hadronisation and beam remnants on the final-state macrojet. Given the amount of high quality DIS data that
currently exists, with more to come from the EIC, it is worth
considering if it is possible to use jets to constrain fundamental QCD
parameters, like the value of $\alpha_s$ and or parameters of various
hadronisation models, as is often done with historical $e^+e^-$
collider data and LHC data. Although it is beyond the scope of this
paper to carry out a fit of such parameters, we here discuss how one
might use the jet algorithms to perform such fits. \\ \\
In Fig.~\ref{fig:hadron}
\begin{figure}[t!]
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth,page=10]{figs_new/paper_rivet_hadr_effects.pdf}\\
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth,page=11]{figs_new/paper_rivet_hadr_effects.pdf}
  \caption{Study of the three jet algorithm sensitivity to
    hadronisation and beam remnants. We show results at the parton
    shower level (PS, dotted), parton shower with beam remnants
    (PS+remn, dashed), and parton shower with beam remnants and
    hadronisation (PS+remn+hadr, solid).}
  \label{fig:hadron}
\end{figure}
we show the normalised energy distribution and lightcone fraction as
discussed above for all three jet algorithms. For each plot we show
results at the parton shower level (PS, dotted), parton shower with beam
remnants (PS+remn, dashed), and parton shower with beam remnants and
hadronisation (PS+remn+hadr, solid). The latter distributions, which also include unstable hadrons decay, coincide with the ones showed in the previous section. 
%
We reminder the reader, that unlike in hadron-hadron
collisions there are no underlying events (UE) in DIS. \\ \\
Starting with the normalised energy fraction we see that the beam
remnants do not contaminate the jet spectra much, although there is
some distortion to the right of the peak. The hadronisation on the
other hand has a larger effect. To the left of the peak the
distortions are very sizable for all three algorithms, but most so for
Centauro. To the right of the peak the C/A and $k_t$-algorithms are
surprisingly insensitive to hadronisation until the spectrum becomes
very hard. Centauro receives very large corrections here too, but the
distribution is rather flat, so we attribute the large shift to the
normalisation of the plots. \\ \\
Turning now to the ligtcone component $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ we see again
that the beam remnants have very little effect on the plots, except
for $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ close to $1$. Hadronisation on the other hand
has a large effect. For Centauro there are shape differences to be
found across the spectrum, except for just above
$z_{\mathrm{jet}}=0.5$. This effect is also observed for C/A and
$k_t$, perhaps even to a larger degree. We suspect that this is due to
the region being dominated by two very well-separated jets. Below
$z_{\mathrm{jet}}=0.5$ the C/A and $k_t$-algorithms have a large
off-set, but this is rather constant until around
$z_{\mathrm{jet}}=0.35$. Centauro on the other hand becomes more and
more sensitive to the hadronisation as $z_{\mathrm{jet}}$ becomes
smaller. This is again due to the tendency of Centauro to produce many
isolated soft jets. \\ \\
Taken together it seems that the jet algorithms discussed here could
be used to constrain hadronisation parameters. 
In particular, the region $0.65<z_{\mathrm{jet}}<0.9$ can be used to fit non-perturbative model.
On the other hand the region just above the shoulder $z_{\mathrm{jet}} =0.5$ seems more insensitive to hadronisation effects and could be used to determine $\alpha_s$ from higher-order analytical calculations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:concl}
In this paper we have presented a generalisation of the
angular-ordered jet algorithm which was first presented in
Ref.~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023chs}. The generalisation follows the usual
idea of introducing an exponent to control how much to weight the
energy of each pseudojet in the clustering. The algorithm is very
simple, and gives jets that are very naturally separated in the
current and target hemispheres. We compared the algorithms to the
recent Centauro jet algorithm, which by design is more sensitive to
soft emissions. One application that we have in mind, is to use the
new jet algorithms to re-analyse old HERA data, with the aim of
1. fitting $\alpha_s$ from jets, and 2. to constrain hadronisation
parameters in parton shower models. Whereas the $\alpha_s$ extraction
is unlikely to be competitive with other extractions on its own, we
think there is value in studying hadronisation in DIS data. This is in
part due to the fact that DIS allows for studying jets in bins of
$Q$. This could provide a natural handle on the energy dependence of
hadronisation parameters. We leave this to future studies. \\ \\The algorithm has been implemented as a plugin to \textsc{FastJet} as
part of \texttt{fjcontrib}.
