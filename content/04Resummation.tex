\chapter{Resummation}
\label{ch:resum}

In this chapter, we discuss the technique of \emph{resummation}. As 
outlined in Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}, following the initial hard 
scattering, the produced coloured partons undergo QCD radiation dominated 
by soft and collinear emissions. For many observables, these emissions 
give rise to logarithmically enhanced contributions that become large in 
the limit where the observable, $v$, is small. In this regime, fixed-order 
perturbation theory breaks down and dedicated resummation techniques are 
required.

Traditionally, resummation for final-state observables was performed in an observable-dependent manner, requiring separate analytical calculations for each case~\cite{Collins:1984kg, Catani:1992ua, Bonciani:2003nt}. The first steps towards a more general approach were taken in~\cite{Banfi:2001bz}, where a systematic semi-numerical framework was developed to achieve NLL accuracy for a broad class of observables. This framework was subsequently extended and implemented in the computer code \textsc{CAESAR} (\emph{Computer Automated Expert Semi-Analytical Resummer})~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}. In this approach, the resummation formula is expressed in terms of a set of observable-dependent parameters, which can be determined automatically and numerically for a given observable. This method has been used to determine the resummation of a broad class of observables, such as event-shape observables and jet rates. This framework was later extended in 
\textsc{ARES} (\emph{Automated Resummation for Event Shapes})~\cite{Banfi:2014sua, 
Banfi:2016zlc, Banfi:2018mcq, Arpino:2019ozn}, which achieves NNLL 
accuracy for jet observables in $e^+e^-$ collisions. In this thesis, we 
work within this framework.

The structure of this chapter is as follows. We begin by introducing event-shape observables and the properties they must satisfy. We then motivate the need for resummation through a fixed-order calculation of a standard event shape, thrust, making explicit the origin and structure of the large logarithms. Finally, we present the NLL resummation formula and the key ingredients 
required to evaluate it for a given observable. This chapter provides the 
necessary foundation for the original research presented in 
Chapter~\ref{ch:hhZjet}.

\section{Event-shapes}
\label{sec:event-shapes}

Event shapes are IRC safe observables constructed 
from the four-momenta of final-state particles, which characterise the geometry 
of an event by quantifying the distribution of energy-momentum flow in the 
hadronic final state. Unlike jet algorithms (see Sec.~\ref{subsec:jet-algorithm}), which cluster particles into 
discrete jets, event shapes provide a continuous description of the 
event geometry, making them powerful probes of QCD 
radiation. They have been widely used in precision studies of QCD, most 
notably in the extraction of the strong coupling constant 
$\alpha_s$~\cite{Becher:2008cf, Abbate:2010xh}. Notable examples 
include:

\begin{itemize}
\item{\textbf{Thrust}}~\cite{Farhi:1977sg, Brandt:1964sa} is defined as,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust}
	T \equiv \max_{\vec{n}} \frac{\sum_i |\vec{p}_i \cdot \vec{n}|}{\sum_i |\vec{p}_i|}\,, \quad \tau \equiv 1 - T \,,
\end{equation}
where $\vec{p}_i$ denotes the three-momentum of particle $i$ in the final state, and the unit vector $\vec{n}$ that maximises this quantity is referred to as the \emph{thrust axis}, $\vec{n}_T$. The thrust axis separates the event into two distinct hemispheres, $\mathcal{H}_1$ and $\mathcal{H}_2$, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:thrust}. It is conventional to work with the variable $\tau$, as defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:thrust}.
For $e^+e^- \to \mathrm{hadrons}$, thrust provides a measure of how ``pencil-like'' an event is: $\tau \to 0$ corresponds to the Born-level configuration with a perfectly back-to-back $q\bar{q}$ pair, while larger values of $\tau$ indicate more spherically distributed multi-particle final state.

\item \textbf{Jet broadenings}~\cite{Clavelli:1979md, Rakow:1981qn, 
Catani:1992jc} quantify the transverse momentum of final-state hadrons 
relative to the thrust axis. The event is divided into two hemispheres 
$\mathcal{H}_1$ and $\mathcal{H}_2$, and for each hemisphere the 
broadening is defined as

\begin{equation}
	B_\ell \equiv \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{H}_\ell} |\vec{p}_i \times \vec{n}_T|}{2 \sum_i |\vec{p}_i|}, \quad \ell = 1, 2\,.
\end{equation}
The wide-jet broadening, narrow-jet broadening, and total 
broadening are defined respectively as  
\begin{equation}
    B_W \equiv \max\{B_1, B_2\}\,, \qquad 
    B_N \equiv \min\{B_1, B_2\}\,, \qquad 
    B_T \equiv B_1 + B_2\,.
\end{equation}

\item{\textbf{Jet masses}}~\cite{Chandramohan:1980ry, Clavelli:1981yh} are defined by computing the invariant mass of each hemisphere,
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{\ell}\equiv \frac{\left(\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}_{\ell}}p_i\right)^2}{\left(\sum_iE_i\right)^2}\,,\quad \ell=1, 2\,,
\end{equation}
where $E_i$ is the energy of the particle $i$ in the final-state.
The heavy-jet mass and light-jet mass are defined respectively as
\begin{equation}
	\rho_H\equiv\max\left\{\rho_1,\rho_2\right\}\,,\quad \rho_L\equiv\min\left\{\rho_1,\rho_2\right\}\,.
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

While event shapes offer detailed insight into the structure of hadronic 
final states, their perturbative predictions are complicated by large 
logarithmic contributions arising from soft and collinear emissions in 
the limit where the observable approaches zero. To illustrate this 
explicitly, we now examine how such logarithms emerge in the fixed-order 
calculation of thrust.

\section{Motivation: thrust and the need for resummation}
\label{sec:thrust-example}

To motivate the need for resummation, we consider a fixed-order 
calculation of the thrust distribution. At Born level, the process 
$e^+e^- \to q\bar{q}$ produces a perfectly back-to-back quark--antiquark 
pair with $\tau = 0$, so the distribution is simply
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau} = \delta(\tau),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_0$ is the leading-order cross section defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma-LO}.

The first non-trivial contribution arises at $\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s)$ 
from the real-emission process $e^+e^- \to q\bar{q}g$. For a 
three-particle final state with energy fractions satisfying 
$x_q + x_{\bar{q}} + x_g = 2$, one can show using 
Eq.~\eqref{eq:thrust} that thrust reduces to
\begin{equation}
T = \max[x_q, x_{\bar{q}}, x_g]\,,
\end{equation}
with $T_{\min} = 2/3$ for the symmetric
configuration $x_q = x_{\bar{q}} = x_g = 2/3$, and $T_{\max} = 1$.
Using the real-emission cross section in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qqg-real}, the
differential distribution is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{dT}
    = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}
      \int_0^1 dx_q \int_0^1 dx_{\bar{q}}\,
      \frac{x_q^2 + x_{\bar{q}}^2}{(1-x_q)(1-x_{\bar{q}})}
      \, \Theta\!\left(x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1\right)
      \delta\!\left(T - \max[x_q, x_{\bar{q}}, x_g]\right)\,.
\end{equation}
As discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:IR-div}, the integrand has soft and collinear singularities 
in the limits $x_q \to 1$ and $x_{\bar{q}} \to 1$. Regulating these via dimensional regularisation
with $d = 4 - 2\varepsilon$, in direct analogy with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qqg-real-d-dim}, gives
\begin{multline}
    \frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{dT}
    = C_F\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}
      \left(\frac{4\pi\mu_R^2}{s}\right)^\varepsilon
      \frac{1}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}
      \int_0^1 dx_q \int_0^1 dx_{\bar{q}} \\
    \times
      \frac{(x_q^2+x_{\bar{q}}^2)
            -\varepsilon(2-x_q-x_{\bar{q}})^2}
           {(1-x_q)^{1+\varepsilon}\,
            (1-x_{\bar{q}})^{1+\varepsilon}\,
            (x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1)^\varepsilon}
      \,\Theta\!\left(x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1\right)
      \delta\!\left(T - \max[x_q, x_{\bar{q}}, x_g]\right)\,,
\end{multline}
subject to the constraint $x_q + x_{\bar{q}} + x_g = 2$.
As in Sec.~\ref{subsec:IR-div}, this gives rise to poles in $\varepsilon$, which
are cancelled upon adding the virtual one-loop correction to
$e^+e^- \to q\bar{q}$. The full result is obtained by noting that the regulated distribution must 
integrate to give the total cross section in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma-full}.
Using this result and the change of variable  to $\tau \equiv 1 - T$, 
the complete thrust distribution up to $\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s)$ is
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:full-thrust-NLO}
    \frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau}
    =\; \delta(\tau)
    + C_F\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}
      \left\{
        \delta(\tau) \left( \frac{\pi^2}{3} - 1 \right)
      \right. \\
    + \left[
        3\,\Theta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}-\tau\right)(1+\tau)(3\tau-1)
        + \frac{(4+6\tau(\tau-1))\ln(1-2\tau)}{1-\tau}
      \right]
      \left( \frac{1}{\tau} \right)_+ \\
    \left.
      - \frac{4+6\tau(\tau-1)}{1-\tau}
        \left( \frac{\ln \tau}{\tau} \right)_+
    \right\}\,,
\end{multline}
where the plus distributions are defined by
\begin{equation}
    \int_0^1 d\tau
    \left[\frac{\ln^k\tau}{\tau}\right]_{\!+}
    g(\tau)
    \equiv
    \int_0^1 d\tau\,\frac{\ln^k\tau}{\tau}
    \left[g(\tau)-g(0)\right]\,,
\end{equation}
so that for $\tau > 0$ they reduce to the ordinary functions
$\ln^k\tau/\tau$. 

Neglecting the $\delta$-function contributions, the terms that diverge as
$\tau \to 0$ are the \emph{logarithmically enhanced terms},
\begin{equation}
    \left(\frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau}\right)_{\!\text{log-enh}}
    = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}
      \left[
        -3 \left(\frac{1}{\tau}\right)_+
        - 4 \left(\frac{\ln \tau}{\tau}\right)_+
      \right]\,.
\end{equation}
The cumulative distribution, or cumulant, is defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cumulant}
    \Sigma(\tau)
    = \frac{1}{\sigma_0} \int_0^\tau d\tau'\, \frac{d\sigma}{d\tau'}\,.
\end{equation}
Using the identities
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:plus-dist-integrals}
    \int_0^\tau d\tau'\,\left(\frac{1}{\tau'}\right)_+ = -L\,, 
    \qquad
    \int_0^\tau d\tau'\,\left(\frac{\ln\tau'}{\tau'}\right)_+ 
    = -\frac{L^2}{2}\,,
\end{equation}
where $L \equiv \ln(1/\tau)$, the contribution from the logarithmically 
enhanced terms to the cumulant is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust-cumulant}
    \Sigma_{\text{log-enh}}(\tau)
    = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \left( -2L^2 + 3L \right)\,.
\end{equation}
All remaining terms in Eq.~\eqref{eq:full-thrust-NLO} remain finite as 
$\tau \to 0$ and do not contribute logarithmic enhancements.

These logarithmic contributions diverge as $\tau \to 0$, corresponding to
the limit of an exactly back-to-back $q\bar{q}$ pair with $T \to 1$.
Despite this perturbative divergence, the physical distribution must vanish
in this limit -- a feature clearly visible in the experimental data shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:thrust-data-FO}, and a property of all IRC safe
event-shape observables.

\paragraph{Breakdown of the fixed-order expansion.}
The divergence of the fixed-order prediction signals a breakdown of the
perturbative expansion in the region $\tau \to 0$. At higher orders, the
cumulant receives contributions of the form $\alpha_s^n L^m$ with
$m \leq 2n$: each power of $\alpha_s$ corresponds to an additional emission,
and each emission can generate up to two logarithms -- one from the soft
limit and one from the collinear limit. These contributions grow as $\tau \to 0$, 
spoiling the convergence of the perturbative series. This behaviour is 
particularly problematic because it occurs precisely in the region where 
the bulk of the data lies. \add{FO is inadequate.}

\paragraph{Resummation.}
To restore predictive power in the region $\tau \ll 1$, the perturbative 
expansion must be reorganised. When $\alpha_s L \sim 1$, successive 
terms in the perturbative series are no longer suppressed, so an 
expansion in powers of $\alpha_s$ alone is insufficient. Instead, one 
performs a systematic \emph{resummation} of the logarithmically enhanced 
contributions to all orders in $\alpha_s$. Therefore, in the region
$\alpha_s L\sim 1$, the cumulant can be
reorganised as follows
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:log-counting}
  \Sigma(\tau) = e^{L g_1(\alpha_s L)}\left[G_2(\alpha_s L)+ \alpha_s G_3(\alpha_s L)+\dots\right]\,.
\end{equation}
The function $g_1(\alpha_s L)$ resums all leading logarithmic (LL)
terms, $\alpha_s^n L^{n+1}$, in $\ln\Sigma$. For rIRC safe
(see Sec.~\ref{sec:obs-req}) observables, $g_1$ exponentiates. The function $G_2(\alpha_s L)$
resums next-to-leading logarithmic (NLL) contributions
($\alpha_s^n L^n$) to $\ln\Sigma$, and so on. For example,
in Chapter.~\ref{ch:hhZjet} we are working at NNLL accuracy,
 i.e.\ calculating $g_1$, $G_2$ and $G_3$.

\ABquestion{A question about fixed-order vs. resummation.}
\ndp{It is worth emphasising the distinction between fixed-order and logarithmic
accuracy. In a fixed-order calculation, improving the accuracy means
computing additional Feynman diagrams at one higher power of $\alpha_s$.
By contrast, each level of logarithmic accuracy already encodes an infinite
set of diagrams --- those contributing at the corresponding logarithmic
order at every power of $\alpha_s$. Advancing from one logarithmic order to the next therefore 
requires identifying which physical effects contribute at that order and 
computing each of their contributions at the necessary accuracy.}

probability of emitting no radiation above the scale $\tau$ -- and a peak
at moderate $\tau$ arising from the combination of this suppression and
the growth of fixed-order contributions at larger values. The Sudakov form

prediction leads to significantly improved agreement between theory and
experiment.}

\section{Problem Specification}
\label{sec:problem-spec}

We consider events with $n_i$ incoming partons and an arbitrary number $n$ of
final-state particles, whose momenta are denoted $p_1,\dots,p_n$.
At Born level the process involves $N$ hard partons in total -- including
both the $n_i$ incoming legs and the $N - n_i$ outgoing legs -- which define
the underlying hard configuration. We define an observable $V(p_1,\dots,p_n)$ as a non-negative function of
the final-state momenta. We assume the observable to be IRC safe and to vanish
smoothly as the event approaches an $N$-parton configuration.
Such observables are commonly referred to as $(N\!+\!1)$-jet observables,
where both the outgoing jets and the incoming beam jets are included in the
jet counting.

To select events containing $N$ or more well-separated hard jets, we 
introduce a selection function $\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)$, defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:H-def}
\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)=
\begin{cases}
1\,, & \text{if the event satisfies the selection criteria,}\\
0\,, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
This can be implemented, for instance, through a jet algorithm that counts 
the number of jets in the event, or by imposing a cut on a suitable 
$N$-jet observable. The total $N$-jet cross section for selected events is 
then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sigma-H}
  \sigma_{\mathcal{H}}
  = \sum_{n=N-n_i}^{\infty}
    \int d\Phi_n\,\frac{d\sigma_n}{d\Phi_n}\,
    \mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)\,,
\end{equation}
where $d\sigma_n/d\Phi_n$ is the differential cross section for producing
$n$ final-state particles and $d\Phi_n$ is the corresponding $n$-particle
phase space, defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:m-phase-space}, and the sum begins at
$n = N - n_i$, the minimum number of final-state particles required to
form the $N$-parton Born configuration.

We are particularly interested in the cumulative distribution of the 
observable $V$ for selected events in which $V$ takes a value smaller 
than $v$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Sigma-H}
  \Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v)
  = \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}}
    \sum_{n=N-n_i}^{\infty}
    \int d\Phi_n\,\frac{d\sigma_n}{d\Phi_n}\,
    \Theta\!\left(v - V(p_1,\dots,p_n)\right)
    \mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)\,.
\end{equation}
In the limit $v\ll 1$, the cumulant $\Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v)$ assumes the
factorised form~\cite{Banfi:2004yd} \ABquestion{Is the notation in this equation 
correct -- specifically $ \mathcal{H}(p_{n_i+1},\dots,p_N)$?}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Sigma-H-fact}
  \Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v)
  \simeq
  \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}}
  \int d\Phi_N\,\frac{d\sigma_N}{d\Phi_N}\,
  \Sigma\!\left(\{p\},v\right)
  \mathcal{H}(p_{n_i+1},\dots,p_N)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\{p\} \equiv \{p_1,\dots,p_N\}$ denotes the full set of $N$ Born
momenta, with $p_1,\dots,p_{n_i}$ the incoming legs and
$p_{n_i+1},\dots,p_N$ the outgoing legs. The term $d\sigma_N/d\Phi_N$ is the
leading-order cross section for the Born configuration, and the integral runs
over the $N$-particle Born phase space $d\Phi_N$. 
\ndp{The factorisation in Eq.~\eqref{eq:Sigma-H-fact} separates the Born kinematics, encoded in
$d\sigma_N/d\Phi_N$ and $\mathcal{H}$, from the effects of soft and
collinear radiation, which are captured entirely by $\Sigma(\{p\},v)$.}
It is in the latter that large logarithms in $v$ arise in the limit $v \ll 1$, 
as illustrated for thrust in Sec.~\ref{sec:thrust-example}, and which requires resummation.


\section{Observable Requirements}
\label{sec:obs-req}

In addition to IRC safety, observables must satisfy a number of further 
conditions in order to be resummed within the \textsc{CAESAR} and 
\textsc{ARES} frameworks. Specifically, the observable $V(p_1, p_2, \dots)$ must satisfy
the following conditions.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Vanishes smoothly in the soft-collinear limit}: when a 
single additional emission of momentum $k$ is soft and collinear to a 
hard leg $\ell$, the observable can admit the parametric form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:general-sc-V}
    V(\{\tilde{p}\},k) \simeq V_{\text{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k) 
    \equiv d_\ell\left(\frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{Q}\right)^a 
    e^{-b_\ell\eta^{(\ell)}} g_\ell(\phi^{(\ell)})\,,
\end{equation}
where $V_{\text{sc}}$ denotes the observable evaluated in the 
soft-collinear limit. Here $\{\tilde{p}\}$ represents the Born momenta 
after recoil from the emission $k$, and $k_t^{(\ell)}$, $\eta^{(\ell)}$, 
$\phi^{(\ell)}$ denote the transverse momentum, rapidity, and azimuthal 
angle of $k$ with respect to the emitting leg $p_\ell$ respectively. 
The scale $Q$ is an arbitrary hard scale of the process, 
serving as a reference normalisation for the transverse momenta. 
The parameters $a$,  $b_\ell$, $d_\ell$, and the function $g_\ell(\phi)$ characterise the 
observable in this limit and can be extracted for each leg, enabling the 
construction of the resummation formula. IRC safety requires $a > 0$ and 
$b_\ell > -a$.

As a concrete example, consider thrust in the soft-collinear limit.
Evaluating $\tau = 1 - T$ when an additional emission is soft and collinear
to leg $\ell$, one finds~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}:
\begin{equation}
	\tau\simeq\tau_\mathrm{sc}=\sum_{\ell=1,2}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}_\ell}\frac{k_{ti}^{(\ell)}}{Q}e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}\,,
\end{equation}
which is of the form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:general-sc-V}, with parameters
\begin{equation}
    a = b_\ell = d_\ell = g_\ell(\phi) = 1\,, \quad \ell = 1, 2\,.
\end{equation}
This particularly simple parameter set gives rise to a correspondingly 
simple resummation structure.


\item \textbf{Recursively IRC (rIRC) safe}: this condition requires that, for an ensemble of soft and collinear emissions, the addition of a further emission that is relatively much softer and/or more collinear should not significantly modify the value of the observable. A formal definition is given in Refs.~\cite{Banfi:2004yd, Banfi:2003je}; we 
summarise the key requirements here. 

Consider momenta $\kappa_i(\zeta_i)$ such that the observable satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rIRCa}
	V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_i(\zeta_i))=\zeta_i\,,
\end{equation}
where in the soft and/or collinear limits $\zeta_i\to0$, the azimuthal angle $\phi_i$ of each emission $\kappa_i(\zeta_i)$ is held fixed. The functions $\kappa_i(\zeta_i)$ may differ for each emission but must satisfy Eq.~\eqref{eq:rIRCa}.The collinear splitting of an existing emission is denoted 
\begin{equation}
	\kappa_i(\zeta)\to\{\kappa_{i_a},\kappa_{i_b}\}(\zeta,\mu)\,,
\end{equation}
\ndp{where $\mu^2$ is the re-scaled invariant mass of the collinear pair}, 
\begin{equation}
	\mu^2=\frac{(\kappa_{i_a}+\kappa_{i_b})^2}{\kappa_{ti}^2}\,,\quad \lim_{\mu\to0}(\kappa_{i_a}+\kappa_{i_b})=\kappa_{i}\,.
\end{equation}

The conditions for rIRC safety are the following:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \label{cond:rIRC-a} The limit 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rIRC-a}
\lim_{\bar{v}\to0}\frac{1}{\bar{v}}V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\bar{v}\zeta_1),\dots,\kappa_m(\bar{v}\zeta_m))\,,
\end{equation}
must be finite and non-zero for any choice of non-zero $\zeta_i$. 
The parameter  $\bar{v}$ is introduced to distinguish it from the observable
value $v$ we wish to resum. This requirement ensures that the
 soft-collinear scaling of the observable is independent of the number
 of emissions.
	
\item The following two conditions must hold:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The addition of a much softer and/or more collinear emission
        must leave the observable unchanged:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:rIRC-b-i}
\lim_{\zeta_{m+1}\to0}\lim_{\bar{v}\to0}\frac{1}{\bar{v}}
V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\bar{v}\zeta_1),\dots,\kappa_m(\bar{v}\zeta_m),
\kappa_{m+1}(\bar{v}\zeta_{m+1}))\\
=\lim_{\bar{v}\to0}\frac{1}{\bar{v}}V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\bar{v}\zeta_1),\dots,
\kappa_m(\bar{v}\zeta_m))\,.
\end{multline}

\item Analogously, a collinear splitting of an existing emission
        must also leave the observable unchanged:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:rIRC-b-ii}
\lim_{\mu\to0}\lim_{\bar{v}\to0}\frac{1}{\bar{v}}
V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\bar{v}\zeta_1),\dots,\{\kappa_{i_a},\kappa_{i_b}\}(\bar{v}\zeta_i,\mu),\dots,\kappa_m(\bar{v}\zeta_m))\\
=\lim_{\bar{v}\to0}\frac{1}{\bar{v}}V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\bar{v}\zeta_1),\dots,
\kappa_m(\bar{v}\zeta_m))\,.
\end{multline}
\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
The rIRC safety condition is precisely what allows the leading-logarithmic
contributions to exponentiate. Observables that violate rIRC safety do not
exponentiate (cf.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:log-counting}); a well-known example is the \textsc{JADE} 3-jet resolution
parameter in $e^+e^-$ collisions~\cite{Catani:1992tm}. 

\item \textbf{Continuously global}: an observable is said to be \emph{global} if it is sensitive to emissions in all regions of phase space, i.e.\ it is non-zero for any emission of an additional parton that is not infinitely soft or collinear. 
Continuous globalness further requires that, for a single soft emission, the parametric dependence of the observable on the emission’s transverse momentum be uniform across phase space, meaning that the parameter $a$ appearing in Eq.~\eqref{eq:general-sc-V} must be the same for all legs. This condition ensures that non-global logarithms~\cite{Dasgupta:2001sh,Dasgupta:2002dc}, which arise when different regions of phase space exhibit different transverse-momentum dependences, do not appear. Non-global logarithms lie beyond the scope of this thesis.
\end{enumerate}
\reference{~\cite{Banfi:2004yd, Banfi:2003je}.}

\section{NLL Resummation}
\label{sec:NLL-resum}

The cumulant for an observable $v$ in a process with hard legs
$\{p\}$ and $n$ real emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cumu-gen}
\Sigma\left(\{p\},v\right)=\mathcal{V}(\{p\})\sum_{n=0}^\infty S(n) \int\left(\prod_{i=1}^n\left[dk_i\right]\right)\mathcal{M}^2\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\,\Theta\!\left(v-V\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{V}(\{p\})$ encodes the virtual corrections to the Born process, normalised to the Born matrix element squared,
    \item $S(n)$ is a symmetry factor,
    \item the Lorentz-invariant phase space in $d=4-2\varepsilon$ dimensions is \ABquestion{Should I express this in
    rapidity instead?}
    \begin{equation}
    [dk] \equiv \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^{d-1}} \delta(k^2) \Theta(k^0)\,,
    \end{equation}
    \item $\mathcal{M}^2(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n)$ is the squared matrix element for $n$ real emissions off the Born configuration $\{\tilde{p}\}$.
\end{itemize}
We now describe the approximations that allow this to be evaluated at NLL
accuracy.

\subsection{Approximations at NLL}
\label{subsec:NLL-approx}

\paragraph{Colour coherence and the physical coupling.}
To simplify the matrix element, we use colour coherence: a soft gluon
emitted at large angles from a set of collinear partons resolves only the
total colour of the collinear ensemble, not the individual colour charges.
This guarantees that emissions widely separated in angle are effectively
independent, so that the squared matrix element for $n$ such emissions
approximately factorises into a product of independent single-emission
contributions.

However, this factorisation is not exact: correlated gluon clusters, 
in which a primary emission subsequently branches into further gluons, 
give rise to corrections to the single-gluon emission probability. At
NLL, these corrections can be absorbed into a redefinition of the
coupling constant, giving rise to the \emph{physical coupling}
$\alpha_s^{\mathrm{phys}}$, defined as the effective strength of soft
radiation when all subsequent branchings from an initial emission are
included~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq}. This is related to the
$\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ coupling $\alpha_s$
by\footnote{This scheme is an extension of the CMW scheme of
Ref.~\cite{Catani:1990rr}.}
 \begin{equation} 
 \alpha_s^{\mathrm{phys}} = \alpha_s\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \right)^nK^{(n)}\right)\,,
\end{equation} 
where the first two coefficients are 
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:alpha-phys-K}
\begin{align}
    K^{(1)} &= C_A\!\left(\frac{67}{18}-\frac{\pi^2}{6}\right)
               - \frac{5}{9}n_f\,, \\
    K^{(2)} &= C_A^2\!\left(\frac{245}{24} - \frac{67}{9}\zeta_2 
               + \frac{11}{6}\zeta_3 + \frac{11}{5}\zeta_2^2\right)
               + C_F n_f\!\left(-\frac{55}{24} + 2\zeta_3\right)
               \notag \\
             &\quad + C_A n_f\!\left(-\frac{209}{108} 
               + \frac{10}{9}\zeta_2 - \frac{7}{3}\zeta_3\right)
               - \frac{n_f^2}{27}
               + \frac{\pi\beta_0}{2}\!\left(
                 C_A\!\left(\frac{808}{27} - 28\zeta_3\right) 
                 - \frac{224}{54}n_f\right)\,.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\paragraph{Factorisation of the matrix element.} 
At NLL accuracy, correlated clusters contribute only through the 
coupling redefinition above. The squared matrix element for $n$ widely 
separated soft and collinear emissions then factorises into a product of 
$n$ independent single-emission matrix elements in the soft-collinear 
limit, $M_{\mathrm{sc}}^2(k_i)$, as defined in 
Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2014sua}. The observable constraint may likewise be 
taken in the soft-collinear limit,  
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vsc}
  \frac{\Vsc{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}
  \equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n)}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
which is guaranteed to exist for all rIRC safe observables by condition~\ref{cond:rIRC-a}.

The virtual factor takes the form
$\mathcal{V}(\{\tilde{p}\})=e^{-\int[dk]M^2(k)}$ at NLL, ensuring
that the inclusive sum over all real and virtual contributions equals

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

To evaluate $\Sigma(\{p\},v)$, we split the exponent in

\end{equation}

\label{subsec:NLL-radiator}

\subsubsection{Soft radiator}

The soft radiator is a sum over colour dipoles $(\ell\bar\ell)$ formed

\begin{equation}

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

\end{equation}
so that at NLL each emission $k_i$ is parametrised by $\{\ell_i,\phi_i^{(\ell_i)},\zeta_i\}$. 
Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:measure-replace} into Eq.~\eqref{eq:Fv-sc} gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Fv-zeta-reg}
  \mathcal{F}(v) = e^{-\int_{\epsilon}^{1}\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}R'\left(\zeta v\right)}
  \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!}
  \int_{\epsilon}^\infty\!\prod_{i=1}^n\left(\sum_{\ell_i}R_{\ell_i}'\left(\zeta_i v\right)\frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)
  \Theta\!\left(v-V_{\mathrm{sc}}\!\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where the cutoff $\epsilon\ll 1$ ensures that emissions with $\zeta_i \ll \epsilon$ cancel between the real and virtual contributions, up to power corrections in $\epsilon$, \ndp{that vanish because of rIRC safety}. 
Approximating $R'(\zeta v) \simeq R'(v)$, thereby neglecting subleading effects due to corrections of relative order $\alpha_s$, the exponent evaluates to $\epsilon^{R'}$,
giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Fv-reg-approx}
    \mathcal{F}(v)
    \simeq \epsilon^{R'}
      \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!}
      \int_{\epsilon}^\infty\!
      \prod_{i=1}^n\!\left(
        \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}
        \sum_{\ell_i}R_{\ell_i}'(v)
        \frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}
      \right)
      \Theta\!\left(v-V_{\mathrm{sc}}\!\left(
        \{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\right)\,.
\end{equation}
\ABquestion{Do we need to discuss why we can just add in the NLL radiator
opposed to the $R(v)$ or is it presumed?}
This can be written compactly as
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:FNLL-formula}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\!\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)
    = \int\!\dZ\,\Theta\!\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where the soft-collinear measure $\dZ$ is defined by
\begin{multline}
    \label{eq:dZ-def}
    \int\!\dZ\,G(\{k_i\}) \\
    \equiv \epsilon^{\RpNLL}\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!}
    \int\prod_{i=1}^n\!\left(\sum_{\ell_i}R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell_i}
    \int_0^\infty\frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}
    \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)
    G(k_1,\dots,k_n)\,,
\end{multline}
for any function $G$ of the emissions. 

\subsection{The NLL Cumulant}
\label{subsec:NLL-cumulant}

Combining the results of the preceding subsections, the master formula
for the NLL cumulant for rIRC safe, continuously global event-shape
observables is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:NLL-cumulant}
\Sigma\left(\{p\},v\right)
= e^{-R_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)}\,
  \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\!\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)\,,
\qquad R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\simeq -v\frac{dR_{\mathrm{NLL}}}{dv}\,.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Numerical implementation.}

The formula in Eq.~\eqref{eq:NLL-cumulant} applies to any continuously
global rIRC safe observable. The observable-dependent inputs -- the
parameters $a$, $b_\ell$, $d_\ell$, $g_\ell(\phi)$ and the function
$V_{\mathrm{sc}}$ -- can be determined numerically for a given
observable due to rIRC safety: it is precisely rIRC safety that
guarantees the soft-collinear limit of the observable exists and can be
evaluated with suitable ensembles of soft and collinear test gluons.
This is implemented in the \textsc{CAESAR} computer
code~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}, in which emissions are generated through a
simplified parton-shower event generator and passed to a subroutine that
evaluates $V_{\mathrm{sc}}$. \textsc{CAESAR} is capable of
verifying rIRC safety directly from the observable's expression in terms
of final-state momenta.

The successor code \textsc{ARES}~\cite{Banfi:2014sua, Banfi:2016zlc,
Banfi:2018mcq, Arpino:2019ozn} extends this framework to NNLL accuracy.
The principal difference from \textsc{CAESAR} lies in the treatment of

entirely numerically, \textsc{ARES} proceeds analytically as far as
possible, resorting to Monte Carlo integration only where necessary. A
comparison between the analytical result and the numerical output of

\subsection{Additive Observables}
\label{subsec:additive}

A particularly important class of observables are those that are 
\emph{additive}: the observable in the soft-collinear limit can be 
expressed as a sum of independent contributions from individual 
emissions,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:additive-def}
    V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n) = \sum_{i=1}^n V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)\,.
\end{equation}
This structure allows $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ to be computed 
analytically. In fact for all additive observables, the result 
$\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$, yields the same result for all such observables.
Thrust is one such example, and the observable studied in 
Chapter~\ref{ch:hhZjet} --- namely one-jettiness --- is also additive, 
as we shall see.

To illustrate how additivity simplifies the calculation, we outline the 
derivation of $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ for additive observables.
Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:additive-def} into
Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-formula} and introducing the rescaled variable
$\zeta_i = V_{\mathrm{sc}}(k_i)/v$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
 \label{eq:FNLL-add-1}
 \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)=\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(\RpNLL)^n}{n!}  \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i}{2\pi}\right) \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\zeta_i\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\RpNLL\equiv \sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell_i}$ since the
only dependence on the hard leg $\ell_i$ in this contribution enters
through the radiator, and where $\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}\to\phi_i$ for
brevity. We next express the Heaviside function using the Mellin
representation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mellin-transform}
    \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\zeta_i\right)=\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
    \frac{d\nu}{2\pi i\nu} e^\nu\prod_{i=1}^ne^{-\nu\zeta_i}\,, \quad c>0\,,
\end{equation}
so that Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-1} becomes,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-2}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)=\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i \nu}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu}\ \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(\RpNLL)^n}{n!}  \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i}{2\pi} e^{-\nu\zeta_i}\right) \,.
\end{equation}
Since the integrand contains no $\phi$-dependence, the azimuthal integrals are trivial. The sum over $n$ exponentiates, giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-3}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)= \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i \nu}e^\nu \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \exp\left[ \RpNLL \int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta} e^{-\nu\zeta}\right] \,.
\end{equation}
The limit $\epsilon\to 0$ becomes finite only after combining the prefactor
$\epsilon^{\RpNLL}$ with the exponent. Writing
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon^{\RpNLL}=\exp\left[-\RpNLL\int_{\epsilon}^1\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\right]\,,
  \end{equation}
and combining with the exponentiated integral in Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-3} gives the 
convergent expression
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-4}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)= \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i \nu}e^\nu \exp\left[ \RpNLL \int_{0}^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta} \left(e^{-\nu\zeta} - \Theta(1-\zeta)\right)\right] \,.
\end{equation}
The remaining $\zeta$-integral evaluates to
\begin{equation}
	 \int_{0}^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta} \left(e^{-\nu\zeta} - \Theta(1-\zeta)\right) = -\gamma_E -\ln\nu\,,
\end{equation}
so that Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-4} reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-5}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)= e^{-\gamma_E \RpNLL} \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu^{1+\RpNLL}}\,.
\end{equation}
Applying the standard inverse Mellin formula 
\begin{equation}
    \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i\,\nu}\,\frac{e^\nu}{\nu^{z}} 
    = \frac{1}{\Gamma(1+z)}\,,
\end{equation}
we arrive at the final result
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-final}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\!\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right) 
    = \frac{e^{-\gamma_E R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}}{\Gamma\!\left(1+R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)}\,,
\end{equation}
in agreement with the numerical results shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:FNLL-CAESAR-comp}.

formalism. This has been done for \textsc{ARES} in the context of $e^+e^-$
annihilation for jet observables, for both dijet and jet-rates and the most 
recent development for event shapes in three-jet production in Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.
In Chapter~\ref{ch:hhZjet}, we present for the first time the \textsc{ARES} method
for hadronic collisions.
