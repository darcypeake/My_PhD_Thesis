\chapter{Resummation}
\label{ch:resum}
In this chapter, we discuss the technique of \emph{resummation}. As outlined in Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}, following the initial hard scattering, the produced coloured partons undergo QCD radiation dominated by soft and collinear emissions. For many observables, these emissions give rise to logarithmically enhanced contributions that become large in the limit where the observable, $v$, is small. In this regime, fixed-order perturbation theory breaks down, indicating the need for dedicated resummation techniques.

Traditionally, resummation for final-state observables was performed in an observable-dependent manner, requiring separate analytical calculations for each case~\cite{Collins:1984kg, Catani:1992ua, Bonciani:2003nt}. The first steps towards a more general approach were taken in~\cite{Banfi:2001bz}, where a systematic semi-numerical framework was developed to achieve NLL accuracy for a broad class of observables. This framework was subsequently extended and implemented in the computer code \textsc{CAESAR} (\emph{Computer Automated Expert Semi-Analytical Resummer})~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}. In this approach, the resummation formula is expressed in terms of a set of observable-dependent parameters, which can be determined automatically and numerically for a given observable. This method has been used to determine the resummation of a broad class of observables, such as event-shape observables and jet-rates.

Subsequently, \textsc{ARES} (\emph{Automated Resummation for Event
Shapes}) was developed to achieve NNLL accuracy for jet observables in
$e^+e^-$ collisions~\cite{Banfi:2014sua, Banfi:2016zlc, Banfi:2018mcq, Arpino:2019ozn}.
In this thesis, we focus on the \textsc{ARES} formalism.

The structure of this chapter is as follows. We begin by introducing event-shape observables and the properties they must satisfy. We then motivate the need for resummation through a fixed-order calculation of a standard event shape, thrust, making explicit the origin and structure of the large logarithms. Finally, we present the NLL and NNLL resummation within the \textsc{ARES} frameworks. This chapter provides the necessary background and context for the original research presented in Chapter~5. \\ \\

\section{Event-shapes}
\label{sec:event-shapes}
Event shapes are IRC safe observables constructed 
from the four-momenta of final-state particles, which characterise the geometry 
of an event by quantifying the distribution of energy-momentum flow in the 
hadronic final state. Unlike jet algorithms (see Sec.~\ref{subsec:jet-algorithm}), which cluster particles into 
discrete jets, event shapes provide a continuous description of the 
event geometry, making them sensitive to QCD radiation. 
They have been widely used in precision studies of QCD, most notably in extractions of the 
strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$~\cite{Becher:2008cf, Abbate:2010xh}. Notable examples include:

\item{\textbf{Thrust}}~\cite{Farhi:1977sg, Brandt:1964sa} is defined as,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust}
	T \equiv \max_{\vec{n}} \frac{\sum_j |\vec{p}_j \cdot \vec{n}|}{\sum_j |\vec{p}_j|}\,, \quad \tau \equiv 1 - T \,,

\item{\textbf{Jet Broadenings}}~\cite{Clavelli:1979md, Rakow:1981qn, Catani:1992jc} quantify the transverse momentum of final-state hadrons relative to the thrust axis by dividing the event into the two hemispheres, $\mathcal{H}_L$ and $\mathcal{H}_R$. For each hemisphere, the broadening is defined as
\begin{equation}
	B_\ell \equiv \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{H}_\ell} |\vec{p}_j \times \vec{n}_T|}{2 \sum_j |\vec{p}_j|}, \quad \ell = 1, 2\,.
\end{equation}
The wide-jet broadening, narrow-jet broadening, and total 
broadening are defined respectively as  
\begin{equation}
    B_W \equiv \max\{B_1, B_2\}\,, \qquad 
    B_N \equiv \min\{B_1, B_2\}\,, \qquad 
    B_T \equiv B_1 + B_2\,.
\end{equation}
\item{\textbf{Jet Masses}}~\cite{Chandramohan:1980ry, Clavelli:1981yh} are defined by computing the invariant mass of each 
hemisphere,
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{\ell}\equiv \frac{\left(\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}_{\ell}}p_j\right)^2}{\left(\sum_jE_j\right)^2}\,,\quad \ell=1, 2\,,
\end{equation}
where $E_j$ is the energy of the particle $j$ in the final-state.
The heavy-jet mass and light-jet mass are defined respectively as
\begin{equation}
	\rho_H\equiv\max\left\{\rho_1,\rho_2\right\}\,,\quad \rho_L\equiv\min\left\{\rho_1,\rho_2\right\}\,.
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

While event shapes offer detailed insight into the structure of hadronic 
final states, they also introduce important theoretical challenges. As exclusive observables (see Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}), their perturbative predictions are affected by the appearance of large logarithmic contributions, which arise from soft and collinear emissions in the limit where the observable approaches zero. To illustrate this explicitly, we now examine how such logarithms emerge in the fixed-order calculation of thrust.\\ \\

\paragraph{Breakdown of the fixed-order expansion.}
The divergence of the fixed-order prediction signals a breakdown of the
perturbative expansion in the region $\tau \to 0$. At higher orders, the
cumulant receives contributions of the form $\alpha_s^n L^m$ with
$m \leq 2n$: each power of $\alpha_s$ corresponds to an additional emission,
and each emission can generate up to two logarithms -- one from the soft
limit and one from the collinear limit. These contributions grow as $\tau \to 0$, 
spoiling the convergence of the perturbative series.

\ndp{This behaviour is particularly problematic because it occurs in the 
region where most of the data lies. The dominance of this region can be 
understood from the underlying event kinematics. At 
leading order, $e^+e^-$ annihilation produces a quark--antiquark pair 
back-to-back, and the dominant soft and collinear radiation does not 
significantly alter the thrust axis, keeping $\tau \approx 0$. Additional 
hard wide-angle radiation, which would drive $\tau$ away from zero, is 
suppressed by powers of $\alpha_s$, so the bulk of events are dijet-like 
with small $\tau$.}

\paragraph{Resummation.}
To restore predictive power in the region $\tau \ll 1$, the perturbative
expansion must be reorganised. When $\alpha_s L \sim 1$, successive terms
in the perturbative series are no longer small, so an
expansion in powers of $\alpha_s$ alone is insufficient. Instead, one
performs a systematic \emph{resummation} of the enhanced contributions to all
orders in $\alpha_s$, yielding an exponentiated form for the cumulant,
\begin{equation}
	\Sigma(\tau) \simeq 
	\exp \Big\{ 
	L g_1(\alpha_s L) 
	+ g_2(\alpha_s L) 
	+ \alpha_s g_3(\alpha_s L) 
	+ \cdots 
	\Big\}.
	\label{eq:resummed-cumulant}
\end{equation}
Each function $g_i$ resums logarithms at successive levels of accuracy: 
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Function & Logarithms resummed & Accuracy \\
\midrule
$g_1(\alpha_s L)$ & $\alpha_s^n L^{n+1}$ & LL \\
$g_2(\alpha_s L)$ & $\alpha_s^n L^{n}$   & NLL \\
$g_3(\alpha_s L)$ & $\alpha_s^n L^{n-1}$ & NNLL \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
where LL denotes leading-logarithmic accuracy and NLL, NNLL denote
next-to-leading and next-to-next-to-leading accuracy respectively.

\ndp{It is worth emphasising the distinction between fixed-order and logarithmic
accuracy. In a fixed-order calculation, improving the accuracy means
computing additional Feynman diagrams at one higher power of $\alpha_s$.
By contrast, each level of logarithmic accuracy already encodes an infinite
set of diagrams --- those contributing at the corresponding logarithmic
order at every power of $\alpha_s$. Advancing from one logarithmic order
to the next therefore requires identifying which physical effects and sets
of diagrams contribute at that order, and computing each of their
contributions at the necessary accuracy.}

Resummation gives rise to the \emph{Sudakov peak} visible in the
experimental data in Fig.~\ref{fig:thrust-data-FO}: a suppression of the
distribution at small $\tau$ driven by the vanishing probability of emitting
no radiation, \ndp{and a turnover toward a peak at moderate $\tau$
where hard radiation becomes significant.} The resummed prediction
leads to significantly improved agreement between theoretical predictions 
and experimental data. \ABquestion{Should I discuss the Sudakov form factor?}

\section{Problem Specification}

We consider events with $n_i$ incoming partons and an arbitrary number $n$ of
final-state particles, whose momenta are denoted $p_1,\dots,p_n$.
At Born level the process involves $N$ hard partons in total -- including
both the $n_i$ incoming legs and the $N - n_i$ outgoing legs -- which define
the underlying hard configuration.
We define an observable $V(p_1,\dots,p_n)$ as a non-negative function of
the final-state momenta. We assume the observable to be IRC safe and to vanish
smoothly as the event approaches an $N$-parton configuration.
Such observables are commonly referred to as $(N\!+\!1)$-jet observables,
where both the outgoing jets and the incoming beam jets are included in the
jet counting.

To select events containing $N$ or more well-separated hard jets, we introduce
a selection procedure. This can be implemented, for instance, through a jet
algorithm that counts the number of jets in the event, or by imposing a cut on
a suitable $N$-jet observable. We encode this requirement in a selection
function $\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)$, defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:H-def}
\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)=
\begin{cases}
1\,, & \text{if the event satisfies the selection criteria,}\\
0\,, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The total $N$-jet cross section for events satisfying the selection is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sigma-H}
  \sigma_{\mathcal{H}}
  = \sum_{n=N-n_i}^{\infty}
    \int d\Phi_n\,\frac{d\sigma_n}{d\Phi_n}\,
    \mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)\,,
\end{equation}
where $d\sigma_n/d\Phi_n$ is the differential cross section for producing
$n$ final-state particles and $d\Phi_n$ is the corresponding $n$-particle
phase space, defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:m-phase-space}. 

We are particularly interested in the cumulative distribution for selected
events in which the observable takes a value smaller than $v$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Sigma-H}
  \Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v)
  = \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}}
    \sum_{n=N-n_i}^{\infty}
    \int d\Phi_n\,\frac{d\sigma_n}{d\Phi_n}\,
    \Theta\!\left(v - V(q_1,\dots,q_n)\right)
    \mathcal{H}(q_1,\dots,q_n)\,.
\end{equation}
In the limit $v\ll 1$, the cumulant $\Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v)$ assumes the
factorised form (cf.\ Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2004yd})
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Sigma-H-fact}
  \Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v)
  \simeq
  \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}}
  \int d\Phi_N\,\frac{d\sigma_N}{d\Phi_N}\,
  \Sigma\!\left(\{p\},v\right)
  \mathcal{H}(p_{n_i+1},\dots,p_N)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\{p\} \equiv \{p_1,\dots,p_N\}$ denotes the full set of $N$ Born
momenta, with $p_1,\dots,p_{n_i}$ the incoming legs and
$p_{n_i+1},\dots,p_N$ the outgoing legs. The term $d\sigma_N/d\Phi_N$ is the
leading-order cross section for the Born configuration, and the integral runs
over the $N$-particle Born phase space $d\Phi_N$. In the limit $v \ll 1$, large logarithms in $v$ appear in $\Sigma(\{p\},v)$, as shown in 
Sec.~\ref{sec:thrust-example} for the observable thrust.

\item The following equality must hold:

\section{NLL Resummation}
\label{sec:NLL-resum}
The cumulant for an observable $v$ in a process with hard legs
$\{p\}$ and $n$ real emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cumu-gen}
\Sigma\left(\{p\},v\right)=\mathcal{V}(\{p\})\sum_{n=0}^\infty S(n) \int\left(\prod_{i=1}^n\left[dk_i\right]\right)\mathcal{M}^2\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\,\Theta\!\left(v-V\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{V}(\{p\})$ encodes the virtual corrections to the Born process, normalised to the Born matrix element squared,
    \item $S(n)$ is a symmetry factor,
    \item the Lorentz-invariant phase space in $d=4-2\varepsilon$ dimensions is
    \begin{equation}
    [dk] \equiv \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^{d-1}} \delta(k^2) \Theta(k^0)\,,
    \end{equation}
    \item $\mathcal{M}^2(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n)$ is the squared matrix element for $n$ real emissions off the Born configuration $\{\tilde{p}\}$.
\end{itemize}
To compute the cumulant at NLL accuracy, several approximations can be made.

To begin, we use the notion of colour coherence to simplify the matrix element squared. 
Colour coherence is the idea that a soft gluon emitted at large angles from a set of 
collinear partons only identifies the total colour of the collinear ensemble, rather than
the individual colour charges. In this capacity, colour coherence guarantees that emissions
widely separated in angle are effectively independent. Gluon-correlated clusters -- in
which a primary emission subsequently branches -- give rise to a
correction to the single-gluon emission probability. At NLL, this correction
can be absorbed into a redefinition of the coupling, giving rise to
the physical coupling $\alpha_s^{\mathrm{phys}}$, which is defined as the strength of the soft radiation such that it includes all subsequent branchings from the initial emission~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq}. This is related to the coupling in the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$-scheme, $\alpha_s$, by\footnote{This scheme is an extension of the CMW scheme introduce in Ref.~\cite{Catani:1990rr}.}: 
 \begin{equation} \alpha_s^{\mathrm{phys}} = \alpha_s\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \right)^nK^{(n)}\right)\,,
\end{equation} 
where the first two coefficients are 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\label{eq:alpha-phys-K}
	&K^{(1)}=C_A\left(\frac{67}{18}-\frac{\pi^2}{6}\right)-\frac{5}{9}n_f\,,\\
	&K^{(2)}=C_A^2\left(\frac{245}{24}-\frac{67}{9}\zeta_2+\frac{11}{6}\zeta_3+\frac{11}{5}\zeta_2^2\right)+C_Fn_f\left(-\frac{55}{24}+2\zeta_3\right)+\\
	&+C_An_f\left(-\frac{209}{108}+\frac{10}{9}\zeta_2-\frac{7}{3}\zeta_3\right)-\frac{1}{27}n_f^2+\frac{\pi\beta_0}{2}\left(C_A\left(\frac{808}{27}-28\zeta_3\right)-\frac{224}{54}n_f\right)\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

At NLL accuracy, correlated clusters contribute only through this
coupling redefinition, and the squared matrix element for $n$ widely
separated soft and collinear emissions factorises into a product of $n$
independent single-emission matrix elements, in the soft-collinear limit $M_{\mathrm{sc}}^2(k_i)$, as defined
in Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2014sua}. The observable constraint $V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},\{k_i\})$ may likewise be taken in this limit for the same reasoning, and is defined as 
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vsc}
  \frac{\Vsc{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}
  \equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n)}{v}\,.
\end{equation}
This soft-collinear limit of the observable is guaranteed to exist for all rIRC safe observables, 


At NLL, the virtual correction, for which there is no real emission in phase space, takes
the form $\mathcal{V}(\{\tilde{p}\})=e^{-\int[dk]M^2(k)}$, ensuring
that the inclusive sum over all real and virtual contributions equals
unity. Both the real and virtual contributions are separately regulated, \ndp{ensuring finite contributions in each}.
Moreover, at NLL, there are $n$-identical gluons and so we can set $S(n)=1/n!$, leading to
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:cumu-factor-more}
    \Sigma\left(\{p\},v\right)=e^{-\int[dk]M^2(k)}\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{n!} \int\prod_{i=1}^n\left[dk_i\right]M_{\mathrm{sc}}^2\left(k_i\right)\,\Theta\!\left(v-V_{\mathrm{sc}}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\right)\,.
\end{equation}
The exponent in Eq.~\eqref{eq:cumu-factor-more} can be split
according to whether an emission's observable value
$V(\{\tilde{p}\},k)$ is larger or smaller than $v$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exp-rad}
  \int[dk]\,M^2(k)
  = \underbrace{\int[dk]\,M^2(k)\,\Theta\!\left(V(\{\tilde{p}\},k)-v\right)}_{\displaystyle R(v)}
  + \int[dk]\,M_{\mathrm{sc}}^2(k)\,\Theta\!\left(v-V_{\mathrm{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where the soft-collinear limit can be taken in the second term due to rIRC safety.

\section{Additive Observables}
\label{subsec:additive-observables}
\add{Change text here.}
In this section, we briefly discuss a class of observables that exhibit an additive structure. Additivity refers to the property that an observable can be expressed as a sum of contributions from individual emissions. For a set of soft and collinear emissions $k_1,\dots , k_n$, this implies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:additive-def}
\Vsc{k_1,\dots , k_n}=\sum_{i=1}^n\Vsc{k_i}\,.
\end{equation}
Additive observables are particularly important because they allow $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ and all contributions appearing in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFNNLL} to be computed analytically. \ABquestion{This is not actually true since $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}$
needs to be computed numerically in the end.}

Thrust is one such example, and we will see in Ch.~\ref{ch:hhZjet} that two 
of the observables studied there --- namely one-jettiness and transverse 
thrust --- are also additive. \ABquestion{Are the other event shapes mentioned 
at the beginning of this section additive?}


To illustrate how this structure simplifies calculations, we outline the derivation of two contributions. Firstly the NLL function, $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$, and then the NNLL soft-collinear contribution, $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{sc}}$.

Importantly, the results for both of these contributions are the same across 
all additive observables, independent of the specific observable under 
consideration. This does not extend to the remaining terms in 
Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFNNLL}, which remain observable-dependent. A complete set of 
equations for additive observables in the three-jet case can be found in 
Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.



\subsection{The NLL Function $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\RpNLL)$}
Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:additive-def} into Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-formula} and introducing the rescaled variable
\begin{equation}
    \zeta_i = \frac{\Vsc{k_i}}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
we obtain
\begin{equation}
 \label{eq:FNLL-add-1}
 \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)=\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(\RpNLL)^n}{n!}  \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i}{2\pi}\right) \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\zeta_i\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\RpNLL\equiv \sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell_i}$, which we can use since the only dependence on hard leg $\ell_i$ in this contribution is on the radiator, and where we have relabelled $\phi_i^{(\ell_i)} \to \phi_i$ for brevity.

We next re-write the Heaviside function using the Mellin transform,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mellin-transform}
    \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\zeta_i\right)=\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
    \frac{d\nu}{2\pi i\nu} e^\nu\prod_{i=1}^ne^{-\nu\zeta_i}\,, \quad c>0\,,
\end{equation}
so that Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-1} becomes,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-2}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)=\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i \nu}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu}\ \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(\RpNLL)^n}{n!}  \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i}{2\pi} e^{-\nu\zeta_i}\right) \,.
\end{equation}
Since the integrand contains no $\phi$-dependence, the azimuthal integrals are trivial. The sum over $n$ exponentiates, giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-3}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)= \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i \nu}e^\nu \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \exp\left[ \RpNLL \int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta} e^{-\nu\zeta}\right] \,.
\end{equation}
The limit $\epsilon\to 0$ becomes finite only after combining the prefactor
$\epsilon^{\RpNLL}$ with the exponent. Writing
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon^{\RpNLL}=\exp\left[-\RpNLL\int_{\epsilon}^1\frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\right]\,,
  \end{equation}
and combining with the exponentiated integral gives rise to the convergent expression
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-4}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)= \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i \nu}e^\nu \exp\left[ \RpNLL \int_{0}^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta} \left(e^{-\nu\zeta} - \Theta(1-\zeta)\right)\right] \,.
\end{equation}
The remaining $\zeta$-integral evaluates to
\begin{equation}
	 \int_{0}^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta} \left(e^{-\nu\zeta} - \Theta(1-\zeta)\right) = -\gamma_E -\ln\nu\,,
\end{equation}
so that Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-4} reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-4}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)= e^{-\gamma_E \RpNLL} \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu^{1+\RpNLL}}\,.
\end{equation}
Applying the standard inverse Mellin formula \ABquestion{Is this the standard inverse Mellin formula?}
\begin{equation}
    \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i\,\nu}\,\frac{e^\nu}{\nu^{z}} 
    = \frac{1}{\Gamma(1+z)}\,,
\end{equation}
we arrive at the final well-known result
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-final}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\!\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right) 
    = \frac{e^{-\gamma_E R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}}{\Gamma\!\left(1+R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)}\,.
\end{equation}
This is in agreement with the result shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:FNLL-CAESAR-comp}.
