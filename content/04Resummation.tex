\chapter{Resummation}
\label{ch:resum}
In this chapter, we discuss the technique of \emph{resummation}. As outlined in Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}, following the initial hard scattering, the produced coloured partons undergo QCD radiation dominated by soft and collinear emissions. For many observables, these emissions give rise to logarithmically enhanced contributions that become large in the limit where the observable, $v$, is small. In this regime, fixed-order perturbation theory breaks down, indicating the need for dedicated resummation techniques.

Traditionally, resummation for final-state observables was performed in an observable-dependent manner, requiring separate analytical calculations for each case. \add{References for observable-dependent resummations?} The first steps towards a more general approach were taken in~\cite{Banfi:2001bz}, where a systematic semi-numerical framework was developed to achieve NLL accuracy for a broad class of observables. This framework was subsequently extended and implemented in the computer code \textsc{CAESAR} (\emph{Computer Automated Expert Semi-Analytical Resummer})~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}. In this approach, the resummation formula is expressed in terms of a set of observable-dependent parameters, which can be determined automatically and numerically for a given observable. This method has been used to determine the resummation of a broad class of observables, such as: event-shape observables and jet-rates.

Subsequently, \textsc{ARES} (\emph{Automated Resummation for Event
Shapes}) was developed to achieve NNLL accuracy for event-shape observables in
$e^+e^-$ collisions. It was first applied to two-jet
observables~\cite{Banfi:2014sua}, before being extended to jet rates
in~\cite{Banfi:2016zlc}. Most recently the resummation for three-jet observables was presented in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. \ABquestion{What about Ref.~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq}?} In this thesis, we focus on the \textsc{ARES} formalism.

The structure of this chapter is as follows. We begin by introducing event-shape observables and the properties they must satisfy. We then motivate the need for resummation through a fixed-order calculation of a standard event shape, thrust, making explicit the origin and structure of the large logarithms. Finally, we present the NLL and NNLL resummation within the \textsc{ARES} frameworks. This chapter provides the necessary background and context for the original research presented in Chapter~5.

\section{Event-shapes}
\label{sec:event-shapes}
Event shapes are IRC safe observables constructed 
from the four-momenta of final-state particles, which characterise the geometry 
of an event by quantifying the distribution of energy-momentum flow in the 
hadronic final state. Unlike jet algorithms (see Sec.~\ref{subsec:jet-algorithm}), which cluster particles into 
discrete jets, event shapes provide a continuous and global description of the 
event geometry, making them sensitive to radiation across the entire phase space. 
They have been widely used in precision studies of QCD, most notably in extractions of the 
strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$~\cite{Becher:2008cf, Abbate:2010xh}. Notable examples include: 
\begin{itemize}
\item{\textbf{Thrust}}~\cite{Farhi:1977sg, Brandt:1964sa} is defined as,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust}
	T \equiv \max_{\vec{n}} \frac{\sum_j |\vec{p}_j \cdot \vec{n}|}{\sum_j |\vec{p}_j|}\,, \quad \tau \equiv 1 - T \,,
\end{equation}
where $\vec{p}_j$ denotes the three-momentum of particle $j$ in the final state, and the unit vector $\vec{n}$ that maximises this quantity is referred to as the \emph{thrust axis}. The thrust axis separates the event into two distinct hemispheres, $\mathcal{H}_L$ and $\mathcal{H}_R$, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:thrust}. It is conventional to work with the variable $\tau$, as defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:thrust}. 
For $e^+e^- \to \mathrm{hadrons}$, thrust provides a measure of how ``pencil-like'' an event is: $\tau \to 0$ corresponds to the Born-level configuration with a perfectly back-to-back $q\bar{q}$ pair, while larger values of $\tau$ indicate more spherically distributed multi-particle final states.

\item{\textbf{Jet Broadenings}}~\cite{Clavelli:1979md, Rakow:1981qn, Catani:1992jc} quantify the transverse momentum of final-state hadrons relative to the thrust axis by dividing the event into the two hemispheres, $\mathcal{H}_L$ and $\mathcal{H}_R$. For each hemisphere, the broadening is defined as
\begin{equation}
	B_\ell \equiv \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{H}_\ell} |\vec{p}_j \times \vec{n}_T|}{2 \sum_j |\vec{p}_j|}, \quad \ell = L, R\,.
\end{equation}
The wide-jet broadening, narrow-jet broadening, and total 
broadening are defined respectively as  
\begin{equation}
    B_w \equiv \max\{B_L, B_R\}\,, \qquad 
    B_n \equiv \min\{B_L, B_R\}\,, \qquad 
    B_t \equiv B_L + B_R\,.
\end{equation}
\item{\textbf{Jet Masses}}~\cite{Chandramohan:1980ry, Clavelli:1981yh} are defined by computing the invariant mass of each 
hemisphere,
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{\ell}\equiv \frac{\left(\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}_{\ell}}p_j\right)^2}{\left(\sum_jE_j\right)^2}\,,\quad \ell=L,R\,,
\end{equation}
where $E_j$ is the energy of the particle $j$ in the final-state.
The heavy-jet mass and light-jet mass are defined respectively as
\begin{equation}
	\rho_h\equiv\max\left\{\rho_L,\rho_R\right\}\,,\quad \rho_l\equiv\min\left\{\rho_L,\rho_R\right\}\,.
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

While event shapes offer detailed insight into the structure of hadronic 
final states, they also introduce important theoretical challenges. As exclusive observables (see Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}), their perturbative predictions are affected by the appearance of large logarithmic contributions, which arise from soft and collinear emissions in the limit where the observable approaches zero. To illustrate this explicitly, we now examine how such logarithms emerge in the fixed-order calculation of thrust.\\ \\

\section{Motivation: Thrust and the Need for Resummation}
\label{sec:thrust-example}
To motivate the need for resummation, we consider a fixed-order calculation of the thrust distribution. At Born level, the process $e^+e^- \to q\bar{q}$ yields
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau} = \delta(\tau),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_0$ denotes the leading-order cross section defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma-LO}.

The first non-trivial contribution arises from the term at $\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s)$ from the real-emission process $e^+e^- \to q\bar{q}g$. For a three-particle final state, thrust is determined by the largest momentum fraction, 
\begin{equation}
T = \max[x_q, x_{\bar{q}}, x_g],
\end{equation}
with $T_\text{min} = 2/3$ (for equal momenta) and $T_\text{max} = 1$. The corresponding differential distribution can be written as
\begin{equation}
	\frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{dT} = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \int dx_q \int dx_{\bar{q}} \frac{x_q^2 + x_{\bar{q}}^2}{(1-x_q)(1-x_{\bar{q}})} \, \Theta\left(x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1\right)\delta\big(T - \max[x_q, x_{\bar{q}}, x_g]\big),
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
	\left(\frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau}\right)_{\text{log-enh}} = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \left[ -3 \left(\frac{1}{\tau}\right)_+ - 4 \left(\frac{\ln \tau}{\tau}\right)_+ \right].
\end{equation}

The cumulative distribution (or cumulant) is the integrated differential distribution, and is defined as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cumulant}
	\Sigma(\tau) 
	= \frac{1}{\sigma_0} \int_0^\tau d\tau' \frac{d\sigma}{d\tau'}\,.
\end{equation}
Evaluating this for the logarithmically enhanced terms gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust-cumulant}
	\Sigma_{\text{log-enh}}(\tau) = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} 
	\left( -2L^2 + 3L \right),
\end{equation}
where $L \equiv \ln(1/\tau)$. All remaining terms in 
Eq.~\eqref{eq:full-thrust-NLO} stay finite as $\tau \to 0$ and do not 
contribute to the logarithmic enhancement.

More generally, the vanishing 
of the distribution as the observable approaches zero is a universal 
feature of event-shape observables. 

The divergence of the fixed-order prediction signals the breakdown of 
the perturbative expansion in this region and must be addressed. At higher orders in perturbation theory, the cumulant receives contributions 
of the form $\alpha_s^n L^m$ with $m \leq 2n$, which grow as $\tau \to 0$ 
and spoil the convergence of the perturbative series.

This behaviour is particularly problematic because it occurs in the 
region where most of the data lies. The dominance of this region can be 
understood from the underlying event kinematics. At 
leading order, $e^+e^-$ annihilation produces a quark--antiquark pair 
back-to-back, and the dominant soft and collinear radiation does not 
significantly alter the thrust axis, keeping $\tau \approx 0$. Additional 
hard wide-angle radiation, which would drive $\tau$ away from zero, is 
suppressed by powers of $\alpha_s$, so the bulk of events are dijet-like 
with small $\tau$. The structure of these logarithms has a clear 
physical origin: each power of $\alpha_s$ corresponds to an additional 
emission, and each emission can generate up to two logarithms, one from the 
soft limit and one from the collinear limit, so the perturbative expansion at 
order $\alpha_s^n$ contains terms with up to $2n$ powers of $L$, i.e.\ 
$\alpha_s^n L^m$ with $m \leq 2n$.

To restore predictive power, the perturbative expansion must be 
reorganised and \emph{resummed}. Rather than expanding purely in powers of 
$\alpha_s$, one performs an expansion in powers of the logarithms $L$,
which becomes essential in the region $\tau \ll 1$ 
where $\alpha_s L \sim 1$. This procedure, known as \emph{resummation}, systematically accounts for these enhanced contributions to all orders in $\alpha_s$, yielding an exponentiated form for the cumulant,
\begin{equation}
	\Sigma(\tau) \simeq 
	\exp \Big\{ 
	L g_1(\alpha_s L) 
	+ g_2(\alpha_s L) 
	+ \alpha_s g_3(\alpha_s L) 
	+ \cdots 
	\Big\}.
	\label{eq:resummed-cumulant}
\end{equation}

Each function $g_i$ resum logarithms at successive logarithmic accuracies, as summarised below:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Function & Logarithms resummed & Accuracy \\
\midrule
$g_1(\alpha_s L)$ & $\alpha_s^n L^{n+1}$ & LL \\
$g_2(\alpha_s L)$ & $\alpha_s^n L^{n}$   & NLL \\
$g_3(\alpha_s L)$ & $\alpha_s^n L^{n-1}$ & NNLL \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
where LL denotes leading logarithmic accuracy, NLL next-to-leading 
logarithmic accuracy, and so on. Resummation gives rise to a turning 
point in the distribution, visible in the experimental data in Fig.~\ref{fig:thrust-data-FO}, 
known as the \emph{Sudakov peak}, and leads to significantly improved 
agreement between theoretical predictions and experimental data.

The appearance of large logarithms and the need for resummation are not 
unique to thrust -- the structure of Eq.~\eqref{eq:resummed-cumulant} 
applies broadly to all IRC safe observables e.g.\ event-shape observables. Given their 
importance in probing QCD dynamics, accurate and systematic resummation 
techniques are essential. A formalism known as \textsc{ARES} provides us with an observable-independent semi-numerical method to resum event shapes, which we will now discuss. \\ \\

\section{Problem Specification}
We consider events with $n_i$ incoming partons and an arbitrary number $N$ of final-state particles, whose momenta are denoted by $q_1, \dots, q_N$. At Born level, the process consists of $n$ hard partons (including both incoming and outgoing legs), which define the underlying hard configuration.

We define an observable $V(q_1, q_2, \dots)$ as a non-negative function of the final-state momenta. We assume that the observable is IRC safe and that it vanishes smoothly in the limit where the event approaches an $n$-jet configuration. Observables with these properties are commonly referred to as $(n+1)$-jet observables, where both the outgoing jets and the incoming beam jets are included in the jet counting.

To select events containing $n$ or more well-separated hard jets, we introduce a selection procedure. \ABquestion{Could I add in something a little more pedological i.e. `we expect expect there to be at least $n$-jets since jets occur from the hard legs within the Born level configuration or is it unnecessary?} This can be implemented, for instance, through a jet algorithm that counts the number of jets in the event, or by imposing a cut on a suitable $n$-jet observable. \begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}(q_1,\dots,q_N)=
\begin{cases}
1\,, & \text{if the event satisfies the selection criteria},\\
0\,, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The total cross section for events passing this selection, i.e.\ the total $n$-jet cross section, is given by
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}=\sum_{N=n-n_i}^\infty\int d\Phi_N\frac{d\sigma_N}{d\Phi_N}\mathcal{H}(q_1,\dots,q_N)\,,
\end{equation}

\Sigma_{\mathcal{H}}(v) \simeq \frac{1}{\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}} \int d\Phi_N \frac{d\sigma_N}{d\Phi_N}\,
\Sigma\big(\{p\},v\big) \,
\mathcal{H}(p_{n_i+1},\dots,p_n) \,,

\section{Observable Requirements}
In addition to IRC safety, there are a few additional properties that observables must posses in order to be resummed by the \textsc{ARES} method. Specifically, the observable $V(p_1, p_2, \dots)$ must satisfy the following conditions: 
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Vanish smoothly}: when a single additional emission of momentum $k$ is soft and collinear to a hard leg $\ell$. In this limit, the observable can be written as
\begin{equation}
	V(\{\tilde{p}\},k)\simeq V_{\text{sc}}(\{\tilde{p}\},k)\equiv d_\ell\left(\frac{k_t^{(\ell)}}{Q}\right)^a e^{-b_\ell\eta^{(\ell)}} g_\ell(\phi^{(\ell)})\,,
	\label{eq:general-sc-V}
\end{equation}
where $V_{\text{sc}}$ denotes the observable evaluated in the soft and collinear limit. The set $\{\tilde{p}\}$ represents the Born momenta after recoil from the emission $k$, whose transverse momentum, rapidity, and azimuthal angle with respect to the emitting leg $p_\ell$ are given by $\left\{k_t^{(\ell)}, \eta^{(\ell)}, \phi^{(\ell)}\right\}$, respectively.  The energy $Q$ is an arbitrary hard scale of the process and serves as a reference normalisation for the transverse momenta. The parameters $a$, $b_\ell$, $d_\ell$, and the function $g_\ell(\phi)$ characterise the observable in this limit. Writing observables in this form allows these parameters to be extracted for each leg, enabling the construction of the resummation formula. 
For example, for soft and/or collinear emissions, the observable thrust can be approximated to~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}:
\begin{equation}
	\tau\simeq\tau_{sc}=\sum_{\ell=1,2}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}_\ell}\frac{k_{ti}^{(\ell)}}{Q}e^{-\eta^{(\ell)}}\,,
\end{equation}
and so for a single soft/collinear emission it has the form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:general-sc-V}, with the parameters
\begin{equation}
a=b_\ell=d_\ell=g_\ell(\phi)=1\,,\quad \ell=1,2\,.
\end{equation}
These parameters produce a remarkably simple resummation structure.
More examples will be shown in Ch.~\ref{ch:hhZjet}. Note that IRC safety implies that $a>0$ and $b_\ell>-a$.

\item \textbf{Recursively IRC (rIRC) safe}: this condition requires that, for an ensemble of soft and collinear emissions, the addition of a further emission that is relatively much softer and/or collinear should not significantly modify the value of the observable. A formal definition of rIRC safety is given in Refs.~\cite{Banfi:2004yd, Banfi:2003je} and is summarised below.

 Consider momenta $\kappa_i(\lambda_i)$ such that the observable satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rIRC1}
	V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_i(\lambda_i))=\lambda_i\,,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_i\to0$ in the soft and/or collinear limits, while the azimuthal angles $\phi_i$ of the emissions $\kappa_i(\lambda_i)$ are fixed. The functions $\kappa_i(\lambda_i)$ may differ for each emission but must satisfy Eq.~\eqref{eq:rIRC1}. The conditions for rIRC safety are then:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item The following limit must be well-defined and non-zero:
	\begin{equation}
	\lim_{\varepsilon\to0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\varepsilon\lambda_1),\dots,\kappa_m(\varepsilon\lambda_m))\,,
	\end{equation}
	which can be interpreted as requiring the soft and collinear scaling of the observable to be independent of the number of emissions.
	\item The following equality must hold:
	\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
	\lim_{\lambda_{m+1}\to0}\lim_{\varepsilon\to0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}V(&\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\varepsilon\lambda_1),\dots,\kappa_m(\varepsilon\lambda_m), \kappa_{m+1}(\varepsilon\lambda_{m+1}))\\
		&= \lim_{\varepsilon\to0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}V(\{\tilde{p}\},\kappa_1(\varepsilon\lambda_1),\dots,\kappa_m(\varepsilon\lambda_m))\,,
	\end{aligned}
	\end{equation}
	showing that the addition of an additional much softer and/or collinear emission must not affect the value of the observable. 
\end{enumerate}
The condition of rIRC safety allows for the leading-logarithmic terms to exponentiate. Observables which are rIRC un-safe e.g.\ the \textsc{JADE} 3-jet resolution parameter in $e^+e^-$ do not exponentiate.

\item \textbf{Continuously global}: an observable is said to be global if it is sensitive to emissions in all regions of phase space, i.e.\ it is non-zero for any emission of an $(n+1)^{\text{th}}$ parton that is not infinitely soft or collinear. \section{Kinematics}
\label{sec:kinematics}

To illustrate the \textsc{ARES} formalism, we consider a concrete example. Since the analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:hhZjet} closely follows the approach of Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}, we present the formalism in the context of three-jet event shapes in $e^+e^-$ collisions, in the near-to-planar limit~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. Wherever possible, we keep the discussion general, and specialise to the three-jet configuration only when necessary.

We consider a generic, continuously global and rIRC-safe observable 
$V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n)$, which vanishes in the three-jet limit,
\begin{equation}
V(\{\tilde{p}\}) = 0\,,
\end{equation}
where $\{\tilde{p}\} = \{\tilde{p}_1,\tilde{p}_2,\tilde{p}_3\}$ denote the recoiled Born-level momenta after the emission of $n$ soft and/or collinear partons $k_1,\dots,k_n$. 

At Born level, a three-jet event consists of a quark, an anti-quark, and a gluon. Their momenta can be parameterised as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3-born-kin}
p_1 = E_1(1,0,0,1)\,,\quad
p_2 = E_2(1,0,\sin\theta_{12},\cos\theta_{12})\,,\quad
p_3 = E_3(1,0,-\sin\theta_{13},\cos\theta_{13})\,,
\end{equation}
where $\theta_{ij}$ denotes the angle between the momenta $p_i$ and $p_j$.

An emission $k$ with respect to to a hard leg $\ell$ can be expressed through a Sudakov decomposition along the corresponding light-like direction $p_\ell$:
\begin{equation}
k^{(\ell)} = x^{(\ell)} p_\ell + \bar{x}^{(\ell)} \bar{p}_\ell + k_\perp^{(\ell)}\,,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{p}_\ell = (E_\ell,-\vec{p}_\ell)$, and $x^{(\ell)}$, $\bar{x}^{(\ell)}$ are longitudinal momentum fractions.  \ABquestion{checking that $x$'s are longitudinal momentum since they aren't defined in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}?} The  two-dimensional space-like vector $k_\perp^{(\ell)}$ lies in the plane orthogonal to $\vec{p}_\ell$ and has magnitude
\begin{equation}
-\left(k_\perp^{(\ell)}\right)^2
= \frac{(2p_\ell \cdot k)(2k \cdot \bar{p}_\ell)}{2p_\ell \cdot \bar{p}_\ell}
\equiv \left(k_t^{(\ell)}\right)^2\,,
\end{equation}
where $k_t^{(\ell)}$ is the transverse momentum with respect to leg $\ell$. The corresponding rapidity is defined as
\begin{equation}
\eta^{(\ell)} = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{x^{(\ell)}}{\bar{x}^{(\ell)}}\,.
\end{equation}

It is often convenient to describe emissions using dipole kinematics. In this case, the emission $k$ can be conveniently described using Sudakov variables such that it is decomposed along a pair of light-like momenta $(p_i,p_j)$ forming the $(ij)$ dipole:
\begin{equation}
k^{(ij)}=z^{(i)}p_i+z^{(j)}p_j+\kappa^{(ij)}\cos\phi^{(ij)}n_{\mathrm{in}}^{(ij)}+\kappa^{(ij)}\sin\phi^{(ij)}n_{\mathrm{out}}^{(ij)}\,.
\label{eq:Sudakov-decomp}
\end{equation}
where $z^{(i)}$ and $z^{(j)}$ are energy fractions. The invariant transverse momentum $\kappa^{(ij)}$ is given by 
\begin{equation}
\left(\kappa^{(ij)}\right)^2=\frac{(2p_i\cdot k)(2p_j\cdot k)}{(2p_i\cdot p_j)}\,.
\end{equation}

The unit vectors $n_{\mathrm{in}}^{(ij)}$ and $n_{\mathrm{out}}^{(ij)}$ define the directions in and orthogonal to the dipole plane:
\begin{equation}
n_{\mathrm{in}}^{(ij)}=\left(\cot\frac{\theta_{ij}}{2},\frac{\vec{n}_i+\vec{n}_j}{\sin\theta_{ij}}\right)\,,\quad n_{\mathrm{out}}^{(ij)}=\left(0,\frac{\vec{n}_i\times\vec{n}_j}{\sin\theta_{ij}}\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\vec{n}_\ell\equiv\vec{p}_\ell/E_\ell$ for $\ell=i,j$. 

The rapidity of the emission with respect to the dipole is defined as
\begin{equation}
\eta^{(ij)} = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{z^{(i)}}{z^{(j)}}\,.
\end{equation}
In the limit where the emission becomes collinear to one of the dipole legs, this quantity is related to the single-leg rapidity via
\begin{equation}
\eta^{(i)} \simeq \eta^{(ij)} + \ln\frac{2E_i}{Q_{ij}}\,,\quad 
\eta^{(j)} \simeq -\eta^{(ij)} + \ln\frac{2E_j}{Q_{ij}}\,,\quad 
Q_{ij}^2 = 2(p_i \cdot p_j)\,.
\end{equation}
In the same collinear limit, the dipole transverse momentum reduces to the transverse momentum with respect to the emitting leg, $\kappa^{(ij)} \to k_t^{(\ell)}$.

\section{NLL Resummation}
\label{sec:NLL-resum}
In the near-to-planar limit, $v \ll 1$, the cumulant for a three-jet observable can be written in factorised form as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3jet-cumu-gen}
\Sigma\left(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v\right)=\mathcal{V}(\{p\})\sum_{n=0}^\infty S(n) \int\left(\sum_{i=1}^n\left[dk_i\right]\right)|\mathcal{M}\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)|^2\,\Theta\!\left(v-V\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{V}(\{p\})$ encodes the virtual corrections to the Born process, normalised to the Born matrix element squared\,,
    \item $S(n)$ is a symmetry factor e.g., $1/n!$ for $n$ identical gluons\,,
    \item The Lorentz-invariant phase space in $d=4-2\varepsilon$ dimensions is
    \begin{equation}
    [dk] \equiv \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^{d-1}} \delta(k^2) \Theta(k^0)\,,
    \end{equation}
    \item $|\mathcal{M}(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n)|^2$ is the squared matrix element for $n$ real emissions off the three-particle Born configuration $\{\tilde{p}\}$\,.
\end{itemize}

This expression provides the starting point for constructing the cumulant at NLL accuracy. At this level,  only soft emissions widely separated in angle contribute to the cumulant, such that the matrix-element squared can be factorised into the product of single-emission squared matrix-elements, radiated according to the physical coupling~\cite{Banfi:2018mcq}. 

This coupling, $\alpha_s^{\mathrm{phys}}$, is defined as the strength of the soft radiation such that it includes all subsequent branchings from the initial emission. It is related to the strong coupling in the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$-scheme, $\alpha_s$, by\footnote{Please note that the first interpretation of this factorisation was present in Ref.~\cite{Catani:1990rr}, in which the CMW coupling, $\alpha_s^{\mathrm{CMW}}$ was introduced through: $\alpha_s^{\mathrm{CMW}} = \alpha_s\left(1+\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} K^{(1)}\right)+\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^3)$.}:
 \begin{equation} \alpha_s^{\mathrm{phys}} = \alpha_s\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \right)^nK^{(n)}\right)\,,
\end{equation} 
where
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	&K^{(1)}=C_A\left(\frac{67}{18}-\frac{\pi^2}{6}\right)-\frac{5}{9}n_f\,,\\
	&K^{(2)}=C_A^2\left(\frac{245}{24}-\frac{67}{9}\zeta_2+\frac{11}{6}\zeta_3+\frac{11}{5}\zeta_2^2\right)+C_Fn_f\left(-\frac{55}{24}+2\zeta_3\right)+\\
	&+C_An_f\left(-\frac{209}{108}+\frac{10}{9}\zeta_2-\frac{7}{3}\zeta_3\right)-\frac{1}{27}n_f^2+\frac{\pi\beta_0}{2}\left(C_A\left(\frac{808}{27}-28\zeta_3\right)-\frac{224}{54}n_f\right)\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Separating emissions into \emph{resolved} and \emph{unresolved} regions, and combining them with the corresponding virtual corrections, the cumulant can be written in a multiplicative form,
\begin{equation} 
\label{eq:NLL-cumulant}
\Sigma\left(\{p_1,p_2,p_3\},v\right)=e^{-R_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)}\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)\,,\quad R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\simeq -v\frac{dR_{\mathrm{NLL}}}{dv}\,,
\end{equation}
which is general and not specific to the three-jet case. We now discuss the two components that enter this expression.

 \subsection{The NLL Radiator}
 The Sudakov exponent $R_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:NLL-cumulant}, referred to as the NLL radiator, encodes the probability of no emissions with $V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)>v$. It arises from the combination of virtual corrections with \emph{unresolved} real emissions, defined by $V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)<\epsilon v$, where $\epsilon$ is a cutoff.
 
The radiator is made up of contributions arising from the soft \emph{and} collinear region in the form of double logarithms, as well as single logarithms coming from virtual corrections from soft large-angle and hard-collinear regions. The derivative $R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ only includes logarithmic contributions up to NLL accuracy. 

For a three-jet configuration, the NLL radiator is a sum over the dipoles $(q\bar q)$, $(qg)$ and $(g\bar q)$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:NLL-radiator}
\begin{split}
	R_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)=\sum_{(ij)\in\{(q\bar{q}), (qg), (g\bar{q})\}}C_{(ij)}\left(\sum_{\ell=i,j}\left[r_\ell(L)+r'_\ell(L)\left(\langle \ln(d_\ell g_\ell)\rangle - b_\ell\ln\frac{2E_\ell}{Q}\right) \right. \right. & \\
	\left. \left. +B_\ell T\left(\frac{L}{a+b_\ell}\right)\right] +2\ln\frac{Q_{ij}}{Q}T\left(\frac{L}{a}\right)\right)\,,
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $Q_{ij}^2=2p_i\!\cdot p_j$ and $C_{(ij)}$ is the colour factor of the dipole,
\begin{equation}
C_{(ij)}=
\begin{cases}
2C_F-C_A\,, & q\bar{q}\,,\\
C_A\,,& qg,\,g\bar{q}\,.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The functions $r_\ell(L), r'_\ell(L)$ and $T(L)$ are integrals over the real emission matrix elements and explicit expressions can be found in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. The azimuthal average is
\begin{equation}
\langle f \rangle \equiv \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}f\left(\phi\right)\,.
\end{equation}

 \subsection{The NLL Function}
 \label{subsec:FNLL}
To define the NLL-function, $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:NLL-cumulant}, each emission $k_i$ is parametrised by $\{\ell_i,\phi_i^{(\ell_i)},\zeta_i\}$, 
where; $\ell_i$ denotes the leg to which the emission is collinear, $\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}$ is the azimuthal angle with respect to that leg, and $\zeta_i \equiv \frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(k_i)}{v}$. For event shapes, the dependence on emission rapidity can be integrated analytically, which in turn can yield a Jacobian factor $R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell} \equiv 2 C_\ell\, r'_\ell(L)$, with $C_\ell=C_F$ for quark legs and $C_\ell=C_A$ for gluon legs. With this parametrisation,
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:FNLL-formula}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)= \int\! \dZ \,\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\,,
  \end{equation}
where the soft-collinear approximation to the observable is defined as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vsc}
  \frac{\Vsc{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{V({\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n})}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to approximation of the observable when all emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ are simultaneously soft and collinear. Recursive IRC safety guarantees that this limit exists.
Moreover, the soft-collinear measure $\dZ$ is defined as
  \begin{multline}
    \label{eq:dZ-def}
    \int\! \dZ \,G(\{k_i\}) \\ \equiv \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!} \int \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)G(k_1,\dots,k_n)\,.
  \end{multline}
As before, the term with the Sudakov exponent, $\epsilon^{R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}$, arises from \emph{unresolved} emissions with $\zeta_i<\epsilon$ together with virtual corrections. The remaining terms occur due to rIRC safety, where emissions contributing to 
$V\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)$ are restricted to the region where $\epsilon v\lesssim
 V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)\sim v$ with $\epsilon \ll v$. Together, with the corresponding virtual corrections, they build up $ \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$.
 
 It is instructive to briefly comment on the \textsc{CAESAR} formalism. This framework, developed prior to \textsc{ARES}, provides an automated approach to NLL resummation. In \textsc{CAESAR}, the soft-collinear approximation to the observable (cf.\ Eq.~\ref{eq:general-sc-V}) is determined numerically, allowing both $R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ and $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ to be computed automatically. In contrast, \textsc{ARES} performs the resummation analytically as far as possible, resorting to numerical Monte Carlo methods only when necessary. Furthermore, while \textsc{CAESAR} is limited to NLL accuracy, \textsc{ARES} has been extended to achieve NNLL precision for a range of observables.

\subsection{The NNLL Radiator}

The NNLL radiator, $R_{\mathrm{NNLL}}(v)$ can be broken into two pieces, the soft radiator $R_{\mathrm{s}}(v)$ and the hard-collinear radiator $R_{\mathrm{hc}}(v)$:
\begin{equation}
	R_{\mathrm{NNLL}}(v)= R_{\mathrm{s}}(v)+R_{\mathrm{hc}}(v)\,.
\end{equation}

The soft radiator is defined as 
\begin{equation}
	R_{\mathrm{s}}(v)=\sum_{(ij)}C_{(ij)}\sum_{\ell\in(ij)}\mathcal{R}_{\ell}^{(ij)}(v)\,,
\end{equation}
where for each dipole $\mathcal{R}_{\ell}^{(ij)}(v)$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:RS-ell}
	\mathcal{R}_{\ell}^{(ij)}(v)=\mathcal{R}_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}(v)+\delta R_{\ell}^{(ij)}(v)\,.
\end{equation}
The $\mathcal{R}_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}(v)$ is calculated to be
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{R}_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}(v)=R_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}(v)+\left(R_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}\right)'(v)\Big\langle\ln\left(d_\ell^{(ij)}g_\ell(\phi)\right)\Big\rangle+\frac{1}{2}\left(R_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}\right)''(v)\Big\langle\ln^2\left(d_\ell^{(ij)}g_\ell(\phi)\right)\Big\rangle+\dots\,,
\end{equation}
where 
\begin{equation}
	R_{\ell,0}^{(ij)}(v)=-\frac{\lambda}{\alpha_s(Q)\beta_0}g_1^{(\ell)}(\lambda)-g_2^{(\ell)}(\lambda)-\mathfrak{g}_2^{(\ell)}(\lambda,Q_{ij})-\frac{\alpha_s(Q)}{\pi}\left(g_3^{(\ell)}+\mathfrak{g}_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda,Q_{ij})\right)\,,
\end{equation}
and 
\begin{equation}
	d_{\ell}^{(ij)}=d_\ell\left(\frac{Q_{ij}}{Q}\right)^a\left(\frac{Q_{ij}}{2E_\ell}\right)^{b_\ell}\,.
\end{equation}
The second term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:RS-ell} is defined to be
\begin{equation}
	\delta R_{\ell}^{(ij)}(v)=-\frac{\alpha_s(Q)}{\pi}\delta g_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda)\,.
\end{equation}
 The functions $g_1^{(\ell)}(\lambda), g_2^{(\ell)}(\lambda), \mathfrak{g}_2^{(\ell)}(\lambda,Q_{ij}),g_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda) \mathfrak{g}_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda,Q_{ij}),$ and $\delta g_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda)$ are defined in Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.
The hard-collinear radiator is defined as
\begin{equation}
	R_{\mathrm{hc}}(v)=\sum_{\ell}R_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}(v)\,,
\end{equation}
where 
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{hc},\ell}(v)=-h_2^{(\ell)}(\lambda)-\frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}h_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda)\,.
\end{equation}
The functions $h_2^{(\ell)}(\lambda),h_3^{(\ell)}(\lambda)$ can be found in Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}.

We will outline the remaining terms by splitting the NNLL cumulant in Eq.~\eqref{eq:Sigma-NNLL-fs-ee} into two pieces: the hard-collinear cumulant $\Sigma_{\mathrm{hc}}$ and the soft-collinear cumulant $\Sigma_{\mathrm{sc}}$. By separating the two, we will be able to identify physically how the contributions arise to form the NNLL cumulative distribution. We will discuss these two cumulants in the following.

 \begin{multline}

\end{equation}

\subsection{Additive Observables}
\label{subsec:additive-observables}
In this section, we briefly discuss a class of observables that exhibit an additive structure. Additivity refers to the property that an observable can be expressed as a sum of contributions from individual emissions. For a set of soft and collinear emissions $k_1,\dots , k_n$, this implies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:additive-def}
\Vsc{k_1,\dots , k_n}=\sum_{i=1}^n\Vsc{k_i}\,.
\end{equation}
Additive observables are particularly important because they allow $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ and all contributions appearing in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFNNLL} to be computed analytically. 

To illustrate how this structure simplifies calculations, we outline the derivation of $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ for a generic additive observable. Although this contribution has already been discussed for thrust in Sec.~\ref{subsec:FNLL}, the result derived below holds universally for all additive observables. This universality does not extend to the terms in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFNNLL}, which remain observable dependent. A set of equations for additive observables in the three-jet case can be found in Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. We will see in Ch.~\ref{ch:hhZjet} that two of the observables studied there, namely one-jettiness and transverse thrust, are also additive. We now proceed with the explicit derivation.

Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:additive-def} into Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-formula}, we obtain
  \begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FNLL-add-1}
  \begin{aligned}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)=&\; \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!} \int \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)\times\\
    & \times \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\,.
    \end{aligned}
  \end{equation}

We now introduce the rescaled variable, which is ubiquitous in the \textsc{ARES} formalism,
\begin{equation}
    \zeta_i = \frac{\Vsc{k_i}}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
such that Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-1} becomes
  \begin{equation}
   \label{eq:FNLL-add-2}
  \begin{aligned}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)=&\; \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!} \int \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)\times\\
    & \times \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\zeta_i\right)\,.
    \end{aligned}
  \end{equation}
  
We employ the Mellin representation of the Heaviside function,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mellin-transform}
    \Theta\left(1-\sum_{i=1}^n\zeta_i\right)=\int_{\mathcal{C}}
    \frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu}\prod_{i=1}^ne^{-\nu\zeta_i}\,,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}$ denotes a contour in the complex $\nu$-plane running from $c-i\infty$ to $c+i\infty$.

Substituting this into Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-2}, we obtain
  \begin{equation}
   \label{eq:FNLL-add-3}
  \begin{aligned}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)=&\; \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \int_{\mathcal{C}}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu}\ \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!} \int \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i} e^{-\nu\zeta_i} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right) \,.
            \end{aligned}
  \end{equation}

Since the observable is additive, each emission contributes an identical factor. The single-emission integral is therefore
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}(\nu,\epsilon)\equiv\sum_{\ell}R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell}
    \int_\epsilon^\infty \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta}\,e^{-\nu\zeta}
    \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi^{(\ell)}}{2\pi}
    = R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\,\Gamma(0,\nu\epsilon)\,,
\end{equation}
where $R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\equiv\sum_\ell R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell}$.

The sum over emissions exponentiates, yielding
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:FNLL-add-4}
  \begin{aligned}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)=&\; \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \int_{\mathcal{C}}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\frac{e^\nu}{\nu} e^{R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\Gamma\left[0,\nu\epsilon\right]}\,.
            \end{aligned}
  \end{equation}
  
In the limit $\epsilon \to 0$, we use the small-argument expansion
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma(0,\nu\epsilon)=-\ln(\nu\epsilon)-\gamma_E+\mathcal{O}(\nu\epsilon)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_E$ is the Euler--Mascheroni constant. Substituting this into Eq.~\eqref{eq:FNLL-add-4}, gives rise to 
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right) = e^{-\gamma_E R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}
    \int_{\mathcal{C}}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\,
    \nu^{-1-R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}\,e^{\nu}\,.
\end{equation}

Using the identity
\begin{equation}
\int_\mathcal{C}\frac{d\nu}{2\pi i}\nu^{-z}e^\nu = \frac{1}{\Gamma(z)}\,,
\end{equation}
we obtain the final result
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FNLL-add-final}
\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)
= \frac{e^{-\gamma_E R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}}{\Gamma\left(1+R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}\right)}\,,
\end{equation}
which agrees with the result shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:FNLL-CAESAR-comp}.
