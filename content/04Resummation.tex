As discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}, after the initial hard scattering of an event, the produced coloured partons undergo QCD radiation dominated by soft and collinear emissions. For many observables, these emissions give rise to logarithmically enhanced terms, which become significantly large when the observable, $v$, is small. This can ruin fixed-order perturbation theory and therefore indicates that dedicated techniques are necessary in this region. There are several approaches that exist such as parton showers and effective field theories. In this chapter we focus on a technique known as \emph{resummation}. In particular, we discuss the semi-numerical resummation frameworks known as: \textsc{CAESAR} (\emph{Computer Automated Expert Semi-Analytical Resummer})~\cite{Banfi:2004yd} and \textsc{ARES} (\emph{Automated Resummation for Event Shapes})~\cite{Banfi:2014sua}. Both provide systematic methods to resum large logarithms for a class of IRC safe observables known as event shapes, though they differ in their logarithmic accuracy as will become clear later. \\ \\

We then provide the motivation for resummation through a fixed-order calculation of a specific event shape known as thrust, which makes explicit the origin and structure of the large logarithms. \section{Event-shapes}
\label{sec:event-shapes}
Event shapes are infrared and collinear (IRC) safe observables constructed from the 
four-momenta of final-state particles. They characterise the geometry of an event by 
quantifying the distribution of its hadronic energy-momentum flow, and have been 
extensively used in precision QCD studies. \add{Discussion of event shapes vs. jet algorithms} Notable examples include:
\begin{itemize}
\item{\textbf{Thrust}}~\cite{Farhi:1977sg, Brandt:1964sa} is defined as,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust}
	T \equiv \max_{\vec{n}} \frac{\sum_j |\vec{p}_j \cdot \vec{n}|}{\sum_j |\vec{p}_j|}\,, \quad \tau \equiv 1 - T \,,
\end{equation}
where $\vec{p}_j$ denotes the three-momentum of particle $j$ in the final state, and the unit vector $\vec{n}$ that maximises this quantity is referred to as the \emph{thrust axis}. It is conventional to work with the variable $\tau$, as defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:thrust}. 
For $e^+e^- \to \mathrm{hadrons}$, thrust provides a measure of how ``pencil-like'' an event is: $\tau \to 0$ corresponds to the Born-level configuration with a perfectly back-to-back $q\bar{q}$ pair, while larger values of $\tau$ indicate more spherically distributed multi-particle final states, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:thrust}. Thrust is among the most well-studied event shapes in 
$e^+e^-$ collisions, having been used extensively at LEP for precise determinations 
of $\alpha_s$~\cite{Becher:2008cf, Abbate:2010xh}.

\item{\textbf{Jet Broadenings}} are defined by dividing the event into two hemispheres 
$\mathcal{H}_L$ and $\mathcal{H}_R$ with respect to the thrust axis $\vec{n}_T$, and 
computing
\begin{equation}
	B_\ell\equiv\frac{\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}_{\ell}}|\vec{p}_i\times\vec{n}_T|}{2\sum_i|\vec{p}_i|}\,,\quad \ell=L,R\,.
\end{equation}
From these, one defines the wide-jet broadening, narrow-jet broadening, and total 
broadening respectively as
\begin{equation}
    B_W \equiv \max\{B_L, B_R\}\,, \qquad 
    B_N \equiv \min\{B_L, B_R\}\,, \qquad 
    B_T \equiv B_L + B_R\,.
\end{equation}
\item{\textbf{Jet Masses}} are defined by computing the invariant mass of each 
hemisphere,
\begin{equation}
	\rho_{\ell}\equiv \frac{\left(\sum_{i\in\mathcal{H}_{\ell}}p_i\right)^2}{\left(\sum_iE_i\right)^2}\,,\quad \ell=L,R\,.
\end{equation}
They heavy-jet mass and light-jet mass are defined respectively as
\begin{equation}
	\rho_H\equiv\max\left\{\rho_L,\rho_R\right\}\,,\quad \rho_L\equiv\min\left\{\rho_L,\rho_R\right\}\,.
\end{equation}

\end{itemize}
While event shapes provide detailed information on the distribution of energy and momentum, offering insight into features such as jet substructure and important QCD dynamics, they also have issues in the form of large logarithms, (see Sec.~\ref{sec:event-shapes} and Sec.~\ref{subsec:QCD-ev}). Before addressing how one might go about solving these, it is important to see these logarithms arise in fixed-order calculations. We will show this through a worked example of the event shape, thrust.\ \\

\end{equation}
where $\sigma_0$ denotes the LO cross-section given in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma-LO}. \\ \\

\begin{equation}
	\frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau} = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \left\{ \frac{3(1+\tau)(3\tau-1)}{\tau} + \frac{(4+6\tau(\tau-1))\ln\frac{1-2\tau}{\tau}}{\tau(\tau-1)} \right\}.

    & + \left[ 3(1+\tau)(3\tau-1) + \frac{(4+6\tau(\tau-1))\ln(1-2\tau)}{1-\tau}\right] \left( \frac{1}{\tau} \right)_+  \\

\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Neglecting the $\delta$-function contributions, the terms that become large in the $\tau \to 0$ limit may be referred to as \emph{logarithmically enhanced terms},
\begin{equation}
	\left(\frac{1}{\sigma_0}\frac{d\sigma}{d\tau}\right)_{\text{log-enh}} = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} \left[ -3 \left(\frac{1}{\tau}\right)_+ - 4 \left(\frac{\ln \tau}{\tau}\right)_+ \right].
\end{equation}
The integrated distribution i.e. the cumulative distribution (cumulant) is given by the
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cumulant}
	\Sigma(\tau) 
	= \frac{1}{\sigma_0} \int_0^\tau d\tau' \frac{d\sigma}{d\tau'}\,.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the cumulant for these logarithmically enhanced terms is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:thrust-cumulant}
	\Sigma_{\text{log-enh}}(\tau) = C_F \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} 
	\left( -2L^2 + 3L \right),
\end{equation}
where $L \equiv \ln(1/\tau)$. All remaining terms in Eq.~\eqref{eq:full-thrust-NLO} would remain finite in the cumulant as $\tau \to 0$ and do not contribute to the logarithmic enhancement. \\ \\

In essence, these spoil the convergence of the perturbative series in the small-$\tau$ region, where the bulk of the data lies. \\ \\
To restore predictive power, the perturbative expansion must therefore be reorganised and \emph{resummed}. Rather than expanding solely in powers of the strong coupling $\alpha_s$, one performs an expansion in powers of the logarithms $L$. This reorganisation becomes essential in the region $\tau \ll 1$, where the logarithms are large and $\alpha_s L \sim 1$. This procedure, known as \emph{resummation}, systematically accounts for these enhanced contributions to all orders in $\alpha_s$, yielding an exponentiated form for the cumulant,
\begin{equation}
	\Sigma(\tau) \simeq 
	\exp \Big\{ 
	L g_1(\alpha_s L) 
	+ g_2(\alpha_s L) 
	+ \alpha_s g_3(\alpha_s L) 
	+ \cdots 
	\Big\},
	\label{eq:resummed-cummulant}
\end{equation}
where the functions $g_i$ resum logarithms at successive logarithmic accuracies. The leading-logarithmic contributions $\alpha_s^n L^{n+1}$ are contained in $g_1(\alpha_s L) $, next-to-leading logarithms $\alpha_s^n L^{n}$ in $g_2(\alpha_s L) $, and so on.  \\ \\

Traditionally, NLL resummation for final-state observables was performed in an observable-dependent manner, with separate analytical calculations required for each individual case. The first steps towards a more general approach were taken in~\cite{Banfi:2001bz}, where a systematic semi-numerical framework was developed to perform NLL resummation for a broad class of observables. This framework was later extended and implemented in a computer code, known as \textsc{CAESAR}~\cite{Banfi:2004yd}. In this approach, the resummation formula is written in terms of a set of observable-dependent parameters, which can be determined automatically by the code for an arbitrary observable. 
To begin, we must discuss the conditions an observable must satisfy in order for this resummation approach to be valid.

\subsubsection{Selection of three-jet events}
We consider an observable that satisfies the requirements outlined previously, namely that it is rIRC safe, continuously global, and vanishes in the three-jet limit,

To study event shapes in the near-to-planar region, we require a procedure that selects hadronic events containing three or more well-separated hard jets. This can be achieved, for example, through a jet algorithm that counts the number of jets in the event, or by imposing a cut on some secondary, two-jet observable. We can represent this constraint through a selection function $\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)$, defined in terms of the hadron momenta $p_1,\dots,p_n$, such that
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)=
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if the event satisfies the selection criteria},\\
0 & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The total cross section for events satisfying this selection - i.e. the total 3-jet cross section is defined by
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{\mathcal{H}}\equiv\sum_{n=3}^\infty\int d\Phi_n\frac{d\sigma_n}{d\Phi_n}\mathcal{H}(p_1,\dots,p_n)\,,
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}

At NLL, soft-collinear partons emitted independently off the hard legs, along with their virtual corrections, contribute to the cumulant.  \subsubsection{The NLL Radiator}
 The Sudakov exponent $R_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)$ is referred to as the NLL radiator. This encodes the probability of no emissions with $V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)>v$ and is formed by the combination of virtual corrections with \emph{unresolved} emissions i.e. $V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)<\epsilon v$, where $\epsilon$ is a cut off. The radiator is made up of contributions arising from the soft \emph{and} collinear region in the form of double logarithms, as well as single logarithms coming from virtual corrections from soft large-angle and hard-collinear regions. Note that the derivative $R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ only includes logarithmic contributions up to NLL accuracy. For a three-jet configuration, the NLL radiator is a sum over the dipoles $(q\bar q)$, $(qg)$ and $(g\bar q)$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:NLL-radiator}
\begin{split}
	R_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)=\sum_{(ij)\in\{(q\bar{q}), (qg), (g\bar{q})\}}C_{(ij)}\left(\sum_{\ell=i,j}\left[r_\ell(L)+r'_\ell(L)\left(\langle \ln(d_\ell g_\ell)\rangle - b_\ell\ln\frac{2E_\ell}{Q}\right) \right. \right. & \\
	\left. \left. +B_\ell T\left(\frac{L}{a+b_\ell}\right)\right] +2\ln\frac{Q_{ij}}{Q}T\left(\frac{L}{a}\right)\right)\,,
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $Q_{ij}^2=2p_i\!\cdot p_j$ and $C_{(ij)}$ is the colour factor of the dipole,
\begin{equation}
C_{(ij)}=
\begin{cases}
2C_F-C_A\,, & q\bar{q}\,,\\
C_A\,,& qg,\,g\bar{q}\,.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The functions $r_\ell(L), r'_\ell(L)$ and $T(L)$ are integrals over the real emission matrix elements and explicit expressions can be found in~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. Moreover, the term $\langle \ln(d_\ell g_\ell)\rangle$ is the azimuthal average, which for a function $f(\phi)$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
\langle f \rangle \equiv \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{2\pi}f\left(\phi\right)\,.
\end{equation}

 \subsubsection{The NLL Function}
To define $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$, each emission $k_i$ is parametrised by $\{\ell_i,\phi_i^{(\ell_i)},\zeta_i\}$, 
where; $\ell_i$ denotes the leg to which the emission is collinear, $\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}$ is the azimuthal angle with respect to that leg, and $\zeta_i \equiv \frac{V_{\mathrm{sc}}(k_i)}{v}$. For event shapes, the dependence on emission rapidity can be integrated analytically, which in turn can yield a Jacobian factor $R'_{\mathrm{NLL},\ell} \equiv 2 C_\ell\, r'_\ell(L)$, with $C_\ell=C_F$ for quark legs and $C_\ell=C_A$ for gluon legs. With this parametrisation,
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:FNLL-formula}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}\left(R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}(v)\right)= \int\! \dZ \,\Theta\left(1-\frac{\Vsc{\{k_i\}}}{v}\right)\,,
  \end{equation}
where the soft-collinear approximation to the observable is defined as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:Vsc}
  \frac{\Vsc{k_1,\dots,k_n}}{v}\equiv \lim_{v\to 0}\frac{V({\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n})}{v}\,,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to approximation of the observable when all emissions $k_1,\dots,k_n$ are simultaneously soft and collinear. Recursive IRC safety guarantees that this limit exists.
Moreover, the soft-collinear measure $\dZ$ is defined as
  \begin{multline}
    \label{eq:dZ-def}
    \int\! \dZ \,G(\{k_i\}) \\ \equiv \lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \epsilon^{\RpNLL} \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{n!} \int \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\sum_{\ell_i} R'_{\text{NLL},\ell_i}\int_{\epsilon}^\infty \frac{d\zeta_i}{\zeta_i}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\phi_i^{(\ell_i)}}{2\pi}\right)G(k_1,\dots,k_n)\,.
  \end{multline}
As before, the term with the Sudakov exponent, $\epsilon^{R'_{\mathrm{NLL}}}$, arises from \emph{unresolved} emissions with $\zeta_i<\epsilon$ together with virtual corrections. The remaining terms occur due to rIRC safety, where emissions contributing to 
$V\left(\{\tilde{p}\},k_1,\dots,k_n\right)$ are restricted to the region where $\epsilon v\lesssim
 V(\{\tilde{p}\},k_i)\sim v$ with $\epsilon \ll v$. Together, with the corresponding virtual corrections, they build up $ \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$.

k^{(ij)}=z^{(i)}p_i+z^{(j)}p_j+\kappa^{(ij)}\cos\phi^{(ij)}n_{\mathrm{in}}^{(ij)}+\kappa^{(ij)}\sin\phi^{(ij)}n_{\mathrm{out}}^{(ij)}\,.
\label{eq:Sudakov-decomp}
\end{equation}
Here the momenta $p_i, p_j$ are given in Eq.~\ref{eq:3-born-kin}. The quantity $\kappa^{(ij)}$ denotes the invariant transverse momentum with respect to the $(ij)$ dipole, defined as
\begin{equation}
\left(\kappa^{(ij)}\right)^2=\frac{(2p_i\cdot k)(2p_j\cdot k)}{(2p_i\cdot p_j)}\,,
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the four-momentum of the emission. The vectors $n_{\mathrm{in}}^{(ij)}$ and $n_{\mathrm{out}}^{(ij)}$ are given by
\begin{equation}
n_{\mathrm{in}}^{(ij)}=\left(\cot\frac{\theta_{ij}}{2},\frac{\vec{n}_i+\vec{n}_j}{\sin\theta_{ij}}\right)\,,\quad n_{\mathrm{out}}^{(ij)}=\left(0,\frac{\vec{n}_i\times\vec{n}_j}{\sin\theta_{ij}}\right)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\vec{n}_\ell\equiv\vec{p}_\ell/E_\ell$ for $\ell=i,j$.
The function $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}$ arises when a soft gluon is emitted at wide angles. The general expression for $\delta\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{wa}}$ is given by

\subsection{Additive Observables}
Here we will briefly discuss a certain class of observables which take an additive form. Additivity is the concept that an observable can be written as a the sum of contributions of individual emissions i.e. for soft and collinear emissions $k_1,\dots , k_n$, this means 
\begin{equation}
\Vsc{k_1,\dots , k_n}=\sum_{i=1}^n\Vsc{k_i}\,.
\end{equation}
Additive observables allow $\FNLL$ and for all terms in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dFNNLL} to be calculated analytically. The equations which govern these observables can be found in Ref.~\cite{Arpino:2019ozn}. Examples of additive observables are: thrust introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:thrust-example} and the one-jettiness and transverse thrust introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:event-shapes}.

One may think that once we have the resummed cumulant that this is enough. However we are not quite there. As describe in Sec.~\ref{sec:thrust-example}, perturbation theory breaks down at small values of the observable $v$. In this capacity, it is only in this region that we cannot rely on fixed-order calculations and must turn to resummation. Therefore one must undergo a procedure known as \emph{matching} in order to incorporate both the fixed-order and resummed results into our theoretical predicitions. We will outline this explicitly for a generic event shape $v$. As shown in Sec.~\ref{sec:thrust-example}, the fixed-order cumulant up to second order is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FO-gen}
	\Sigma(v)=1+\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\mathcal{A}(v)+\left(\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\right)^2\mathcal{B}(v,\mu^2)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ are the fixed-order coefficients up to second order and $\mu$ is the renormalisation scale. Moreover, as discussed previously, the resummed cumulant has the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:resummed-gen}
	\Sigma(v)=\left(1+\sum_{k=1}^\infty C_k\,\bar{\alpha}_s^k\right)\exp\left[Lg_1(\alpha_sL)+g_2(\alpha_sL)+\alpha_sg_3(\alpha_sL)+\dots\right]\,,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\alpha}_s\equiv\alpha_s/2\pi$ and $C_k$ are constants. As discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:thrust-example}, the terms $g_i(\alpha_sL)$ contain the resummed contributions at various orders i.e.  $\{g_1,g_2,g_3\}$ contain LL, NLL, and NNLL terms respectively. \\ \\
The idea behind the matching procedure is to combine Eqs.~\eqref{eq:FO-gen} and~\eqref{eq:resummed-gen} and to remove any double counting of logarithmic terms. In order to show this explicitly, we first re-expand the $g_i$ functions in powers of $\bar{\alpha}_sL$ using
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	&Lg_1(\alpha_s L)=G_{12}\bar{\alpha}_s+G_{23}\bar{\alpha}_s^2L^3+\dots\,,\\
	&g_2(\alpha_s L)=G_{11}\bar{\alpha}_sL+G_{22}\bar{\alpha}_s^2L^2+\dots\,,\\
	&\alpha_s g_3(\alpha_s L) =G_{21}\bar{\alpha}_s^2L+G_{32}\bar{\alpha}_s^3L^2\dots\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Substituting these into Eq.~\ref{eq:resummed-gen} and expanding the exponential function we find
\begin{equation}
	\Sigma(v) = 1 + \Sigma_{\mathrm{log}}^{(1)}(v)\,\bar{\alpha}_s+\Sigma_{\mathrm{log}}^{(2)}(v)\,\bar{\alpha}_s^2+\dots\,,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}

	 &\Sigma_{\mathrm{log}}^{(2)}(v)=C_2+(G_{21}+C_1G_{11})L+\left(G_{22}+\frac{1}{2}G_{11}^2+C_1G_{12}\right)L^2\\
	 &\qquad\qquad+(G_{23}+G_{12}G_{11})L^3+\frac{1}{2}G_{12}^2L^4\,.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
