\section{QCD: The basics}
In the 1940s, a number of unstable, strongly interacting particles -- collectively referred to as hadrons -- were discovered. In 1964, Gell-Mann~\cite{Gell-Mann:1964ewy} and Zweig~\cite{Zweig:1964ruk, Zweig:1964jf} proposed the quark model, which interprets hadrons as composite objects built from fundamental spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ constituents known as \emph{quarks}. The six known quarks are organised into three generations -- light, intermediate, and heavy -- as listed in Table~\ref{table:quark-properties}.

In this picture, hadrons are classified into two categories: mesons ($q\bar{q}$), which are 
integer-spin bosons, and baryons ($qqq$), which are half-integer-spin fermions. 
Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ non-Abelian gauge theory 
governing the strong interaction. It describes how quarks bind together to form hadrons,
 with the interaction mediated by gauge bosons known as gluons. 
 Throughout this thesis, quarks and gluons are collectively
referred to as \emph{partons} and carry a conserved charge called
colour.

Quarks transform in the fundamental representation of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$, 
with fields denoted by $q_a$ for $a = 1,2,3$, corresponding to the three colour labels: red, green, and blue. 
The associated antiquark fields are written as $\bar q^a$, where $\bar{q} = q^\dagger \gamma^0$ denotes the Dirac adjoint. 
Gluons, denoted $\mathcal{A}^\mu_A$, transform in the adjoint representation of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$, 
where the index $A$ labels the eight colour degrees of freedom of the gauge group.
 
 The QCD Lagrangian density governing the dynamics of quarks of mass $m_f$ 
 and massless gluons takes the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq: L-QCD}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QCD}}
= \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{classical}}
+ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GF}}
+ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ghost}}\,,
\end{equation}
where the classical contribution contains the kinetic and interaction contributions 
for the quark and gluon fields:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq: L-Classical}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{classical}}
= -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu}^{A} F^{\mu\nu}_{A}
+ \sum_{f=1}^{n_f} \bar{q}^f_a (i\slashed{D} - m_f)_{ab}\, q^f_b \,.
\end{equation}
Here $n_f$ denotes the number of quark flavours, taking the value $n_f = 6$ in the Standard Model 
(see Table~\ref{table:quark-properties}). The gluon dynamics are encoded in the non-Abelian field-strength tensor
\begin{equation}
F_{\alpha\beta}^{A}
= \partial_\alpha \mathcal{A}_\beta^A
- \partial_\beta \mathcal{A}_\alpha^A
- g_s f^{ABC} \mathcal{A}_\alpha^B \mathcal{A}_\beta^C \,,
\end{equation}
where $g_s$ is the strong coupling constant, conventionally expressed through
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_s \equiv \frac{g_s^2}{4\pi}\,.
    \label{eq:coupling-constant}
\end{equation}
The covariant derivative is written using Feynman slash notation, $\slashed{D} = \gamma_\mu D^\mu$, with
\begin{equation}
(D_\mu)_{ab}
= \partial_\mu \delta_{ab}
+ ig_s (t^C \mathcal{A}_\mu^C)_{ab}\,,
\end{equation}
where the gamma matrices satisfy the anti-commutation relation $\{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\} = 2 g^{\mu\nu}$. 
Throughout this work we adopt the metric convention $g^{\mu\nu} = (1,-1,-1,-1)$. 
The matrices $t^C$ are the generators of the fundamental representation of $\mathrm{SU}(3)$, 
given explicitly by 
\begin{equation}
	t^C = \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^C\,,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda^C$ are the eight traceless Hermitian Gell-Mann matrices. 
These satisfy the commutation relation
\begin{equation}
[t^A, t^B] = i f^{ABC} t^C\,,
\end{equation}
where $f^{ABC}$ are the structure constants of the $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ gauge group.
The generators satisfy the Casimir identities, where repeated
indices are summed implicitly:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
&\mathrm{Tr}(t^A t^B) = T_R\, \delta^{AB}, \qquad T_R = \tfrac{1}{2}, \\[1ex]
&t^{A}_{ab} t^{A}_{bc} = C_F\, \delta_{ac}, \qquad \qquad C_F = \tfrac{4}{3}, \\[1ex]
&f^{ABC} f^{ABD} = C_A\, \delta^{CD}, \quad C_A = 3.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The second term in Eq.~\eqref{eq: L-QCD} is the gauge-fixing contribution,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GF}}
    = -\frac{1}{2\xi} \left( \partial^\mu \mathcal{A}_\mu^{A} \right)^{2}\,,
\end{equation}
which is required to remove redundant gauge degrees of freedom arising from 
the local $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ gauge invariance of the QCD Lagrangian. 
The parameter $\xi$ specifies the choice of gauge: for example, 
$\xi = 1$ corresponds to the Feynman gauge, while $\xi = 0$ to the Landau gauge.

The final term in Eq.~\eqref{eq: L-QCD} is the ghost term,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{ghost} = \partial_\mu \eta^{A^\dagger}(D_{AB}^\mu\,\eta^B)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\eta^A$ are complex scalar fields known as Faddeev--Popov ghosts. These fields anti-commute and therefore obey Fermi statistics. Ghosts do not correspond to physical states; instead, they arise as a consequence of the gauge-fixing procedure in non-Abelian gauge theories.

\section{Perturbation Theory}
\label{sec:pert-theory}

With the QCD Lagrangian and its Feynman rules established, we now outline how 
calculations are performed in practice. The probability for one state to transition to another 
through scattering is determined by considering all relevant Feynman diagrams 
for the incoming and outgoing particles. Each diagram is constructed from 
the vertices and propagators of Figs.~\ref{fig:QCD-Propagator-Rules} 
and~\ref{fig:QCD-Vertex-Rules}, and translates into a mathematical expression via 
the corresponding Feynman rules. The sum over all such diagrams yields the 
\emph{scattering amplitude} (or matrix element) $\mathcal{M}$, and the transition probability is 
proportional to the squared modulus $|\mathcal{M}|^2$.

To make this concrete, consider the partonic cross section for a
hard scattering of $2\to m$ massless partons,
\[
    p_a + p_b \rightarrow p_1 + \cdots + p_m\,.
\]
The corresponding partonic cross section is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:2-to-m-xsec}
   \hat{ \sigma}= \frac{1}{2s}\int d\Phi^{(m)}(p_1,\ldots,p_m)\,|\mathcal{M}_m(p_a,p_b; p_1,\ldots,p_m)|^2\,F_J^{(m)}(p_1,\ldots,p_m)\,,
\end{equation}
where the individual components are defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $1/2s$ is the Lorentz-invariant flux factor for the two
  massless incoming partons, with $s=(p_a+p_b)^2$ the
  centre-of-mass energy squared for the hard process.
  
    \item $d\Phi^{(m)}$ denotes the $m$-parton phase space\footnote{This expression can be generalised to include colour-singlet final states by replacing the delta function $\delta(p_i^2)$ with $\delta(p_i^2 - m_i^2)$.},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-phase-space}
d\Phi^{(m)} = \left[\prod_{i=1}^{m} \frac{d^D p_i}{(2\pi)^D} \, \left(2\pi\right) \, \delta\left(p_i^2\right)\,\Theta\left(p_i^0\right)\right]\left(2\pi\right)^D\delta^{(D)}\Big(p_a + p_b - \sum_{j=1}^{m} p_j\Big)\,.
\end{equation}

\item $|\mathcal{M}_m|^2 = \langle\mathcal{M}_m|\mathcal{M}_m\rangle$
  is the squared scattering amplitude for the $2\to m$ process,
  where the inner product includes a sum over the colour and spin
  degrees of freedom of all final-state partons and an average
  over those of the initial-state partons.

\item $F_J^{(m)}$ is the \emph{jet function}, which specifies the observable under consideration.
 Depending on the choice of $\Theta$-functions, $\delta$-functions, or numerical
factors, it encodes cross-sections, differential cross sections or expectation values respectively. 
The choice $F_J^{(m)} = 1$ reduces Eq.~\eqref{eq:2-to-m-xsec} to the total partonic cross-section. 
\end{itemize}

From the structure of the interaction vertices, it is evident that each additional gluon emission (a real correction) or internal loop (a virtual correction) introduces a further power of the strong coupling $\alpha_s$. The cross section therefore admits a perturbative expansion,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sig-pert-theory}
  \hat\sigma =
  \hat\sigma^{(0)}
  +\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\,\hat\sigma^{(1)}
  +\left(\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\right)^2\hat\sigma^{(2)}
  +\dots\,,
\end{equation}
where $\hat\sigma^{(0)}$ is the tree-level (classical) contribution, referred to 
as the leading-order (LO) or Born term, and $\hat\sigma^{(1)},\hat\sigma^{(2)},\ldots$
represent higher-order (quantum) corrections known as next-to-leading order 
(NLO), next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO), and so on.

Since $\alpha_s$ becomes small at high energies -- as discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:running-coupling} --
higher-order contributions are progressively suppressed in this regime. As a result, the perturbative expansion can be well approximated by retaining only the first few terms, yielding a \emph{fixed-order} (FO) prediction. This approach is known as perturbation theory, and its systematic application to QCD processes 
is the basis of perturbative QCD (pQCD). Throughout this thesis, all calculations are performed within the framework of pQCD. 

\section{The running of the strong coupling $\alpha_s$}
\label{sec:running-coupling}

As noted in Sec.~\ref{sec:pert-theory}, pQCD relies on the strong coupling becoming small at high energies. This behaviour reflects the fact that $\alpha_s$ is not a fixed constant but instead \emph{runs} with the renormalisation scale $\mu_R$ -- an unphysical scale introduced by the renormalisation procedure, discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:divergences}. The scale dependence of the coupling is governed by the renormalisation group equation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coupling-DE}
    \mu_R^2 \frac{\partial \alpha_s(\mu_R^2)}{\partial \mu_R^2} = \beta(\alpha_s)\,.
\end{equation}
The QCD $\beta$-function admits a perturbative expansion of the form
\begin{equation}
    \beta(\alpha_s) = -\!\left(\beta_0\,\alpha_s^2 + \beta_1\,\alpha_s^3 + \beta_2\,\alpha_s^4 + \cdots\right),
\end{equation}
whose first three coefficients are 
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:beta}
\begin{align}
\label{eq:beta0}
    \beta_0 &= \frac{11 C_A - 4T_Rn_f}{12\pi}\,,\\[1em]
    \label{eq:beta1}
   \beta_1 &= \frac{17C_A^2 - 5C_A n_f - 3C_F n_f}{24\pi^2}\,,\\[1em]
   \label{eq:beta2}
\beta_2 &= \frac{2857C_A^3 + (54C_F^2 - 615C_F C_A - 1415C_A^2)n_f 
+ (66C_F + 79C_A)n_f^2}{3456\pi^3}\,.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
Only $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are renormalisation-scheme independent; 
higher coefficients depend on the choice of renormalisation scheme, 
taken throughout to be the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ scheme (discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:UV-div}). Solving Eq.~\eqref{eq:coupling-DE} at leading order with reference scale $Q$ gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:scale-dependence}
    \alpha_s(\mu_R^2)
    = \frac{\alpha_s(Q^2)}
    {1 + \alpha_s(Q^2)\,\beta_0\,\ln\!\left(\tfrac{\mu_R^2}{Q^2}\right)}\, .
\end{equation}
The sign of $\beta_0$ and the form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:scale-dependence}
determines the scale dependence of the strong coupling.
As $\mu_R \to \infty$, the coupling decreases, and quarks and gluons behave increasingly like free particles. 
This behaviour is known as \emph{asymptotic freedom}, a regime in which pQCD provides an excellent approximation. 
Conversely, as $\mu_R$ becomes small and approaches a scale known as $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}\approx200\,\mathrm{MeV} $,
the coupling grows large, signalling the breakdown of perturbation theory and 
the emergence of a strongly interacting regime in which quarks and gluons are confined into hadrons. 
This phenomenon is known as \emph{confinement}. 

The running of $\alpha_s$ has been confirmed experimentally across
several orders of magnitude in $Q$, as illustrated in
Fig.~\ref{fig:strong-coupling}, with the five-loop QCD prediction
in excellent agreement with data from a wide range of processes.
Throughout this thesis we work in the high-energy regime
$\mu_R\gg\Lambda_{\rm QCD}$, thereby exploiting asymptotic freedom.

\section{Divergences}
\label{sec:divergences}

While perturbation theory is very effective at high energies, loop and 
real-emission corrections introduce divergences that must be handled 
systematically. In QCD these arise in two distinct forms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ultraviolet (UV) divergences} arise in loop diagrams when the momentum of the
    virtual particle becomes large.
    \item \textbf{Infrared (IR) divergences} arise when a gluon emission becomes soft (low energy) or
    collinear to a hard (high energy) parton.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{UV Divergences}
\label{subsec:UV-div}

One Feynman rule not shown in
Figs.~\ref{fig:QCD-Propagator-Rules} and~\ref{fig:QCD-Vertex-Rules} is the rule associated
with loop diagrams. Each closed loop contributes an integration of the form
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{loop} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{d^Dq}{(2\pi)^D}\,,
\end{equation}
where $q^\mu$ denotes the momentum of the virtual particle running inside the loop, as
illustrated by the fermion bubble diagram in Fig.~\ref{fig:fermion-bubble}.

Taking the quarks to be massless, as assumed throughout this thesis, and applying the
Feynman rules, the diagram gives rise to an integral of the form
\begin{equation}
    I = \int d^4 q \, \frac{N^{\mu\nu}}{q^2 (p+q)^2}\,,
\end{equation}
where $N^{\mu\nu}$ denotes a numerator whose explicit form is not required for the present
discussion. At large loop momenta, the integral reveals a UV divergence,
\begin{equation}
    I \sim \lim_{\Lambda \to \infty} \int_0^\Lambda \frac{dq}{q}
      \sim \lim_{\Lambda \to \infty} \ln \Lambda\,.
\end{equation}
Such divergences cannot be allowed to remain in the theory, 
and therefore a systematic method for handling them is required. 
This is achieved through a procedure known as \emph{regularisation}. 
Although several regularisation procedures  exist, in this thesis we use \emph{dimensional regularisation}~\cite{tHooft:1972tcz, Bollini:1972ui}. This method preserves both Lorentz and gauge invariance and, as discussed below, can also be applied to infrared divergences. 
The basic idea is to promote the space-time dimension to $d = 4 - 2\varepsilon$, such that logarithmic divergences are replaced by poles in $\varepsilon$. 

Once these poles have been isolated, the next step is to remove them. This is achieved by 
a procedure known as \emph{renormalisation} where the parameters of the theory are redefined 
to absorb the divergent contributions via counterterms. Throughout this thesis we work in the modified minimal subtraction ($\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$) renormalisation scheme~\cite{Bardeen:1978yd},
in which the counterterms subtract not only the $1/\varepsilon$ poles but also the accompanying constants $\ln 4\pi$ and $\gamma_E \approx 0.5772$ (the Euler--Mascheroni constant) that arise as a result of using dimensional regularisation. The renormalisation procedure introduces the renormalisation scale $\mu_R$, and as a consequence the strong coupling $\alpha_s$ acquires a dependence on this scale, as discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:running-coupling}.

The  real-emission contribution to the cross section is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:qqg-real}
	\sigma^{q\bar{q}g}=\sigma_0\,\,C_F\,\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\int_0^1dx_q\int_{0}^{1}dx_{\bar{q}}\,\,\frac{x_q^2+x_{\bar{q}}^2}{(1-x_q)(1-x_{\bar{q}})}\,\Theta\left(x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1\right)
\,,
\end{equation}
where the energy fractions are defined by 
\[
    x_i = \frac{2 p_i \cdot p}{s}\,, \qquad p=(p_q+p_{\bar{q}}+p_g)\,,
    \]
 and $s=(p_{e^+}+p_{e^-})^2$ is the centre-of-mass energy squared. 
 The Heaviside theta function enforces the kinematic constraint $x_q + x_{\bar{q}} \geq 1$, 
 which follows directly from energy conservation in the three-particle final state.
 
From the structure of the integrand in in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qqg-real}, 
it is evident that divergences arise in the following limits:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $x_q \to 1$ \emph{and} $x_{\bar{q}} \to 1$: soft emission ($E_g \to 0$);
    \item $x_q \to 1$ \emph{or} $x_{\bar{q}} \to 1$: collinear emission 
          ($\theta_{\bar{q}g},\, \theta_{qg} \to 0$)\,,
\end{enumerate}
where $\theta_{qg}$ and $\theta_{\bar{q}g}$ denote the quark--gluon and 
antiquark--gluon angles respectively.
These are infrared and collinear singularities, collectively referred to as IR (or IRC) divergences. 
Applying dimensional regularisation yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:qqg-real-d-dim}
\begin{aligned}
	\sigma^{q\bar{q}g}=&\sigma_0\,C_F\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\left(\frac{4\pi\mu_R^2}{s}\right)^\varepsilon\frac{1}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}\int_0^1dx_q\int_0^1dx_{\bar{q}}\\
	&\times\frac{(x_q^2+x_{\bar{q}}^2)-\varepsilon(2-x_q-x_{\bar{q}})^2}{(1-x_q)^{1+\varepsilon}\,(1-x_{\bar{q}})^{1+\varepsilon}\,(x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1)^\varepsilon}\,\Theta\left(x_q+x_{\bar{q}}-1\right)\,.
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Performing the integrations gives
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^{q\bar{q}g}
    = \sigma_0\, \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\,
      \frac{C_F}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}
      \left(\frac{4\pi\mu_R^2}{s}\right)^{\varepsilon}
      \left(
          \frac{2}{\varepsilon^2}
        + \frac{3}{\varepsilon}
        - \pi^2
        + \frac{19}{2}
        + \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)
      \right).
\end{equation}

The virtual contribution carries poles of equal magnitude but opposite 
sign to the real emission,
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^{q\bar{q}(g)}
    = \sigma_0\, \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\,
      \frac{C_F}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}
      \left(\frac{4\pi\mu_R^2}{s}\right)^{\varepsilon}
      \left(
         -\frac{2}{\varepsilon^2}
         -\frac{3}{\varepsilon}
         + \pi^2
         - 8
         + \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)
      \right).
\end{equation}
Adding the real and virtual contributions yields a finite NLO correction,
\begin{equation}
   \sigma^{q\bar{q}g} + \sigma^{q\bar{q}(g)}
    = \sigma_0\, \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\,
      \frac{C_F}{\Gamma(1-\varepsilon)}
      \left(\frac{4\pi\mu_R^2}{s}\right)^{\varepsilon}
      \left(\frac{3}{2} + \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)\right).
\end{equation}
Taking the limit $\varepsilon \to 0$, the total cross section up to NLO is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sigma-full}
    \sigma
    = \sigma_0
      \left(1 + \frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi}\,\frac{3}{2} C_F
      + \mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^2)\right).
\end{equation}
The cancellation of singularities between the real and virtual diagrams is a direct consequence of the \emph{KLN theorem}~\cite{Kinoshita:1962ur, Lee:1964is}, which guarantees that IR divergences cancel between real and virtual contributions order by order in perturbation theory for any process, provided one sums over all degenerate initial and final states.

Understanding these factorisation properties is essential for characterising the singular structure of QCD. \subsection{Soft limit}
\label{subsec:soft-fact}
Suppressing the initial-state momenta $(p_a,p_b)$ for brevity, the squared matrix element for the
emission of an additional soft parton of momentum $q$ factorises as
\begin{equation}
    |\mathcal{M}_{m+1}^{(0)}(q,p_1,\ldots,p_m)|^2
    \simeq
    -4\pi\alpha_s\mu_R^{2\varepsilon}
    \sum_{i,j=1}^{m}
        S_{ij}(q)\,
        \left\langle
            \mathcal{M}_m^{(0)}(p_1,\ldots,p_m)
        \left|
            \mathbf{T}_i \cdot \mathbf{T}_j
        \right|
            \mathcal{M}_m^{(0)}(p_1,\ldots,p_m)
        \right\rangle\,,
\end{equation}
where the superscript $^{(0)}$ denotes the tree-level matrix element.
The operators $\mathbf{T}_i$ are the colour-charge operators acting
on parton $i$, which satisfy
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \mathbf{T}_i \cdot \mathbf{T}_j &= \mathbf{T}_j \cdot \mathbf{T}_i\,,
    \qquad (i\neq j),\\[4pt]
    \mathbf{T}_i^2 &= C_i\,,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with $C_i = C_F$ for quarks and $C_i = C_A$ for gluons. 
The eikonal factor $S_{ij}(q)$ encodes the kinematic dependence of the soft emission,
\begin{equation}
    S_{ij}(q) = \frac{2p_i \cdot p_j}{(2p_i \cdot q)(2p_j \cdot q)} \,.
\end{equation}
The factorisation shows that the soft emission depends only on the
colour charges and directions of the hard legs, not on the details
of the hard process.


\subsection{Collinear limit}
\label{subsec:collinear-fact}

When two partons $i$ and $j$, become collinear,
their momenta are conveniently parametrised via the Sudakov decomposition
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    p_i^\mu &= z\, p^\mu + k_\perp^\mu
               - \frac{k_\perp^2}{z}\,\frac{n^\mu}{2p\cdot n}\,, \\
    p_j^\mu &= (1-z)\,p^\mu - k_\perp^\mu
               - \frac{k_\perp^2}{1-z}\,\frac{n^\mu}{2p\cdot n}\,,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $p^\mu$ is a light-like vector ($p^2 = 0$) defining the collinear 
direction, $n^\mu$ is an auxiliary light-like vector ($n^2 = 0$), and $k_\perp^\mu$ 
is the transverse momentum ($k_\perp^2 < 0$, $k_\perp \cdot p = k_\perp \cdot n = 0$).
The parameter $z$ is the longitudinal momentum fraction carried by parton $i$,
and the collinear limit corresponds to $k_\perp^\mu\to0$.

In this limit, the squared matrix element factorises as 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  |\mathcal{M}_{m+1}^{(0)}&(p_1,\ldots,p_i,p_j,\ldots,p_{m+1})|^2
  \simeq\\
  &\frac{8\pi\alpha_s\mu_R^{2\varepsilon}}{s_{ij}}
  \left\langle\mathcal{M}_m^{(0)}(p_1,\ldots,p_{ij},\ldots,p_m)
  \left|\hat{P}_{(ij)\to i +j}(z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)\right|
  \mathcal{M}_m^{(0)}(p_1,\ldots,p_{ij},\ldots,p_m)
  \right\rangle\,,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $p_{ij}^\mu = p_i^\mu + p_j^\mu$ is the momentum of the parent 
parton $(ij)$ and $s_{ij}\equiv (p_i + p_j)^2$. The term $\hat{P}_{(ij) \to i + j}(z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)$ is the unregularised
tree-level Altarelli--Parisi splitting function for the collinear
splitting $ij\to i+j$. The explicit forms are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:AP-functions}
\begin{aligned}
  \hat{P}_{g\to q\bar{q}}^{\mu\nu} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)&=
   \hat{P}_{g\to \bar{q}q}^{\mu\nu} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)=
  T_R\!\left[-g^{\mu\nu}+4z(1-z)
  \frac{k_\perp^\mu k_\perp^\nu}{k_\perp^2}\right]\,,\\[1ex]
  \hat{P}_{q\to qg}^{ss'} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)&=
  \hat{P}_{\bar{q}\to \bar{q}g}^{ss'} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)=
  \delta_{ss'}\,C_F\!\left[\frac{1+z^2}{1-z}
  -\varepsilon(1-z)\right]\,,\\[1ex]
  \hat{P}_{q\to gq}^{ss'} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)&=
   \hat{P}_{\bar{q}\to g\bar{q}}^{ss'} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)=
  \delta_{ss'}\,C_F\!\left[\frac{1+(1-z)^2}{z}
  -\varepsilon z\right]\,,\\[1ex]
  \hat{P}_{g\to gg}^{\mu\nu} (z,k_\perp;\varepsilon)&=
  2C_A\!\left[-g^{\mu\nu}\!\left(\frac{z}{1-z}+\frac{1-z}{z}\right)
  -2(1-\varepsilon)z(1-z)
  \frac{k_\perp^\mu k_\perp^\nu}{k_\perp^2}\right]\,,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the gluon splitting functions $\hat{P}^{\mu\nu}$ carry Lorentz
indices $\mu,\nu$ corresponding to the two polarisation states
of the parent gluon, while the quark splitting functions
$\hat{P}^{ss'}$ carry spin indices $s,s'$ of the parent
fermion. \ndp{In both cases the indices are contracted with those
of the reduced matrix element $|\mathcal{M}_m^{(0)}|^2$ in
the collinear factorisation formula.}

\section{IRC Safety} 
\label{sec:IRC}

In Sec.~\ref{sec:divergences} we saw, through the explicit NLO calculation of $e^{+}e^{-}\to q\bar q$, that the IR singularities from the real and virtual  contributions cancel, yielding a finite result. This relies on the fact that the total cross section is a \emph{fully inclusive} observable, for which $F_J^{(m)} = 1$  for any number of final-state particles (see Sec.~\ref{sec:pert-theory}). 

For more general observables, such a cancellation is not guaranteed. To obtain finite predictions order by order in perturbation theory, the observable must be \emph{infrared and collinear safe} (IRC safe): its value must be insensitive to the emission of additional soft and/or collinear partons. At NLO, this requirement can be expressed in terms of the jet function as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
F_J^{(m+1)}(p_1,\ldots,p_i,\ldots,p_{m+1})
&\;\longrightarrow\;
F_J^{(m)}(p_1,\ldots,\cancel{p_i},\ldots,p_{m+1})\,,
\qquad &&E_i \to 0, \\[6pt]
F_J^{(m+1)}(p_1,\ldots,p_i,p_j,\ldots,p_{m+1})
&\;\longrightarrow\;
F_J^{(m)}(p_1,\ldots,\cancel{p_i},\cancel{p_j},p_{ij},\ldots,p_{m+1})\,,
\qquad &&p_i \parallel p_j\,,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:IRC-safety}
\end{equation}
with a diagrammatic representation shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:IRC_safety}.

We emphasise that although Eq.~\eqref{eq:IRC-safety} is written for a single additional parton, the same condition extends to any number of soft or collinear partons. From this definition it follows immediately that fully inclusive observables with $F_J^{(m)} = 1$ are IRC safe. A simple example of an IRC-unsafe observable is the number of final-state partons. 

Although IRC safety ensures a finite result at every order in perturbation theory, it does not guarantee a well-behaved perturbative expansion. Many observables, while IRC safe, do not display a complete cancellation between real and virtual contributions -- only enough to remove divergences. This incomplete cancellation generates large logarithmic terms arising from emissions in the soft and/or collinear regions. Such quantities are referred to as \emph{semi-inclusive} observables~\cite{Bassetto:1983mvz}. A prominent class of examples are event-shape variables, discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:resum}. These logarithms can spoil the convergent nature of the perturbative expansion and must be \emph{resummed} -- the subject of Chapters~\ref{ch:resum} and~\ref{ch:hhZjet}.

\section{The full picture: high-energy collisions}
\label{sec:high-energy-picture}

Up to this point, the discussion has largely focused on physics at the parton level.
In particular, we have relied on concepts such as quark--hadron duality (cf.\ Sec.~\ref{sec:divergences}) to relate partonic cross sections to hadronic observables, and have worked predominantly in the high-energy regime, where asymptotic freedom (cf.\ Sec.~\ref{sec:running-coupling}) allows the use of  perturbative QCD. 

In realistic collider experiments, however, the situation is significantly more complex.
Consider, for example, a hadronic collision at the LHC. The incoming particles are composite hadrons rather than elementary partons, and the final-state particles observed in the detector consist of hadrons produced at energy scales far below that of the hard interaction. A complete description of such a collision therefore necessarily involves physics beyond the perturbative regime. 

The aim of this section is to outline the various stages of a high-energy hadronic collision,
 organised by the relevant energy scale $Q$. These stages are depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}, 
which shows a $t\bar{t}h$ event produced in a proton--proton collision 
as generated by the \textsc{Sherpa}~\cite{Gleisberg:2008ta} collaboration.

\subsection{Hard scattering $(Q \gg \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}})$}
\label{subsec:hard-scattering}

At the highest energy scale, the dynamics of the collision are governed by the hard scattering, represented by the central red blob in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}. In this process, two partons, denoted $i$ and $j$, are extracted from the incoming protons $P_1$ and $P_2$ and undergo a short-distance interaction. The partonic content of the proton is encoded in parton distribution functions (PDFs), $f_{i/P}(x,Q^2)$, which give the probability of finding a parton of flavour $i$ carrying longitudinal momentum fraction $x$ of the proton $P$ at the energy scale $Q^2$.  

PDFs are non-perturbative functions and must be extracted from experimental data.
However, their scale dependence is governed by perturbative QCD through the Dokshitzer--Gribov--Lipatov--Altarelli--Parisi (DGLAP) evolution equations~\cite{Gribov:1972ri,Dokshitzer:1977sg,Altarelli:1977zs}, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DGLAP}
    \frac{\partial f_{i/P}(x,Q^2)}{\partial\ln Q^2}
    = \sum_{j} \int_x^1 \frac{dz}{z}\,
      \frac{\alpha_s(Q^2)}{2\pi}\,
      P_{ij}(z,\alpha_s(Q^2))\,
      f_{j/P}\!\left(\frac{x}{z}, Q^2\right),
\end{equation}
where the Altarelli-Parisi splitting functions $P_{ij}(z)$ admit the perturbative expansion
\begin{equation}
	P_{ij}(z,\alpha_s(Q^2)) = P_{ij}^{(0)}(z)+\frac{\alpha_s}{2\pi} P_{ij}^{(1)}(z)+\dots\,.
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:splitting-func-real-virt}
\begin{aligned}
&P_{q\bar{q}}^{(0)}(z)=C_F\left[\frac{1+z^2}{(1-z)_+}+\frac{3}{2}\delta(1-z)\right]\,,\\
&P_{qg}^{(0)}(z)=T_R\left[z^2+(1-z)^2\right]\,,\\

The hard interaction produces the primary particles of the event -- in this case a $t\bar{t}h$ system, indicated by the smaller red blobs in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}. Since the energy scale $Q$ is much larger than $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, predictions at this stage can be reliably obtained using fixed-order perturbation theory (Sec.~\ref{sec:pert-theory}). Such calculations are typically provided by matrix-element generators such as \textsc{MadGraph}~\cite{Alwall:2014hca} and \textsc{MCFM}~\cite{Campbell:1999ah,Campbell:2011bn,Campbell:2019dru}.

\subsection{QCD evolution $(Q \gtrsim \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}})$}
\label{subsec:QCD-ev}

Following the hard scattering, the coloured partons produced in the event undergo further QCD radiation, illustrated by the red quark and gluon propagators in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}. This radiation is dominated by soft and collinear emissions, which for many observables give rise to logarithmically enhanced contributions (see Sec.~\ref{sec:IRC}). When such logarithms become large, fixed-order perturbation theory is no longer sufficient, and dedicated techniques are required to obtain reliable results in the perturbative regime. The accuracy of these methods is typically characterised in terms of logarithmic accuracy, such as leading logarithmic (LL), next-to-leading logarithmic (NLL), and so on. Common approaches include:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \emph{Parton showers} model the
	soft and collinear emissions from the hard scale down to the hadronisation scale. 
	Well-known implementations include \textsc{Sherpa}~\cite{Gleisberg:2008ta}, \textsc{Pythia}~\cite{Bierlich:2022pfr}, \textsc{Herwig}~\cite{Bahr:2008pv, Bellm:2015jjp}, and \textsc{PanScales}~\cite{vanBeekveld:2023ivn}. While \textsc{Pythia} and \textsc{Herwig} achieve LL accuracy, the \textsc{Sherpa}~\cite{Herren:2022jej, Sherpa:2024mfk} and \textsc{PanScales}~\cite{vanBeekveld:2022zhl, vanBeekveld:2022ukn,vanBeekveld:2023chs} showers have demonstrated NLL accuracy for a broad class of processes and observables, with ongoing work aimed at extending to higher logarithmic accuracy. 
	
\item \emph{QCD resummation} systematically resums logarithmically 
enhanced contributions from soft and collinear radiation to all 
orders in perturbation theory. Semi-numerical
resummation frameworks such as \textsc{CAESAR}~\cite{Banfi:2004yd} and \textsc{ARES}~\cite{Banfi:2014sua, Banfi:2016zlc, Banfi:2018mcq, Arpino:2019ozn} fall into this category. 
For $e^+e^-$, resummation for jet observables has been achieved at 
    NNLL accuracy, while for hadronic collisions 
    the state of the art is generally NLL. The work presented in 
    Chapter~\ref{ch:hhZjet} focuses on extending the \textsc{Ares} 
    framework to hadronic collisions at NNLL accuracy.

\item \emph{Effective field theory (EFT) resummation}, most notably through soft-collinear effective theory (SCET)~\cite{Bauer:2000ew, Bauer:2000yr, Bauer:2001ct, Bauer:2001yt}, describes soft and collinear degrees of freedom through a systematic observable-dependent factorisation formula. In this framework, the relevant dynamics are encoded in separate hard, collinear, soft, and jet functions, which enables the resummation to be carried out analytically for a wide range of observables. In some cases, this can be performed up to $\mathrm{N^3LL}$ accuracy, as demonstrated in Refs.~\cite{Becher:2008cf, Abbate:2010xh, Alioli:2023rxx}. 
	\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hadronisation ($Q \sim \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$)}
\label{subsec:hadronisation}

As the energy scale of the event approaches $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$,  confinement effects dominate and hadronisation occurs. The coloured partons produced by the hard scattering and subsequent QCD evolution (light green blobs in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}) are transformed into colour-singlet hadrons (dark green blobs). These primary hadrons subsequently decay into longer-lived particles (smaller dark green blobs) that are ultimately observed in the detector. Experimentally, these particles appear predominantly as \emph{jets} -- collimated sprays of hadrons -- whose clustering and properties are discussed in Ch.~\ref{sec:jet-phys}. 
%Since hadronisation occurs in the non-perturbative regime, pQCD cannot be used here. Instead, its effects can be simulated through various models such as those found in Refs.~\cite{Sjostrand:1982fn, Andersson:1983jt, Webber:1983if,Dokshitzer:1995qm}. 
 Since hadronisation occurs in the 
non-perturbative regime, pQCD cannot be applied directly; instead, its 
effects are modelled using phenomenological approaches such as the 
string~\cite{Sjostrand:1982fn,Andersson:1983jt} and 
cluster~\cite{Webber:1983if} models, as well as analytical 
power-correction methods~\cite{Dokshitzer:1995qm}.

The three stages described above capture the main features of the QCD dynamics in a high-energy hadronic collision, but several additional ingredients contribute to the structure of a realistic event. Electromagnetic radiation, shown as yellow lines in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}, may occur at any stage of the process. Secondary semi-hard interactions between the proton remnants give rise to the \emph{underlying event}, represented by the purple blob in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event}. These interactions generate further radiation and hadrons, however are not described by standard QCD factorisation and require dedicated modelling. 

Prior to the hard interaction, the incoming partons may radiate, producing \emph{initial-state radiation} (ISR). This radiation evolves down to the hadronisation scale and contributes to the final-state hadrons (dark green blobs). The light blue ovals in Fig.~\ref{fig:high-energy-event} represent the beam remnants: the fragments of the incoming protons that do not participate in the hard scattering.

\subsection{The QCD Factorisation Formula}
\label{subsec:factorisation-formula}

To relate the partonic dynamics discussed in the previous sections 
to experimentally measured hadronic 
cross sections, we employ the QCD factorisation formula,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:factorisation-formula}
    d\sigma(P_1,P_2) = \sum_{i,j}\int_0^1 dx_1\,dx_2\,
    f_{i/P_1}(x_1,\mu_F^2)\,f_{j/P_2}(x_2,\mu_F^2)\,
    d\hat{\sigma}_{ij}\!\left(\alpha_s(\mu_R^2),\mu_R^2,\mu_F^2\right)
    + \mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}^2}{Q^2}\right),
\end{equation}
illustrated schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:factorisation-formula}. 
Here $f_{i/P_1}(x_1,\mu_F^2)$ and $f_{j/P_2}(x_2,\mu_F^2)$ are the 
PDFs for partons $i$ and $j$ inside the incoming protons $P_1$ and 
$P_2$, with $x_1$ and $x_2$ their longitudinal momentum fractions. 
The sum over $i,j$ runs over all contributing parton flavours, and 
$\hat{\sigma}_{ij}$ is the corresponding partonic cross section.

The factorisation theorem achieves a clean separation between long-distance, non-perturbative physics, encoded in the PDFs, and the short-distance hard interaction, captured by $\hat{\sigma}_{ij}$.  
This separation is made precise by the factorisation scale $\mu_F$: 
radiation with transverse momentum below $\mu_F$ is absorbed into the PDFs and treated as part of the proton structure, while harder emissions are included explicitly in $\hat{\sigma}_{ij}$. The power-suppressed corrections $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}^{2}/Q^{2}\right)$ reflect the fact that this description is valid only in the high-energy limit, $Q \gg \Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, which is satisfied throughout the course of this thesis.
